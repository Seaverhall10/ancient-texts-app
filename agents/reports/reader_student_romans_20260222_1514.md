- 2:1 "Therefore you are without excuse, O man, every one who judges" — the Greek's direct address comes through clearly

**Awkward spots needing attention:**

### 1:4 — Syntactic overload
> "the One having been declared Son of God in power according to the Spirit of holiness by the resurrection from the dead — Jesus Christ our Lord —"

The triple prepositional stack ("in power / according to / by the resurrection") is grammatically clear but cognitively dense. A first-time reader stumbles here. Consider a note explaining the phrase structure, or slight reordering: "declared Son of God in power by the resurrection from the dead, according to the Spirit of holiness."

### 2:3 — Missing clarity
> "And do you reckon this, O man, the one judging those who practice such things and doing the same, that you will escape..."

"The one judging" feels like an appositive but could be read as a vocative shift. Suggest: "And do you reckon this, O man—you who judge those practicing such things yet do the same—that you will escape...?" (Em-dashes signal the appositive more clearly.)

### 3:2 — "First indeed"?
> "Much in every way. First indeed, for that they were entrusted..."

"First indeed" sounds stilted. Is this πρῶτον μὲν? Consider "First of all" (standard in most translations) or "In the first place" if you want formality. "For that they were entrusted" is also archaic—"for they were entrusted" removes the odd "for that" construction without losing meaning.

## DOES IT SOUND LIKE SCRIPTURE?

**Yes.** The formal register, retention of "O man," use of "May it never be!" (3:4), and archaic verb forms ("shall prevail," "shall we say") all signal sacred text. This doesn't sound like a modern paraphrase.

**Caution:** "Longsuffering" (2:4) is excellent—don't modernize to "patience." But watch for *unintentional* archaisms that don't serve literalness (see "for that" above).

## VERSES THAT SEEM OFF

### 2:2 — Redundancy?
> "And we know that the judgment of God is according to truth against those who practice such things."

Not wrong, but "according to truth" might confuse: does it mean "truthful" or "in accordance with reality"? Given the rhetorical flow (you judge hypocritically; God judges truly), a note clarifying κατὰ ἀλήθειαν would help. ESV's "rightly" flattens it; your choice preserves ambiguity—but flag it.

### 3:4 — Quotation formatting
> "as it has been written: 'That You may be justified...'"

The purpose clause "That You may..." (ὅπως) beginning the quote feels abrupt. Readers may not realize this is Psalm 51:4. **Needed:** a note identifying the source *and* explaining that the "That" introduces God's purpose in being truthful even when humans lie. Without context, "That You may be justified in Your words" sounds like a fragment.
