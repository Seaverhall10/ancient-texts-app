## 1. John 10:34-36

### Jesus Quotes Psalm 82: "I Said, You Are Gods"

### The Text

> Jesus answered them, "Is it not written in your Law, 'I said, you are gods'? If he called them gods to whom the word of God came -- and Scripture cannot be broken -- do you say of him whom the Father consecrated and sent into the world, 'You are blaspheming,' because I said, 'I am the Son of God'?" (John 10:34-36, ESV)

### Context

The Pharisees have just picked up stones to kill Jesus because he said "I and the Father are one" (10:30). They charge blasphemy: "You, being a man, make yourself God" (10:33). Jesus' response is to quote Psalm 82:6, the divine council judgment text.

### Greek Key Terms

- **ho nomos hymon** (ho nomos humon) -- "your Law." Jesus uses nomos broadly for the entire Hebrew Bible (Psalm 82 is not in the Torah proper). The definite article + possessive gives it a pointed edge: "your own Scripture says this."
- **theoi este** (theoi este) -- "gods you are." Direct quotation of Psalm 82:6 LXX. The Greek theoi is the standard LXX translation of Hebrew 'elohim.
- **hon ho pater hegiasen** (hon ho pater hegiasen) -- "whom the Father consecrated/sanctified." Aorist active indicative of hagiazo. Christ's setting apart is by the Father's direct action -- a unique, unrepeatable consecration.
- **apesteilen eis ton kosmon** (apesteilen eis ton kosmon) -- "sent into the world." The mission language: Christ was sent FROM the Father INTO the world. This presupposes pre-existence and divine origin.

### The Interpretive Crux: What Is Jesus' Argument?

There are three major scholarly positions:

#### Position A: The Traditional "Human Judges" A Fortiori

Many commentators (Calvin, Morris, some Reformed tradition) read this as: "If God called human judges 'gods' because they received God's word and exercised divinely delegated authority, how much more can I -- whom the Father personally consecrated and sent -- claim to be the Son of God?" This is a qal wahomer (light and heavy, lesser to greater) argument. If mere humans can bear the title 'gods,' then Jesus' claim to sonship is far less scandalous than they think.

**Problem**: This reading assumes the 'elohim of Psalm 82 are human judges. As the app's Chapter 5 demonstrates at length (see the five fatal problems in the counter-box), the "human judges" interpretation collapses under grammatical scrutiny. The phrase k'adam t'mutun ("like men you shall die") is a tautology if addressed to humans. The assembly language (ba-'adat-'El) has Ugaritic divine council cognates. The "sons of the Most High" (b'nei 'Elyon) is a divine-being designation.

#### Position B: Heiser's Divine Council A Fortiori (PREFERRED)

Michael Heiser (SBL Regional 2011 paper, "Jesus' Quotation of Psalm 82:6 in John 10:34-36"; Unseen Realm ch. 29; Naked Bible Podcast #109) argues that Jesus' argument is even stronger when the 'elohim are recognized as actual divine beings:

"If God himself called *divine beings* -- members of his own heavenly council -- 'gods' and 'sons of the Most High,' and Scripture cannot be broken on this point, then on what possible basis can you accuse me of blasphemy for calling myself the Son of God? If the title 'god' is biblically applied to lesser beings, how much more does it belong to the one the Father uniquely consecrated and sent?"

The force of the argument is: "Your own Scripture acknowledges the existence of divine beings called 'gods.' I am not claiming to be one of those lesser 'gods.' I am claiming something far greater -- unique Sonship from the Father. Your accusation undershoots the reality."

**Key insight from Heiser**: Jesus is NOT saying "we're all gods" or "the Pharisees are gods." He is saying: "Your Bible already contains the category of divine beings other than YHWH. If *that* is not blasphemy in your Bible, then my claim to unique divine Sonship -- which is *above* their status -- certainly is not."

#### Position C: D.A. Carson's Mediating View

Carson (Gospel According to John, Pillar Commentary) acknowledges the difficulty. He notes that Jesus' argument is somewhat ad hominem -- using the Pharisees' own Scripture against them to blunt their charge -- but the deeper claim remains: Jesus is the unique Son consecrated and sent by the Father. Carson does not commit fully to either the human or divine reading of the Psalm 82 'elohim but recognizes the a fortiori structure works with either.

#### Position D: Keener and Beasley-Murray

Craig Keener (Gospel of John, 2003) emphasizes the rhetorical strategy: Jesus uses Scripture to create an unanswerable dilemma. If "gods" in Psalm 82 is legitimate Scripture, then Jesus' lesser claim to "Son of God" cannot be blasphemy. Beasley-Murray (John, WBC) similarly focuses on the logical force: the argument works regardless of one's interpretation of Psalm 82 because the point is about scriptural precedent for divine titles applied to non-YHWH figures.

### What This Tells Us About Jesus' Divine Council Theology

1. **Jesus knew Psalm 82 and used it deliberately.** He did not stumble onto this text. He selected the ONE psalm that describes God's judgment on the divine council, in the middle of a confrontation about his own divine identity.

2. **Jesus presupposes the Deut 32 framework.** His argument only has maximum force if there is a real category of divine beings called 'elohim who are distinct from YHWH. The Pharisees, steeped in Second Temple Judaism, would have known this tradition.

3. **Jesus places himself ABOVE the council members.** He is not one of the 'elohim of Psalm 82. He is "the one the Father consecrated and sent" -- a unique, unrepeatable mission. The 'elohim are subordinates who failed; Jesus is the Son who fulfills.

4. **"Scripture cannot be broken" (he graphe ou dynatai lythenai).** Jesus affirms the authority of the passage in the strongest possible terms. He treats Psalm 82 as binding, authoritative, and non-negotiable -- including its testimony about divine beings called 'elohim.

### Divine Council Confidence Rating: **STRONG**

The divine council reading makes the a fortiori argument more powerful and more coherent. It is not the only viable reading, but it is the best reading when Psalm 82 is taken at grammatical face value.

## 2. 1 Peter 3:18-22

### Christ Proclaims to the Imprisoned Spirits

### The Text

> For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, that he might bring us to God, being put to death in the flesh but made alive in the spirit, in which he went and proclaimed [ekerxyen] to the spirits in prison, because they formerly did not obey, when God's patience waited in the days of Noah, while the ark was being prepared... [Christ] has gone into heaven and is at the right hand of God, with angels, authorities and powers having been subjected to him. (1 Peter 3:18-20, 22, ESV)

### Greek Key Terms

- **ekerxyen** (ekerxyen, from kerysso): Aorist active indicative, 3rd person singular. "He proclaimed / heralded." This is the critical verb. It is NOT euengelisato ("he preached good news / evangelized"). Kerysso means to make an official announcement as a herald (keryx). The word choice tells us the content: this was a victory proclamation, not an offer of salvation. Christ announced his triumph to the imprisoned spirits.

- **pneumasin en phylake** (pneumasin en phylake): "To spirits in prison." Pneumata ("spirits") without qualifier in the NT typically designates supernatural beings (cf. Heb 1:14 "ministering spirits"; Mark 1:27 "unclean spirits"; Heb 12:23 uses "spirits of righteous men" but only with the qualifier "of righteous men made perfect"). The bare noun "spirits" plus "in prison" points to non-human entities.

- **apeithesasin pote** (apeithesasin pote): "Who formerly disobeyed." Aorist active participle of apeitheo ("to disobey, be disobedient"). The temporal marker "formerly" (pote) + the specific dating "in the days of Noah" (en hemerais Noe) locks the identity: these are beings who rebelled in the pre-Flood era.

- **hypotagenton** (hypotagenton): Aorist passive participle of hypotasso, genitive masculine plural. "Having been subjected." The passive voice: these powers did not voluntarily submit; they were forcibly subjugated. Christ is the one to whom they were subjected.

### Five Major Scholarly Interpretations

#### View 1: Christ (in his pre-incarnate spirit) preached through Noah to Noah's contemporaries

**Advocates**: Augustine, Bede, Aquinas, more recently Wayne Grudem (1 Peter, TNTC, 1988, pp. 157-161 + 203-239 appendix -- 16% of the commentary devoted to this question).

**Argument**: The "spirit" in v.18 ("made alive in the spirit") refers to the Holy Spirit. Christ, through the Holy Spirit, preached through Noah to the people of Noah's generation. The "spirits in prison" are the human souls now imprisoned in Hades because they rejected Noah's preaching.

**Problems**: (1) The word pneumasin ("spirits") without qualification almost never means human souls in the NT. (2) The verb is kerysso (herald/proclaim), not euangelizo (preach good news) -- an odd choice if salvation was being offered. (3) The grammar of "in which he went and proclaimed" most naturally reads as sequential: Christ went somewhere and did something. (4) This view requires an unusual reading of "spirits in prison" as referring to humans who are now dead but were alive when the preaching occurred.

#### View 2: Christ descended to Hades between death and resurrection and preached to imprisoned human souls (the "Harrowing of Hell")

**Advocates**: Clement of Alexandria, some medieval tradition, some Roman Catholic theology.

**Argument**: Between his death and resurrection, Christ descended to the realm of the dead and offered salvation (or announced salvation) to those who died in the Flood.

**Problems**: (1) Same pneumasin problem as View 1. (2) If Christ offered salvation to the Flood generation, why them specifically? (3) This contradicts the general NT teaching that judgment follows death (Heb 9:27).

#### View 3: Christ proclaimed victory to the imprisoned Watchers of Genesis 6 / 1 Enoch (PREFERRED)

**Advocates**: W.J. Dalton (Christ's Proclamation to the Spirits, 1965; "The Interpretation of 1 Peter 3:19 and 4:6: Light from 2 Peter," Biblica 60, 1979, 547-555), J.R. Michaels (1 Peter, WBC, 1988), Karen Jobes (1 Peter, BECNT, 2005), Bo Reicke, Michael Heiser (Unseen Realm, 2015; Reversing Hermon, 2017).

**Argument**: The "spirits in prison" are the rebellious divine beings (bene ha'elohim / Watchers) of Genesis 6:1-4 who are imprisoned as described in:
- **1 Enoch 10:4-6**: Raphael binds Azazel and casts him into darkness; "on the day of the great judgment he shall be cast into the fire."
- **1 Enoch 10:11-12**: "Go to the watchers... bind them for seventy generations underneath the rocks of the ground until the day of their judgment."
- **2 Peter 2:4**: "God did not spare angels when they sinned, but cast them into Tartarus (tartarosas) and committed them to chains of gloomy darkness to be kept until the judgment."
- **Jude 6**: "And the angels who did not stay within their own position of authority, but left their proper dwelling, he has kept in eternal chains under gloomy darkness until the judgment of the great day."

The connection to "the days of Noah" is decisive. Peter explicitly dates the disobedience to the pre-Flood era. In Second Temple Judaism, the Watcher tradition (1 Enoch 6-16, Book of Giants, Jubilees 5) was the dominant interpretive framework for Genesis 6:1-4. Peter's audience would have immediately understood "spirits who disobeyed in the days of Noah" as the Watchers.

What Christ "proclaimed" (kerysso) was NOT an offer of salvation but a VICTORY ANNOUNCEMENT. He heralded his triumph over the powers. This is the same event described from a different angle in Colossians 2:15 (triumphal procession) and Philippians 2:10 (every knee bowing, including katachthonion -- "under the earth").

**The sequence**: Christ died (v.18) --> was made alive in the spirit (v.18) --> went and proclaimed to the imprisoned spirits (v.19) --> went into heaven (v.22) --> sat at God's right hand with all powers subjected (v.22). The proclamation to the Watchers occurs between death/resurrection and ascension. It is a stop on the victory tour.

#### View 4: Christ proclaimed to fallen angels at the ascension (not during the descent)

**Advocates**: Some scholars combine Views 3 and 4, arguing the proclamation occurred at the ascension (going through the heavenly realms) rather than during a "descent to hell."

**Argument**: The verb poreuomai ("went") in v.19 is the same verb used in v.22 for Christ going into heaven. Both journeys could be the ascension. Christ passed through the spiritual realms on his way to God's right hand and announced his victory to the imprisoned powers as he passed.

**Support**: This avoids the complicated question of Christ's "descent into hell" and focuses on the ascension as the moment of public victory.

#### View 5: Dalton's Refinement -- 1 Enoch 12-16 Background

W.J. Dalton's influential monograph argues that 1 Peter 3:19 is best understood against the specific background of 1 Enoch 12-16, where Enoch is commissioned to go to the imprisoned Watchers and announce God's judgment. Christ's proclamation is a typological fulfillment: as Enoch proclaimed judgment to the Watchers, Christ proclaimed his victory. The parallel is exact:

| 1 Enoch 12-16 | 1 Peter 3:18-22 |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Enoch goes to the imprisoned Watchers | Christ goes to the spirits in prison |
| Enoch announces God's judgment | Christ proclaims (kerysso) |
| The Watchers disobeyed in the pre-Flood era | The spirits "formerly did not obey... in the days of Noah" |
| The Watchers are bound until the great day | Angels, authorities, and powers are now subjected to Christ |

### The Theological Arc (Connecting to the App)

1. **Genesis 6:1-4**: The bene ha'elohim cross the ontological boundary and produce the Nephilim. [THC Ch.2]
2. **1 Enoch 6-16**: The Watcher tradition expands the Genesis account. They are imprisoned. [THC Ch.13]
3. **2 Peter 2:4 / Jude 6**: Imprisoned angels, Tartarus, chains of darkness.
4. **1 Peter 3:19**: Christ visits the prison and proclaims his victory.
5. **Colossians 2:15**: The triumphal procession over the powers. [THC Ch.7, section 7.1]
6. **Philippians 2:10**: Every knee bows -- including those "under the earth" (katachthonion). [THC Ch.7, section 7.5]
7. **1 Peter 3:22**: Angels, authorities, and powers subjected to the risen Christ.

This is a single narrative arc: rebellion --> imprisonment --> victory announcement --> total subjugation.

### Divine Council Confidence Rating: **STRONG**

The Watcher/fallen-angel interpretation is the majority scholarly position among specialists (Dalton, Michaels, Jobes, Reicke, Heiser, Selwyn). The connection to 1 Enoch is widely acknowledged. Grudem's human-souls view, while defended at length, requires grammatical readings that strain the text. The pneumasin + en phylake + "in the days of Noah" convergence is too precise to be coincidental.

## 3. 1 Corinthians 11:10

### "Because of the Angels"

### The Text

> For this reason, and because of the angels, the woman ought to have a sign of authority on her head. (1 Corinthians 11:10, NIV)

> dia touto opheilei he gyne exousian echein epi tes kephales dia tous angelous

### Greek Key Terms

- **dia tous angelous** (dia tous angelous): "Because of the angels." Preposition dia + accusative = "because of, on account of." The angels (tous angelous) have the definite article, suggesting specific, known angelic beings, not angels in general.

- **exousian echein epi tes kephales**: "To have authority on the head." The word exousia ("authority") is debated. Is the covering itself a symbol of authority? Or does the woman exercise her own authority (actively, not passively) by wearing the covering? Many scholars now read this as the woman's OWN authority -- she has authority over her own head -- with the covering as its sign.

### Three Major Scholarly Interpretations

#### View 1: The Watcher Tradition -- Angels Who Lusted After Women

**Advocates**: Tertullian (De Virginibus Velandis, c. AD 200); Joseph Fitzmyer ("A Feature of Qumran Angelology and the Angels of 1 Cor 11:10," NTS 4, 1957, 48-58); some Second Temple specialists.

**Argument**: Paul alludes to the Genesis 6 / 1 Enoch Watcher tradition. The Watchers were celestial beings who lusted after human women. Paul warns that women should have a covering as a sign of protection or authority, because angels might again be tempted by uncovered women.

Fitzmyer's landmark 1957 article connected this passage to the Dead Sea Scrolls (1QSa, 1QM, 4QFlor), which exclude those with physical blemishes from the assembly "because the holy angels are present." The Qumran community believed angels attended their worship gatherings, and any impropriety or disorder would offend them. Fitzmyer argued Paul shares this assumption.

**Evaluation**: The Watcher allusion is possible but not the strongest interpretation. The problems: (1) Paul does not explicitly mention Genesis 6 or the Watchers. (2) The idea that angels would be "tempted" by uncovered women seems to attribute an ongoing vulnerability to celestial beings that Paul nowhere else suggests. (3) The context is about worship order, not sexual temptation.

#### View 2: Angels as Worship Guardians Who Expect Order (MAJORITY VIEW)

**Advocates**: Gordon Fee (First Epistle to the Corinthians, NICNT, 1987, rev. 2014); Anthony Thiselton (First Epistle to the Corinthians, NIGTC, 2000); most modern commentators.

**Argument**: Angels are present in the worshiping assembly as guardians of divine order. Just as in the heavenly throne room (Isaiah 6; Revelation 4-5), angels attend where God is worshiped. Improper worship behavior -- including violations of the creation-order structure Paul is arguing for -- offends the angelic beings who uphold that order.

This connects to the broader Second Temple understanding that angels observe human worship (cf. 1 Cor 4:9, "We have become a spectacle to the world, to angels and to men"; Eph 3:10, wisdom made known "to the rulers and authorities in the heavenly realms" through the church). The Qumran evidence Fitzmyer adduced supports this: angels are present in the assembly, and the assembly must be kept in proper order because of their presence.

**Evaluation**: This is the strongest reading. It does not require a speculative Watcher allusion, it fits Paul's immediate context (worship order), and it coheres with Paul's theology of angelic observation of the church (1 Cor 4:9; Eph 3:10).

#### View 3: Angels as Divine Council Members Who Report

**Advocates**: Some scholars influenced by the divine council framework.

**Argument**: The angels Paul references are divine council members who observe human assemblies and report to God's throne. The church's worship is visible to the heavenly court. The woman's head covering is a sign of proper order that testifies before the watching council.

**Evaluation**: This is a subset of View 2, with the specific addition of the divine council reporting function. It is plausible but adds a layer not explicitly in the text.

### The Divine Council Connection

The divine council dimension is real but indirect. Paul's statement presupposes:

1. **Angels are present in Christian worship.** This is a divine council assumption: the heavenly beings are not distant; they attend where God's people gather.
2. **What the church does is visible to, and matters to, the spiritual realm.** This is directly stated in Ephesians 3:10: the manifold wisdom of God is made known to the rulers and authorities THROUGH THE CHURCH.
3. **The Qumran parallel confirms the Jewish background.** The idea that holy angels attend the assembly and expect order is not Pauline innovation; it is Second Temple Jewish worship theology that Paul inherited and Christianized.
4. **The Watcher tradition may be a secondary echo.** Even if Paul's primary point is worship order, the phrase "because of the angels" would have evoked the Watcher tradition for anyone steeped in 1 Enoch. The warning has layers: "Be orderly because angels are present" + (undertone) "you know what happened the last time the boundary between divine and human was violated."

### Divine Council Confidence Rating: **INFERENTIAL**

The divine council connection is real (angels present in worship, the church visible to the spiritual realm) but the specific interpretation of this verse is about worship order, not directly about the divine council framework. The Watcher allusion is a possible secondary meaning but not the primary one.

## 4. Hebrews 1:5-14 and 2:5-9

### Angels, the Son, and the World to Come

### The Texts

**Hebrews 1:5-6**:
> For to which of the angels did God ever say, "You are my Son, today I have begotten you"? Or again, "I will be to him a father, and he shall be to me a son"? And again, when he brings the firstborn into the world, he says, "Let all God's angels worship him."

**Hebrews 1:13-14**:
> And to which of the angels has he ever said, "Sit at my right hand until I make your enemies a footstool for your feet"? Are they not all ministering spirits sent out to serve for the sake of those who are to inherit salvation?

**Hebrews 2:5-9**:
> For it was not to angels that God subjected the world to come, of which we are speaking. It has been testified somewhere, "What is man, that you are mindful of him, or the son of man, that you care for him? You made him for a little while lower than the angels; you have crowned him with glory and honor, putting everything in subjection under his feet."... But we see him who for a little while was made lower than the angels, namely Jesus, crowned with glory and honor because of the suffering of death.

### Greek Key Terms (Hebrews 1)

- **prototokos** (prototokos): "Firstborn." Not first-created (protoktistos, which Hebrews does NOT use) but firstborn -- a title of rank, supremacy, and inheritance rights. The Son is the supreme heir, above all angelic beings.

- **proskynesatosan auto pantes angeloi theou** (proskynesatosan auto pantes angeloi theou): "Let all God's angels worship him." Aorist imperative, 3rd person plural. A COMMAND for all angels to worship the Son.

### The Critical Quotation: Hebrews 1:6 and Deuteronomy 32:43

This is one of the most important textual connections for the divine council framework.

Hebrews 1:6 quotes a text that is NOT in the standard Masoretic Text of Deuteronomy 32:43. The MT reads only: "Rejoice, O nations, with his people." But the LXX and the Dead Sea Scrolls (4QDt^j / 4Q44) preserve a longer reading:

**Dead Sea Scrolls (4QDt^j)**: "Rejoice, O heavens, with him, and worship him, all you divine ones [bene 'elohim]"

**Septuagint**: "Rejoice, O heavens, with him, and let all the angels of God [angeloi theou] worship him"

The author of Hebrews quotes the LXX/DSS reading of Deuteronomy 32:43. This is explosive because Deuteronomy 32 is THE divine council chapter. The Song of Moses -- the text that establishes the territorial allotment of nations to the bene 'elohim (32:8-9) and the divine council framework -- also contains a command for all divine beings to worship the Son.

The author of Hebrews reaches into the Song of Moses and pulls out a verse that commands the entire divine council to worship Jesus. This is not a random proof-text. It is a deliberate selection from the most important divine council passage in the Hebrew Bible.

### Why the Angel Argument? (Hebrews 1:5-14)

The sustained argument that the Son is superior to angels occupies the ENTIRE first chapter of Hebrews. This raises the question: Why? Who needed convincing?

**Three possibilities:**

1. **Some readers confused Jesus with an exalted angel.** In Second Temple Judaism, angels were extremely prominent. Some traditions exalted specific angels (Michael, Metatron, Yahoel) to near-divine status. If some early believers thought Jesus was merely the highest angel, the author corrects this forcefully.

2. **Angel worship was a real temptation.** Colossians 2:18 warns against "worship of angels" (threskeia ton angelon). If the same tendency existed among Hebrews' audience, the author demolishes it by demonstrating the Son's absolute superiority over every angelic being.

3. **The Deuteronomy 32 framework required clarification.** If the audience understood the divine council theology -- that God allotted nations to divine sons (bene 'elohim / angels) -- they needed to know where Jesus stands in that hierarchy. The answer: He is not a member of the council. He is the one the entire council is commanded to worship. He does not receive a territorial allotment; He receives the name above every name.

### Hebrews 2:5-9 -- The World to Come and Angelic Administration

This is where the Deuteronomy 32 framework becomes unmistakable.

**"For it was not to angels that God subjected the world to come [ten oikoumenen ten mellousan]"** (Heb 2:5).

The Greek is precise. Ten oikoumenen = "the inhabited world" (the same word used in the LXX for the nations of the earth). Ten mellousan = "the coming one / the one about to be."

The statement presupposes its converse: **the PRESENT world WAS subjected to angels.** This is the Deuteronomy 32:8 allotment. The nations were distributed to the bene 'elohim. That was the current age's governing structure. But the world to come -- the messianic age, the new creation -- will NOT be under angelic administration.

**Who will administer the world to come?** The author answers by quoting Psalm 8:4-6: "What is man that you are mindful of him?" The human race -- through the representative man Jesus, who was made "lower than the angels for a little while" (Heb 2:9) -- will exercise the dominion that the angels forfeited. The Psalm 82 verdict (corrupt 'elohim stripped of authority) is being executed, and HUMANS (in Christ) are the replacements.

The author of Hebrews explicitly argues:
1. The present world is under angelic administration (implied by the negation in 2:5).
2. This angelic administration is being superseded.
3. The world to come belongs to humanity, through Christ, who took on human nature (2:14-17).
4. The Son was temporarily made lower than the angels (the incarnation) but is now crowned with glory and honor -- and His crowning means human beings inherit what the angels lost.

### The Psalm 8 --> Psalm 82 Connection

The author's quotation of Psalm 8 ("What is man?") is not random. It connects to Psalm 82:8 ("Arise, O God, judge the earth, for you shall inherit all the nations"). Both psalms ask: who governs the world? Psalm 82 says the current divine administrators have failed and will be judged. Psalm 8 says humanity was given dominion over creation. Hebrews brings these together: the failed angelic administration (Deut 32 / Ps 82) is replaced by human dominion in Christ (Ps 8), who was made lower than the angels for a time but is now crowned above them all.

### Hebrews 1:14 -- Angels as Servants of the Heirs

"Are they not all ministering spirits [leitourgika pneumata] sent out to serve [eis diakonian apostellomena] for the sake of those who are to inherit salvation [tous mellontas kleronomein soterian]?"

The inversion is stunning. Under the Deuteronomy 32 arrangement, the bene 'elohim were the administrators and humanity was the administered. Under the new arrangement, the angels are servants (leitourgika pneumata, "serving/ministering spirits") and the HUMANS who believe in Christ are the HEIRS (kleronomein, "to inherit" -- the same inheritance language from Deut 32:9 and Psalm 82:8).

### Divine Council Confidence Rating: **CERTAIN**

The Deuteronomy 32 framework is not merely compatible with Hebrews -- it is the operating framework. The quotation of Deut 32:43 LXX/DSS in Heb 1:6, the sustained angel-Son comparison, the explicit statement that the world to come is NOT subjected to angels, and the Psalm 8 citation as the replacement model all demand the divine council reading. This is the most explicit NT engagement with the Deut 32 worldview outside of Paul's cosmic-powers vocabulary.

## 5. Revelation 12:7-12

### War in Heaven

### The Text

> Now war arose in heaven, Michael and his angels fighting against the dragon. And the dragon and his angels fought back, but he was defeated, and there was no longer any place for them in heaven. And the great dragon was thrown down, that ancient serpent, who is called the devil and Satan, the deceiver of the whole world -- he was thrown down to the earth, and his angels were thrown down with him. And I heard a loud voice in heaven, saying, "Now the salvation and the power and the kingdom of our God and the authority of his Christ have come, for the accuser of our brothers has been thrown down, who accuses them day and night before our God. And they have conquered him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony, for they loved not their lives even unto death." (Revelation 12:7-11, ESV)

### Greek Key Terms

- **polemos en to ourano** (polemos en to ourano): "War in heaven." Polemos = sustained warfare (not a single battle, which would be mache). This is a cosmic military conflict.

- **ho drakon ho megas** (ho drakon ho megas): "The great dragon." The definite articles + the adjective megas give this figure specificity and grandeur. This is THE dragon, the great one.

- **ho ophis ho archaios** (ho ophis ho archaios): "The ancient serpent." Archaios = "from the beginning, primeval." This explicitly links the dragon to the nachash of Genesis 3.

- **ho kaloumenos diabolos kai ho Satanas** (ho kaloumenos diabolos kai ho Satanas): "Called the devil and Satan." Diabolos = Greek for "slanderer/accuser" (the LXX translation of Hebrew satan). Satanas = transliteration of Hebrew hassatan ("the adversary").

- **ho planon ten oikoumenen holen** (ho planon ten oikoumenen holen): "The deceiver of the whole inhabited world." Present active participle of planao ("to lead astray, deceive"). Oikoumene = the inhabited world, the same word used in Hebrews 2:5 for the world subjected/not subjected to angels.

- **ho kategor ton adelphon hemon** (ho kategor ton adelphon hemon): "The accuser of our brothers." Kategor (or kategoros) = "accuser, prosecutor." This is the heavenly-court function of the satan figure in Job 1-2 and Zechariah 3:1 -- the prosecutor who stands before God's throne and brings charges.

### The Identification Chain (v.9)

Revelation 12:9 performs a definitive unification of figures scattered across the Hebrew Bible and Second Temple tradition:

1. **The dragon** = primordial chaos monster (Leviathan, Rahab, Tannin -- Isa 27:1; Ps 74:13-14; Job 26:12-13)
2. **The ancient serpent** = the nachash of Genesis 3 (THC Ch.3)
3. **The devil** = diabolos, the slanderer
4. **Satan** = hassatan, the adversary/prosecutor of Job 1-2

This is one figure. One rebellion. One narrative arc from Genesis 3 to Revelation 20.

### The Timing Debate: Three Major Views

#### View A: Pre-Creation (Original Rebellion)

**Advocates**: Some traditional interpreters, some dispensationalists.

**Argument**: Revelation 12:7-9 describes the original fall of Satan before or at creation, when he was expelled from heaven along with one-third of the angels (Rev 12:4 -- "his tail swept down a third of the stars of heaven").

**Problems**: (1) Satan appears in heaven as the accuser in Job 1-2 and Zechariah 3:1, long after creation. If he was expelled before creation, how is he in the heavenly council? (2) Verse 11 says "they conquered him by the blood of the Lamb" -- this makes no sense as a pre-creation event. (3) The heavenly voice says "NOW have come the salvation and the power" (v.10) -- the arti ("now") is temporal, marking a new development.

#### View B: At the Cross / Resurrection / Ascension (PREFERRED)

**Advocates**: G.K. Beale (Revelation, NIGTC, 1999); Richard Bauckham (Theology of the Book of Revelation, 1993); Sam Storms; many amillennial and already/not-yet interpreters; Michael Heiser.

**Argument**: The war in heaven is the heavenly counterpart of the earthly cross-and-resurrection event. When Christ died and rose, the cosmic consequences rippled through the spiritual realm. Michael and the loyal angels executed the heavenly dimension of Christ's victory. Satan lost his standing as the accuser (he can no longer bring charges against the redeemed, because the blood of the Lamb has satisfied the legal claims) and was expelled from the heavenly court.

**Key evidence**:
- v.10: "NOW have come the salvation and the power and the kingdom of our God and the authority of his Christ" -- this language matches inaugurated eschatology: Christ's authority has arrived.
- v.11: "They conquered him by the blood of the Lamb" -- the cross is the mechanism of the victory.
- v.10: "The accuser of our brothers has been thrown down, who accuses them day and night before our God" -- the accusatory function has been terminated. In Jewish theology, the satan was the heavenly prosecutor. When Christ's blood covers the accused, the prosecutor has no case. He is disbarred.
- **Jesus' own words**: "Now will the ruler of this world be cast out" (John 12:31); "I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven" (Luke 10:18). Jesus speaks of Satan's expulsion as a present-tense or recently-past event connected to his ministry.

Beale writes: "Christ's resurrection and the beginning of his rule are immediately reflected in heaven by Michael's and his angels' defeat of the devil and his hosts. Christ's redemptive work on earth unleashes the effect in heaven of Michael's victory... The actions described are the heavenly counterpart of earthly events."

#### View C: Future (Mid-Tribulation)

**Advocates**: Many dispensationalist/futurist interpreters (Walvoord, Thomas, LaHaye).

**Argument**: The war occurs at the midpoint of the seven-year tribulation. Satan is cast from heaven to earth, triggering the "great tribulation" of the second half (3.5 years = 1,260 days of Rev 12:6).

**Problems**: (1) v.11 explicitly ties the victory to "the blood of the Lamb" -- a past event, not future. (2) The language of v.10 ("now have come...") marks a transition that has already occurred. (3) This view requires a literal chronological reading of Revelation that most scholars find inconsistent with the book's apocalyptic genre.

### Connection to Isaiah 14, Ezekiel 28, Genesis 3

The app's THC Ch.3 (Nachash) and Ch.15, section 15.3 already make the identification chain. Revelation 12:9 is the capstone that explicitly names the connections:

- **Genesis 3**: "the ancient serpent" = the nachash
- **Isaiah 14:12**: "How you have fallen from heaven, O shining one" = the dragon "thrown down" from heaven
- **Ezekiel 28:14-16**: The anointed cherub who was "cast from the mountain of God" = "there was no longer any place for them in heaven"
- **Job 1-2**: The satan who accuses before God's throne = "the accuser of our brothers who accuses them day and night before our God"

### Verse 11 and Colossians 2:15

"And they have conquered him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony, for they loved not their lives even unto death."

The mechanism of victory is threefold:
1. **The blood of the Lamb** -- the objective, historical work of Christ on the cross. This connects directly to Colossians 2:15: Christ "disarmed the rulers and authorities and put them to open shame, by triumphing over them in him."
2. **The word of their testimony** -- the believers' proclamation of Christ's victory. This connects to Ephesians 3:10: "through the church, the manifold wisdom of God should be made known to the rulers and authorities."
3. **They loved not their lives even unto death** -- martyrdom as participation in Christ's own death, the supreme refusal to bow to the dragon's demand for worship.

The cross defeats Satan legally (the accuser has no case). Testimony defeats Satan publicly (his deception is exposed). Martyrdom defeats Satan practically (his ultimate weapon -- death -- is rendered powerless when believers willingly embrace it).

### Divine Council Confidence Rating: **CERTAIN**

Revelation 12 is explicitly a divine council text. It names the council's adversary by every title the Hebrew Bible gives him. It describes a war among the heavenly host. It identifies Michael (Israel's sar from Daniel 10-12) as the commander of the loyal angels. It describes the termination of the satan's accusatory role in the heavenly court. The divine council framework is not an interpretive overlay here -- it IS the passage.

- **THC Chapter 15, section 15.3**: Already contains the identification chain from Rev 12:9 and the dragon imagery. This research adds the timing debate and the v.11/Col 2:15 connection.

## 6. 1 Corinthians 6:2-3

### Believers Judging Angels

### The Text

> Or do you not know that the saints will judge the world? And if the world is to be judged by you, are you incompetent to try trivial cases? Do you not know that we will judge angels? How much more, then, matters pertaining to this life! (1 Corinthians 6:2-3, ESV)

### Greek Key Terms

- **ouk oidate hoti tous angelous krinoumen** (ouk oidate hoti tous angelous krinoumen): "Do you not know that we will judge angels?" Krinoumen = future active indicative of krino ("to judge, decide, govern, render a verdict"). The future tense places this judgment at a specific future time.

- **tous angelous**: "The angels." The definite article tous indicates specific, known angels, not angels in general. Paul does not say "some angels" or "a kind of angel." He says THE angels.

- **hoi hagioi ton kosmon krinousin** (hoi hagioi ton kosmon krinousin): "The saints will judge the world." The saints (hoi hagioi) = the holy ones, the believers. Krinousin = future active of krino. The world (ton kosmon) will be judged by the people of God.

### Which Angels? Three Possibilities

#### View 1: Fallen Angels (the Corrupt 'Elohim of Psalm 82)

**Advocates**: Most commentators, including Heiser (Unseen Realm), Anthony Delgado, and the dominant tradition.

**Argument**: The angels believers will judge are the fallen divine beings -- the corrupt 'elohim of Psalm 82, the territorial princes of Deuteronomy 32, the archai and exousiai of Colossians 2:15. The judgment is the eschatological fulfillment of Psalm 82:8 ("Arise, O God, judge the earth, for you shall inherit all the nations") -- but with a stunning twist: HUMANS participate in that judgment.

The logic chain:
1. Deuteronomy 32:8 -- Nations allotted to the bene 'elohim.
2. Psalm 82:1-7 -- The corrupt 'elohim are indicted and sentenced.
3. Psalm 82:8 -- The psalmist cries out for God to judge the earth and inherit all nations.
4. Daniel 7:9-10 -- The divine court sits, the books are opened.
5. Daniel 7:22 -- "Judgment was given for the saints of the Most High, and the time came when the saints possessed the kingdom."
6. 1 Corinthians 6:3 -- Paul reveals that believers will participate in this judgment of the corrupt divine administrators.
7. Revelation 20:4 -- "I saw thrones, and seated on them were those to whom the authority to judge was committed."
8. Matthew 19:28 -- "You who have followed me will also sit on twelve thrones, judging."

**The divine council inversion**: The 'elohim who "judged unjustly" (Ps 82:2) will themselves be judged by the very humans they were supposed to protect. The corrupt judges become the judged, and the weak creatures they neglected become their judges.

#### View 2: All Angels (including the faithful ones)

**Advocates**: A minority position.

**Argument**: Believers will exercise some kind of governance or assessment over all angelic beings, including the faithful ones, in the eschatological kingdom.

**Problems**: This seems inconsistent with the NT's positive view of faithful angels (Heb 1:14, "ministering spirits"). Why would faithful angels need to be judged?

#### View 3: A General Statement About Believers' Eschatological Authority

**Advocates**: Some who take krino as "govern" rather than "render a verdict."

**Argument**: The Greek krino can mean "to rule, govern, administer" (as Hebrew shophet includes the governance function of Israel's judges). Paul may be saying that believers will have administrative authority over angelic beings in the age to come -- not that they will sit in judgment over them for crimes.

**Evaluation**: This is possible but somewhat weakens the force of Paul's rhetorical point. His argument is: "If you will judge ANGELS, surely you can handle property disputes among yourselves." The rhetorical force requires that judging angels is a bigger deal than judging mundane disputes -- which fits a judicial/verdict sense better than an administrative one.

### The Psalm 82:8 Fulfillment

The connection to Psalm 82:8 is profound. The psalmist cried out: "Arise, O God, judge the earth, for you shall inherit all the nations." The verb is nachal ("to inherit, take possession") -- and Psalm 82:8 says GOD will do this. But Paul says the SAINTS will judge the world (1 Cor 6:2) and judge angels (6:3). This is not a contradiction. It is a fulfillment: God judges the earth THROUGH his people, in union with Christ.

The pattern:
- **Psalm 82:8**: "Arise, O God, judge the earth"
- **Daniel 7:22**: "Judgment was given FOR the saints of the Most High"
- **Matthew 19:28**: "You will sit on twelve thrones, judging"
- **1 Cor 6:2-3**: "The saints will judge the world... we will judge angels"
- **Revelation 20:4**: "I saw thrones, and seated on them were those to whom the authority to judge was committed"

The "we" in 1 Corinthians 6:3 is humanity in Christ -- the new humanity that replaces the failed divine administrators. The Deuteronomy 32 allotment assigned divine beings over the nations. The eschatological kingdom assigns redeemed HUMANS to positions of cosmic authority. The corrupt 'elohim are replaced by the glorified sons of God who earned the title through suffering and faithfulness, not by ontological birthright.

### Divine Council Confidence Rating: **STRONG**

The passage directly addresses the eschatological relationship between believers and angelic beings. The Psalm 82 / Daniel 7 / Matthew 19:28 / Revelation 20:4 chain is tight and mutually reinforcing. Paul assumes his audience already knows this teaching ("Do you not know?"), suggesting it was part of early Christian catechesis.

## 7. Ephesians 1:20-23 and 3:10

### Christ Above All Powers; Wisdom Made Known Through the Church

### The Texts

**Ephesians 1:20-23**:
> ...that he worked in Christ when he raised him from the dead and seated him at his right hand in the heavenly places, far above all rule and authority and power and dominion, and above every name that is named, not only in this age but also in the one to come. And he put all things under his feet and gave him as head over all things to the church, which is his body, the fullness of him who fills all in all.

**Ephesians 3:10**:
> ...so that through the church the manifold wisdom of God might now be made known to the rulers and authorities in the heavenly realms.

### Greek Key Terms (Ephesians 1:20-23)

- **en tois epouraniois** (en tois epouraniois): "In the heavenly places/realms." This phrase occurs five times in Ephesians (1:3, 1:20, 2:6, 3:10, 6:12) -- a uniquely Ephesian theological location. It is the realm where both Christ is enthroned (1:20) and the hostile powers operate (6:12). It is NOT "heaven" in the sense of God's dwelling but the spiritual dimension that overlaps with the physical world.

- **hyperano pases arches kai exousias kai dynameos kai kyriotetos** (hyperano pases arches kai exousias kai dynameos kai kyriotetos): "Far above all rule and authority and power and dominion." The compound preposition hyper-ano ("far above, high above") + the fourfold hierarchy:

  | Greek | Transliteration | Meaning | OT Background |
  |-------|-----------------|---------|----------------|
  | arche | arche | rule, principality | Territorial rulers (Dan 10:13, 20) |
  | exousia | exousia | authority | Delegated power (Deut 32:8) |
  | dynamis | dynamis | power, might | Heavenly host (Ps 103:21 LXX) |
  | kyriotes | kyriotes | dominion, lordship | Ruling authority (cf. Jude 8) |

  This fourfold list is exhaustive. It covers every category of supernatural governance. Christ is above ALL of them.

- **kai en to mellonti** (kai en to mellonti): "And in the one to come" -- not only in this age but in the age to come. This is temporal totality matching the categorical totality of the fourfold list.

- **panta hypetaxen hypo tous podas autou** (panta hypetaxen hypo tous podas autou): "He put all things under his feet." This quotes Psalm 8:6 (the same Psalm used in Hebrews 2:6-8). The "feet" imagery is a royal conquest motif: the victor places his foot on the neck of the defeated. All things -- including the archai and exousiai -- are under Christ's feet.

### Greek Key Terms (Ephesians 3:10)

- **polypoikilos sophia tou theou** (polypoikilos sophia tou theou): "The manifold/many-colored wisdom of God." Polypoikilos is a compound: poly (many) + poikilos (varied, variegated, multi-colored). This word was used for intricate embroidered fabrics and the varied plumage of birds. God's wisdom is not simple or monochrome; it is richly complex, and its FULL complexity is displayed through the church.

- **gnoristo tais archais kai tais exousiais en tois epouraniois** (gnoristhe tais archais kai tais exousiais en tois epouraniois): "Might be made known to the rulers and authorities in the heavenly realms." Aorist passive subjunctive of gnorizo ("to make known, reveal"). The passive indicates God as the agent: GOD causes his wisdom to be made known. The recipients are the archai and exousiai -- the same categories of spiritual powers from 1:21 and 6:12.

- **dia tes ekklesias** (dia tes ekklesias): "Through the church." The preposition dia + genitive = "through, by means of." The church is the INSTRUMENT through which God announces his wisdom to the cosmic powers.

### The Theological Architecture

#### Ephesians 1:20-23: Christ's Position

The passage establishes Christ's place in the cosmic hierarchy:
1. He was raised from the dead (the resurrection).
2. He was seated at God's right hand in the heavenly realms (the ascension/enthronement).
3. He was placed FAR ABOVE every category of spiritual authority (supremacy over the divine council).
4. ALL things were put under his feet (total subjugation, Psalm 8).
5. He was given as HEAD over all things TO THE CHURCH (the church benefits from his cosmic authority).

The Deuteronomy 32 connection: Under the old arrangement, the bene 'elohim held authority over the nations. Under the new arrangement, Christ holds authority over the bene 'elohim, and the church is his body through which that authority is exercised on earth.

#### Ephesians 3:10: The Church as Cosmic Announcement

This may be the most astonishing verse in Paul's entire corpus regarding the church's role in the divine council framework. The church is not merely the recipient of God's blessings. The church is the VEHICLE through which God announces his wisdom TO the cosmic powers.

What does this mean practically?

1. **The very existence of the church is a message to the powers.** The church consists of redeemed people from "every nation under heaven" (Acts 2:5) -- the same nations that were allotted to the corrupt 'elohim in Deuteronomy 32:8. Every Gentile believer is a person reclaimed from a territorial 'elohim's domain. The multi-ethnic church IS the message: "Your dominion is over."

2. **The church demonstrates what the 'elohim failed to do.** The corrupt 'elohim were supposed to govern the nations with justice (Psalm 82:3-4: "Give justice to the weak and the fatherless; maintain the right of the afflicted and the destitute"). The church, at its best, does exactly this -- caring for the poor, defending the oppressed, maintaining justice. Every act of justice by the church is a witness to the powers that their replacement has arrived.

3. **The reconciliation of Jew and Gentile is the specific display.** The context of Ephesians 3:10 is Paul's "mystery" -- that Gentiles are fellow heirs with Jews in one body (3:6). Under Deuteronomy 32, Israel belonged to YHWH and the nations belonged to the 'elohim. The wall between them was the cosmic allotment itself. When that wall is broken down (Eph 2:14, "he has broken down in his flesh the dividing wall of hostility"), the Deuteronomy 32 partition is dissolved. The rulers and authorities in the heavenly realms SEE this happening, and it announces their obsolescence.

4. **The powers are watching.** Paul assumes the spiritual realm observes the church. This connects to 1 Corinthians 4:9 ("We have become a spectacle to the world, to angels and to men"), 1 Corinthians 11:10 ("because of the angels"), and 1 Timothy 5:21 ("In the presence of God and of Christ Jesus and of the elect angels I charge you..."). The angelic realm is not distant or uninvolved; it is an audience to the drama of redemption unfolding in the church.

### Divine Council Confidence Rating: **CERTAIN**

Ephesians 1:20-23 uses the same fourfold hierarchy terminology that maps directly to the divine council categories (archai, exousiai, dynameis, kyriotetes). Ephesians 3:10 explicitly states that the church's purpose includes making God's wisdom known TO the rulers and authorities. The Deuteronomy 32 framework is the necessary background: the nations were allotted to divine rulers; the church is the multi-ethnic community that demonstrates those rulers' displacement. This is as explicit as the NT gets about the church's role in the divine council story.
