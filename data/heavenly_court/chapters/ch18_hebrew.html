Chapter 18

Hebrew & Greek Deep Dive — The Lexicon of the Unseen Realm

A consolidated reference for every key term in the divine-council vocabulary, with root analysis, semantic range, and theological significance.

The following lexicon gathers every major term discussed throughout this study into a single reference. Each entry provides the original language form, root etymology, semantic range, key occurrences, and theological function within the divine-council framework.

Why does a chapter-length treatment of Hebrew, Greek, and Aramaic vocabulary matter for understanding the divine council? Because theology is encoded in vocabulary. The ancient authors did not write systematic theology papers; they selected words whose semantic ranges carried implicit claims about the nature of reality. When the psalmist writes ’elohim in Psalm 82:1, he is making a claim that no English translation can fully capture — a claim about a category of being that is neither “God” in the monotheistic sense nor “god” in the pagan sense, but something the English language has no single word for. When Paul writes archai and exousiai in Ephesians 6:12, he is drawing on a vocabulary that his first-century audience would have recognized as pointing to the same divine-council realities that the Hebrew scriptures describe — but refracted through Hellenistic categories that add new dimensions of meaning.

The careful study of these terms reveals three things that casual reading misses entirely. First, the semantic overlap between Hebrew and Ugaritic/Akkadian cognates demonstrates that the divine-council concept was not a late Israelite invention but part of the oldest stratum of Semitic religious thought. Second, the deliberate translation choices made by the Septuagint translators show how Second Temple Judaism interpreted these terms — choices that directly shaped how the New Testament authors deployed Greek vocabulary to describe spiritual realities. Third, the Aramaic terminology that emerges in Daniel and the intertestamental literature introduces new categories — Watchers, the Son of Man — that bridge the Hebrew Bible and the New Testament in ways that are invisible without linguistic analysis.

This chapter is designed as both a reference tool and a sustained argument. The tables provide quick lookup; the expanded entries below each table provide the depth needed to understand why these terms matter, not just what they mean.

Expanded Etymology: ’elohim (אֱלֹהִים)

The term ’elohim is the single most important word in the divine-council lexicon, and also the most misunderstood. Its root is debated: most scholars derive it from the proto-Semitic *’il- (god, power, might), with the -āh suffix producing the singular ’eloah (אֱלוֹהַ), and the -îm suffix producing the plural. The Akkadian cognate ilu (pl. ilānu) carries the same base meaning: a divine being, a member of the category “deity.” Ugaritic ’il (pl. ’ilm) is virtually identical in form and function.

The critical insight is that ’elohim is a category term, not a proper noun. It does not mean “God” in the way English speakers use that word (as a name for a specific supreme being). It means “a being that belongs to the divine/spiritual realm.” This is why the same word can refer to Yahweh (Gen 1:1), to the members of his council (Ps 82:1, 6), to the spirit of the dead prophet Samuel (1 Sam 28:13), and even to demons (Deut 32:17). The word does not indicate a level of power; it indicates a plane of existence. Whatever inhabits the spiritual world — as opposed to the material, human world — is ’elohim.

This semantic range is precisely what makes Psalm 82 so explosive. When the psalmist writes ’elohim nitsav ba’adat-’El, b’qerev ’elohim yishpot (“God stands in the divine assembly; in the midst of the gods he renders judgment”), both uses of ’elohim are grammatically identical. Context alone distinguishes the presiding ’elohim (singular verb, Yahweh) from the judged ’elohim (plural referents, the council members). This is not ambiguity — it is precision. The Hebrew language encodes a theological reality that English cannot: the supreme God and his subordinate divine beings share an ontological category (both are “spirit beings”) but occupy radically different positions within it.

Key Passage: Psalm 82:1 — Parsing the Double ’elohim
Hebrew: אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת־אֵל בְּקֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים יִשְׁפֹּט

First ’elohim: subject of nitsav (3ms Niphal, “he takes his stand”) — singular verb = singular divine referent = Yahweh.
ba’adat-’El: “in the assembly of El/God” — the council belongs to ’El, the Most High. This is a construct chain identical to Ugaritic ‘dt ’il.

The verse establishes the entire divine-council hierarchy in nine Hebrew words: one supreme ’elohim presides over and judges many subordinate ’elohim within a formal assembly (‘adah).
Expanded Etymology: sôd (סוֹד)

The Hebrew noun sôd derives from the root s-w-d, which in its basic sense means “to sit together, to consult intimately.” The Arabic cognate sāda carries the sense of being a leader or master, while the Ugaritic cognate sôd appears in council contexts. The semantic range of sôd in biblical Hebrew includes three interlocking meanings: (1) a close circle of confidants or advisors, (2) the secret or confidential information shared within that circle, and (3) the act of intimate consultation itself. All three meanings are operative in the divine-council framework.

In Jeremiah 23:18, the prophet asks: “For who among them has stood in the sôd of Yahweh, and has seen and heard his word?” Here sôd means both the council-chamber and the privileged information disclosed there. The true prophet is distinguished from the false prophet precisely by having been granted access to Yahweh’s sôd — both the place and the plans discussed in it. Verse 22 makes this explicit: “But if they had stood in my sôd, they would have proclaimed my words to my people.”

Amos 3:7 makes the theological claim absolute: “Surely the Lord Yahweh does nothing without revealing his sôd to his servants the prophets.” The prophets function as human members of the divine council — they are admitted to the sôd (assembly), receive the sôd (secret decree), and then transmit it to the human realm. This is why prophetic speech begins with “Thus says Yahweh” — the formula of a royal messenger delivering the decree of a king who has deliberated with his council.

Psalm 89:7 [Hebrew v. 8] uses sôd in parallel with qahal (assembly): “God is greatly feared in the sôd of the holy ones, and awesome above all who surround him.” This is the divine council depicted as a royal court, with Yahweh so exalted that even the members of his own inner circle tremble before him.

Expanded Etymology: ‘adah (עֲדָה)

The noun ‘adah derives from the root ‘-d-h, which conveys the idea of appointing, assembling, or meeting at a designated place and time. The root is cognate with Ugaritic ‘dt (assembly, congregation) and Arabic ‘adda (to count, enumerate). In biblical Hebrew, ‘adah most commonly refers to the “congregation of Israel” — the formal assembly of the covenant people (Exod 12:3; Num 1:2; Josh 22:12). But when the psalmist applies it to the divine realm in Psalm 82:1, the word carries all of its “earthly” connotations upward: this is a formal, convened, purposeful assembly, not a casual gathering. The divine council meets by appointment, for business, under the authority of its presider.

The construct phrase ‘adat-’El (“assembly of God/El”) is structurally identical to the Ugaritic ‘dt ’ilm (“assembly of the gods”). This cognate precision is one of the strongest pieces of evidence that the divine-council concept in Israel was not a late theological invention but a very old Semitic inheritance. The psalmist is deploying technical vocabulary that his audience would have recognized from the broader Canaanite religious milieu — but reframing it: the assembly belongs to ’El, who is identified with Yahweh, and the assembled ’elohim are not peer deities but subordinate beings under judgment.

Expanded Etymology: q’dôshîm (קְדֹשִׁים)

The plural noun q’dôshîm comes from the root q-d-sh, whose fundamental meaning is “to be set apart, to be other.” The root appears across all Semitic languages: Akkadian qadāshu (to be pure, consecrated), Ugaritic qdsh (holy, sacred), Aramaic qaddish (holy one). The English word “holy” tends to connote moral purity, but the Semitic root is primarily ontological: it describes something that belongs to a different category of existence, something separated from the ordinary or profane. A qādôsh is a being that belongs to the divine realm — a being that is fundamentally other than human.

In Psalm 89:5 [Hebrew v. 6], the heavens praise Yahweh’s faithfulness “in the assembly of the q’dôshîm.” In verse 7, Yahweh is “feared in the sôd of the q’dôshîm.” These are not human saints but divine beings — members of the heavenly court whose defining characteristic is their otherness from the created material world. Zechariah 14:5 envisions Yahweh coming “with all his q’dôshîm” at the end of days — the divine council descending to earth to execute final judgment.

The term’s semantic range creates an important bridge to the New Testament. When Jude 14 quotes 1 Enoch 1:9 — “Behold, the Lord came with ten thousands of his holy ones (hagioi)” — the Greek hagioi translates q’dôshîm, and the referents are divine-council members accompanying Yahweh in theophanic judgment. The “saints” of this passage are not deceased Christians; they are the q’dôshîm — the holy divine beings of Yahweh’s entourage.

Expanded Etymology: mal’akh (מַלְאָךְ)

The noun mal’akh derives from the root l-’-k, meaning “to send.” The word is thus fundamentally a functional title, not an ontological classification: a mal’akh is “one who is sent” — a messenger, an envoy, an emissary. The Akkadian cognate mālaku carries the same diplomatic/royal-court sense: a messenger dispatched by a king. Ugaritic ml’ak is identical in both form and function. In Aramaic, mal’akha’ continues the same meaning.

The semantic range in biblical Hebrew is striking in its breadth. Mal’akh can refer to: (1) human messengers sent between kings (Gen 32:3; Num 20:14; Judg 11:13); (2) human prophets acting as God’s spokesmen (Hag 1:13; Mal 1:1 — the prophet’s name itself means “my messenger”); (3) the priests (Mal 2:7, “the mal’akh of Yahweh of hosts”); and (4) supernatural divine beings sent from God’s presence to execute his decrees (Gen 19:1; Exod 23:20; 2 Sam 24:16; Ps 103:20).

The phrase mal’akh Yahweh (“the angel/messenger of Yahweh”) presents a special case. In several passages (Gen 16:7–13; Gen 22:11–18; Exod 3:2–6; Judg 6:11–24; Judg 13:3–22), the mal’akh Yahweh speaks as Yahweh in the first person, is identified as Yahweh by the narrator, and yet is somehow distinct from Yahweh. This “two powers” phenomenon — Yahweh’s messenger who is Yahweh — was a major topic of debate in Second Temple Judaism and became a crucial datum for early Christology.

Expanded Etymology: nāchāsh (נָחָשׁ)

The term nāchāsh is a textbook case of how Hebrew roots encode multiple layers of meaning. The consonantal root n-ch-sh supports three distinct vocalizations, each producing a different word: (1) nāchāsh (noun): serpent — the common word for a snake (Gen 3:1; Exod 4:3; Num 21:7); (2) nachash (verb): to practice divination, to observe omens (Gen 30:27; Lev 19:26; Deut 18:10); (3) n’chôshet (noun, related): bronze, copper — a shining metal (Num 21:9; 2 Ki 18:4). The Akkadian cognate naḫāshu means “to be abundant, to prosper” and carries associations of brilliance and radiance.

All three semantic threads converge in the Eden narrative. The being in Genesis 3 is called hannāchāsh (“the nāchāsh”). On the surface level, this is “the serpent.” But the same root yields the adjective “shining, brilliant” and the verb “to divine, to enchant.” The being in Eden is simultaneously a serpent, a shining one (compare sārāph, the “burning/fiery” serpentine beings of Isaiah 6), and a practitioner of forbidden knowledge. Ezekiel 28:13–17 identifies this being as an anointed k’rûv (guardian cherub) who was “on the holy mountain of God” and walked “among the stones of fire” — a resplendent divine being, not a talking garden snake.

The Mosaic connection is also revealing. In Numbers 21:6, Yahweh sends n’chāshîm s’rāphîm (“fiery serpents”) among the people. In verse 8, Moses is told to make a sārāph and set it on a pole; he makes a n’chash n’chôshet (“serpent of bronze/copper”) — a wordplay that exploits the full semantic range of n-ch-sh. The bronze serpent is simultaneously a “serpent made of shining metal” and a representation of the fiery serpentine divine beings. Jesus explicitly connects this image to the crucifixion in John 3:14–15, creating a typological arc from the shining serpent in Eden to the bronze serpent in the wilderness to the Son of Man “lifted up” on the cross.

Expanded Etymology: k’rûv (כְּרוּב) & sārāph (שָׂרָף)

The noun k’rûv (plural k’rûvîm) almost certainly derives from the Akkadian karābu (“to bless, to pray”) or the related kāribu (an intercessory figure, a guardian being). In Mesopotamian art and literature, kāribu are the colossal winged figures — often human-headed bulls or lions — that guard temple and palace entrances. Their function is protective and intercessory: they stand at the boundary between sacred and profane space. This is precisely the function of the biblical cherubim. In Genesis 3:24, cherubim guard the entrance to Eden — the boundary between God’s space and the now-cursed human domain. In Exodus 25:18–22, two cherubim overshadow the kapporet (mercy seat) atop the ark, forming a throne for Yahweh’s invisible presence. Yahweh is regularly called “he who sits enthroned upon the cherubim” (yôshev hak’rûvîm, 1 Sam 4:4; 2 Sam 6:2; Ps 80:1; Ps 99:1). Ezekiel’s elaborate vision (Ezek 1; 10) depicts the cherubim as the living creatures that bear Yahweh’s throne-chariot — composite beings with four faces (human, lion, ox, eagle), four wings, and bodies full of eyes.

The noun sārāph (plural s’rāphîm) derives from the root s-r-p, which means “to burn.” A sārāph is literally a “burning one” or “fiery one.” The term appears in two distinct contexts: as a category of serpentine creature (Num 21:6, 8; Deut 8:15; Isa 14:29; 30:6 — “flying sārāph”) and as a class of divine beings attending Yahweh’s throne (Isa 6:2, 6). In Isaiah’s throne-room vision, the s’rāphîm have six wings (two covering the face, two covering the feet, two for flying) and cry “Holy, holy, holy is Yahweh of Hosts.” One of them takes a burning coal from the altar and touches it to Isaiah’s lips — a purification ritual that grants Isaiah the ability to speak as a member of the divine council.

The connection between the serpentine and the throne-attending uses is not accidental. In the ancient Near East, serpentine divine beings were commonly associated with fire, radiance, and divine guardianship. Egyptian uraeus cobras guarded the pharaoh’s crown; Mesopotamian mūshḫush (serpent-dragons) guarded temple gates. The seraphim of Isaiah 6 are thus the Israelite counterpart to a widespread ancient Near Eastern tradition of fiery, serpentine, divine throne guardians — beings who embody the terrifying holiness of the divine presence.

Expanded Etymology: sār (שַׂר) & ‘Elyôn (עֶלְיוֹן)

The noun sār derives from the root s-r-r, meaning “to exercise authority, to rule.” Its semantic range includes “prince, captain, commander, chief, ruler.” The Akkadian cognate sharru means “king” — a higher register than the Hebrew, where melekh is the standard word for king and sār denotes a subordinate ruler or military commander. In Daniel 10, however, sār takes on a specifically supernatural meaning. The angelic messenger tells Daniel that he was delayed twenty-one days by the sār (prince) of Persia (Dan 10:13), and that the sār of Greece would come after (Dan 10:20). Michael is called “your sār” (Dan 10:21) and “the great sār” (Dan 12:1). This is the Deuteronomy 32 worldview made explicit: each nation has a supernatural sār, a divine-council member assigned as its territorial ruler, and these sārîm can come into conflict with each other — and with God’s emissaries.

The title ‘Elyôn (“Most High”) derives from the root ‘-l-y (“to go up, to be high, to ascend”). The Ugaritic cognate ‘ly functions identically, and the Aramaic form ‘illay (Dan 7:25) carries the same superlative force. ‘Elyôn is the supreme divine title — it positions Yahweh not merely as powerful but as ontologically above all other divine beings. In Deuteronomy 32:8 (reading with 4QDeutj), ‘Elyôn apportions the nations “according to the number of the b’nei ’elohim” — the Most High stands above the entire council, distributing governance to his subordinates. In Psalm 82:6, Yahweh addresses the corrupt council members: “I said, ‘You are ’elohim, and all of you are sons of ‘Elyôn.’” The title establishes the hierarchy: they are ’elohim (divine beings), but he is ‘Elyôn (the one above them all).

Expanded Etymology: n’philim (נְפִילִים) & r’pha’im (רְפָאִים)

The noun n’philim is traditionally derived from the root n-p-l (“to fall”), yielding the meaning “fallen ones.” The question is: fallen in what sense? Three interpretations have been proposed. First, they are the “fallen” divine beings themselves (the b’nei ha’elohim who descended to earth). Second, they are the offspring whose existence results from the “fall” of the divine beings. Third, the Aramaic root npl can mean “giant” (as in the naphila of the Targumim), suggesting that n’philim simply means “giants” without etymological reference to falling. The context of Genesis 6:4 supports the second interpretation: the n’philim are the offspring produced by the union of the b’nei ha’elohim with human women — beings whose very existence is the result of a boundary violation, a “fall” from the proper divine-human separation. The text calls them haggibborim asher me’olam anshei hashem (“the mighty ones of old, men of renown”) — language that maps directly onto the Greek concept of demigod heroes.

The r’pha’im present a different puzzle. The root r-p-’ in its basic form means “to heal” (cf. the verb rāphā’, “to heal,” and the noun rôphe’, “physician”). Yet the noun r’pha’im refers to two seemingly unrelated things: (1) the shades of the dead in Sheol (Isa 14:9; 26:14, 19; Ps 88:10; Prov 2:18; 9:18; Job 26:5) and (2) named giant clans in the Transjordan region (Deut 2:11, 20; 3:11, 13; Josh 12:4; 13:12; 17:15). The Ugaritic cognate rp’um resolves the apparent contradiction: in the Ugaritic texts, the rp’um are the deified dead kings and warriors, the mighty of old who have passed into the underworld and receive cult offerings. They are simultaneously dead heroes and supernatural powers. The biblical r’pha’im carries this same dual register: the giant clans are descended from the pre-flood Nephilim, and when they die, their spirits become the r’pha’im of Sheol — not ordinary human dead, but restless, powerful shades. This explains the otherwise puzzling statement in Isaiah 26:14: “The dead do not live; the r’pha’im do not rise.” This is not a general denial of resurrection but a specific declaration that the hybrid dead — the spirits of the Nephilim/giant lineages — will not participate in the resurrection of the righteous.

Expanded Etymology: shātān (שָׂטָן) & rûach (רוּחַ)

The noun shātān derives from the root s-t-n, meaning “to oppose, to accuse, to act as an adversary.” In its semantic range, shātān can denote: (1) a human adversary (1 Sam 29:4; 1 Ki 5:4; 11:14, 23, 25); (2) the angel of Yahweh acting as an obstructer (Num 22:22, 32 — the angel “stood in the road as a shātān against him”); (3) a specific divine-council member functioning as a prosecuting attorney (Job 1:6–12; 2:1–7; Zech 3:1–2). In Job 1–2, the figure is called hashātān (“the accuser”) with the definite article — indicating a role, not a proper name. He appears among the b’nei ha’elohim when they present themselves before Yahweh, and he functions as the court prosecutor: testing the integrity of Yahweh’s servant Job by bringing accusations. In Zechariah 3, the same figure stands at Yahweh’s right hand to accuse the high priest Joshua, and Yahweh rebukes him. By 1 Chronicles 21:1, the word appears without the definite article — shātān as a proper noun — marking a transition from a council role to a specific identified adversary.

The noun rûach (“spirit, wind, breath”) from the root r-w-ch is one of the most versatile words in biblical Hebrew. Its semantic range includes: (1) wind or moving air (Gen 8:1; Exod 10:13); (2) breath or life-force (Gen 6:17; Ps 104:29); (3) the human spirit or disposition (Gen 41:8; Prov 16:32); (4) the Spirit of God (Gen 1:2; Judg 3:10; Isa 11:2); (5) a divine-council member or spiritual entity (1 Ki 22:21–23; 1 Sam 16:14–16; Judg 9:23). The last category is critical for the divine-council framework. In 1 Kings 22:19–23, Micaiah describes a vision of Yahweh’s council: “I saw Yahweh sitting on his throne, and all the host of heaven standing beside him on his right hand and on his left. And Yahweh said, ‘Who will entice Ahab?’ … Then a rûach came forward and stood before Yahweh, saying, ‘I will entice him.’” This rûach is a divine-council member — a spiritual being who volunteers for a mission, receives authorization from Yahweh, and goes out to execute the decree as a “lying spirit in the mouth of his prophets.” The rûach ra’ah (“evil spirit”) of 1 Samuel 16:14 — described as being “from Yahweh” — belongs to the same category: a spiritual entity operating under divine authorization.

Expanded Analysis: archē (ἀρχή) and exousia (ἐξουσία)

These two terms form the core of Paul’s vocabulary for describing the hostile spiritual powers. Archē derives from archô (“to rule, to begin, to be first”) and carries the meaning “first place, primacy, rule, domain.” In political Greek, archai are magistrates, rulers, governing authorities. In the LXX, the word translates sar (prince/ruler) in Daniel 10, where the sar of Persia and the sar of Greece are supernatural territorial rulers. Paul inherits this precise usage: the archai of Ephesians 6:12 are the spiritual sarîm — the princes of the divine council who were assigned to the nations at Babel and subsequently corrupted their mandate.

Exousia derives from exesti (“it is permitted, it is lawful”) and means “authority, delegated power, the right to act.” In the LXX, the word translates the Aramaic sholtān (authority, dominion) in Daniel 7:6, 14, 27. When Paul pairs archai with exousiai, he is making a specific structural claim: these spiritual beings possess both rank (they are rulers) and jurisdiction (they have delegated authority over specific domains). They are not rogue agents; they were given their authority by the Most High (Col 1:16: “whether thrones or dominions or rulers or authorities — all things were created through him and for him”). Their rebellion does not annul their structural position; it corrupts it. This is why cosmic redemption requires not merely forgiveness of human sin but the disarming of the powers (Col 2:15) and the subjection of all rule, authority, and power to Christ (1 Cor 15:24).

The Greco-Roman audience would have heard these terms through a double lens. In the civic sphere, archai and exousiai were the magistrates and authorities of the Roman provincial system. In the religious/philosophical sphere, they mapped onto the cosmic hierarchy that Hellenistic Judaism and Middle Platonism both recognized: a layered cosmos of spiritual rulers mediating between the supreme God and the material world. Paul’s claim is that both referents are real and connected — the earthly powers and the heavenly powers are not separate categories but different manifestations of the same cosmic governance structure.

Expanded Analysis: dynamis (δύναμις) and kyriotēs (κυριότης)

The noun dynamis (“power, might, miracle”) has a wide semantic range in Greek: physical strength, military force, miraculous ability, and in the plural dynameis, the “powers” — either abstract or personified. The LXX translators chose dynamis to render the Hebrew tsāvā’ (host, army) in the divine title Yahweh ts’va’ôt (“Lord of Hosts”), which becomes Kyrios tôn dynameôn (“Lord of the Powers”). This translation choice is theologically loaded: it reframes the Hebrew military metaphor (Yahweh as commander of the heavenly army) as a claim of cosmic sovereignty (Yahweh as lord of all powers in the universe). When Paul places dynamis in his lists of spiritual entities (Eph 1:21; Rom 8:38), he is drawing on this LXX tradition — the dynameis are the heavenly host, the assembled powers of the cosmos, and Christ is exalted “far above” all of them.

The noun kyriotēs (“lordship, dominion”) derives from kyrios (“lord, master”) and denotes the authority or domain of a lord. It does not directly translate any specific Hebrew term in the LXX and appears to be a New Testament contribution to the vocabulary of cosmic hierarchy. In Colossians 1:16, kyriotētes appears alongside thronoi, archai, and exousiai as one of four categories of created spiritual authority. In Jude 8 and 2 Peter 2:10, false teachers are condemned for “rejecting kyriotēta and blaspheming the doxas (glorious ones)” — a charge that describes treating the angelic hierarchy with contempt. The implication is that even corrupt spiritual powers retain a dignity rooted in their created status; to blaspheme them is to blaspheme the hierarchical order that God established.

Expanded Analysis: kosmokratôr (κοσμοκράτωρ) and stoicheia (στοιχεῖα)

The compound noun kosmokratôr (“world-ruler”) appears only once in the New Testament (Eph 6:12) and is not found in the LXX. Its background lies in Greco-Roman astrological and magical texts, where kosmokratôres are the planetary deities who govern the cosmos — Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, and the rest, each ruling a sphere of influence over earthly affairs. By deploying this term, Paul is making a provocative claim: the spiritual forces that pagan religion identifies as cosmic governors are real entities, but they are not benevolent gods — they are agents of “the darkness of this age” (tou skotous tou aiônos toutou). The full phrase in Ephesians 6:12 is devastating: the believer’s struggle is “against the kosmokratôras of this darkness, against the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly places.” Paul takes the highest title pagan theology can bestow and reassigns it to the hostile side of the cosmic conflict.

The noun stoicheia (singular stoicheion) is one of the most debated terms in Pauline scholarship. Its basic meaning is “element, fundamental component” — it can refer to the letters of the alphabet (the “elements” of writing), the physical elements (earth, water, air, fire), the elemental principles of a philosophical system, or the astral bodies (stars and planets) conceived as divine beings. In Galatians 4:3, 9, Paul says the Galatians were formerly “enslaved to the stoicheia of the world” and asks why they want to return to “weak and worthless stoicheia.” In Colossians 2:8, 20, he warns against being taken captive by philosophy “according to the stoicheia of the world” and declares that believers have “died with Christ to the stoicheia of the world.”

The divine-council framework resolves the ambiguity. The stoicheia are the spiritual beings associated with the fundamental structures of the cosmos — the beings who were assigned governance over the elements, the nations, the calendar, and the natural order. They correspond to the b’nei ’elohim of Deuteronomy 32:8 — the divine-council members who received jurisdiction over the nations and the structures of creation. Paul’s argument is that before Christ, both Jews (under Torah as administered by angels, Gal 3:19) and Gentiles (under the stoicheia as administered by corrupt divine rulers) were in a state of cosmic subjection. The cross liberates humanity from both forms of spiritual bondage.

Expanded Analysis: tartaroô (ταρταρόω) and daimonion (δαιμόνιον)

The verb tartaroô (“to cast into Tartarus”) is a hapax legomenon — it occurs exactly once in the entire New Testament, at 2 Peter 2:4: “God did not spare the angels who sinned, but tartarosas [having cast them into Tartarus], delivered them to chains of gloomy darkness to be kept for judgment.” In Greek mythology, Tartarus is not merely the underworld but the deepest prison beneath the underworld — the place where the Titans were confined after their defeat by the Olympian gods. Peter’s use of this loaded mythological term is deliberate: he is telling his Greco-Roman audience, “You know the story of divine beings who rebelled against the supreme god and were imprisoned in the deepest abyss? That story is real, and here is what actually happened.” The imprisoned beings are the b’nei ha’elohim of Genesis 6:1–4 — the divine-council members who violated the boundary between the spiritual and material realms. Peter is mapping the Hebrew Watchers tradition (1 Enoch 6–16) onto Greco-Roman cosmological categories.

The noun daimonion (“demon, divine being”) has a fascinating semantic history. In classical Greek, daimôn was a neutral or even positive term: a divine being, a guiding spirit (Socrates spoke of his personal daimonion as a benevolent inner voice). In the LXX, however, the translators chose daimonia to translate the Hebrew shedim (שֵׁדִים) in Deuteronomy 32:17 and Psalm 106:37 — passages that describe Israel sacrificing to beings that are “not God” (lo’ ’eloah) and are identified as ’elohim they had not known. This LXX translation choice redefines the Greek word: what the Greeks called daimonia (lesser divine beings worthy of reverence) are what the Hebrews called shedim (entities that receive illicit worship). Paul exploits this bilingual connection in 1 Corinthians 10:20–21: “What pagans sacrifice they offer to daimoniois and not to God. I do not want you to be participants with daimoniois.” The demons of the New Testament are not a new category of being; they are the corrupt ’elohim of the Old Testament, the beings behind the idols, the divine-council members who exchanged their commission for worship.

17.3 Aramaic Terms (Daniel & Intertestamental Literature)

The Aramaic sections of Daniel (2:4b–7:28) and the Aramaic portions of 1 Enoch introduce a set of terms that bridge the Hebrew Bible and the New Testament in crucial ways. Aramaic was the lingua franca of the Near East from the Neo-Babylonian period through the Roman era — the language Jesus spoke daily, the language of the Targumim and much of the Talmud. Its divine-council vocabulary draws on the same Semitic roots as Hebrew but develops them in distinctive directions that profoundly influenced Second Temple Judaism and, through it, early Christianity.

Expanded Analysis: ‘ir (עִיר) — Watcher

The Aramaic noun ‘ir (plural ‘irîn) derives from the root ‘-w-r, meaning “to be awake, to be watchful, to keep vigil.” The Hebrew cognate ‘ēr carries the same sense (cf. Jer 5:6, “a leopard watches over their cities”; Song 5:2, “I slept but my heart was awake”). The term appears in the canonical Bible exclusively in Daniel 4, where Nebuchadnezzar recounts his dream: “I saw in the visions of my head as I lay in bed, and behold, an ‘ir, a qaddish, came down from heaven” (Dan 4:13). The pairing ‘ir w’qaddish (“a watcher and a holy one”) functions as a hendiadys: a holy watcher, a wakeful divine being. In Daniel 4:17, the decree against Nebuchadnezzar is described as “the sentence of the ‘irîn, the decision of the qaddishîn” — the Watchers function as a judicial body, a sub-council within the divine assembly that renders verdicts on earthly rulers.

The Watchers tradition explodes in 1 Enoch (the Book of the Watchers, chapters 1–36, composed c. 3rd century BC in Aramaic). Here the ‘irîn are two hundred divine beings who descend from heaven to Mount Hermon, swear an oath, and take human wives — the events of Genesis 6:1–4 narrated in elaborate detail. Their leader Shemihazah and his lieutenant Azazel teach forbidden knowledge to humanity: metalworking for weapons, cosmetics, sorcery, astrology, and the cutting of roots (pharmacology/herbalism). The offspring of these unions are the Nephilim giants, who devour the produce of humanity and then turn to devouring humans themselves. The faithful angels — Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, Uriel — report the corruption to God, who decrees judgment: the Watchers are bound and imprisoned until the final judgment; the giants are destroyed in the Flood; and the disembodied spirits of the dead giants become the demons (daimonia) who plague humanity until the end of the age.

This Enochic Watcher tradition provides the conceptual bridge between Genesis 6, the divine-council rebellion, and the demonology of the New Testament. When Jude 6 speaks of “the angels who did not stay within their own position of authority, but left their proper dwelling,” he is summarizing the Watcher narrative. When 2 Peter 2:4 says God “cast them into Tartarus,” he is mapping the imprisonment of the Watchers onto Greco-Roman cosmology. The Aramaic term ‘ir stands at the headwaters of this entire tradition.

Expanded Analysis: bar enash (בַר אֱנָשׁ) — Son of Man

The Aramaic phrase bar enash (or bar enosh) is composed of bar (“son,” cognate to Hebrew ben) and enash (“man, human being,” cognate to Hebrew ’enosh and ’ish). At its most basic level, the phrase means “a human being” or “one who looks human.” But in Daniel 7:13–14, the phrase acquires cosmic significance:

Daniel 7:13–14 — The Divine-Human Figure
“I saw in the night visions, and behold, with the clouds of heaven there came one like a bar enash, and he came to the ‘Attiq Yomin (Ancient of Days) and was presented before him. And to him was given sholtān (dominion) and y’qar (glory) and malkhu (kingdom), that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him; his dominion is an everlasting dominion, which shall not pass away, and his kingdom one that shall not be destroyed.”

The scene is a divine-council throne vision. The Ancient of Days is seated (y’tiv, Dan 7:9), thrones (karsawān) are set up, the court sits (dina’ y’tiv, Dan 7:10), and books are opened. Into this assembly comes a figure who looks human (k’bar enash, “like a son of man”) but arrives on the clouds of heaven — a mode of transport exclusively associated with deity in the Hebrew Bible (Ps 68:4; 104:3; Isa 19:1; Deut 33:26). The bar enash is thus a paradox: he has a human form but exercises divine prerogatives. He receives universal, everlasting dominion — authority that elsewhere belongs only to God.

The reception history of this phrase is immense. In 1 Enoch 37–71 (the Parables of Enoch), the “Son of Man” becomes a pre-existent divine figure who sits on the throne of glory and judges the kings and mighty of the earth. In 4 Ezra 13, a “Man” rises from the sea and flies with the clouds of heaven, destroying enemies with the breath of his mouth. Jesus’s self-designation as “the Son of Man” (ho huios tou anthrôpou in Greek, but presumably bar enash in his Aramaic speech) draws on this entire tradition. When he tells the high priest “you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming on the clouds of heaven” (Mark 14:62), the high priest tears his robes — because he recognizes the Daniel 7 allusion and its implication: Jesus is claiming to be the divine-human figure who receives universal dominion from the Ancient of Days. The claim is not merely messianic; it is a claim to participation in the divine council at its highest level.

Expanded Analysis: ‘attiq yômîn (עַתִּיק יוֹמִין) — Ancient of Days

The Aramaic phrase ‘attiq yômîn combines ‘attiq (old, advanced, ancient, from the root ‘-t-q, “to advance, to move forward”) with yômîn (days, from y-w-m). The title conveys absolute eternity: this is the One who has been present through all days, who precedes time itself. The imagery of Daniel 7:9 is unprecedented in the Hebrew Bible in its specificity: “His clothing was white as snow, and the hair of his head like pure wool; his throne was fiery flames; its wheels were burning fire.” The white hair denotes not frailty but ultimate authority — the wisdom of infinite age. The fiery throne recalls Ezekiel’s chariot-throne vision (Ezek 1:4, 13, 27) and establishes that this is the same God who spoke to Israel.

The Ancient of Days presides over the final and definitive session of the divine council. Daniel 7:9–10 describes the scene in terms drawn from the royal-court paradigm that has structured divine-council imagery from its earliest appearances: thrones are set in place, the Judge takes his seat, the court convenes (dina’ y’tiv), and the records are examined (siphrîn p’tichiu). This is Psalm 82 brought to its ultimate fulfillment — the same God who “stands in the divine assembly” to judge the ’elohim now sits in final judgment over all the beast-kingdoms and their supernatural patrons. The beasts of Daniel 7 (representing successive empires) are stripped of their authority, and that authority is transferred to the bar enash and, through him, to the “people of the qaddishîn of the Most High” (Dan 7:27).

Expanded Analysis: shemayya’ (שְׁמַיָּא) — Heaven

The Aramaic noun shemayya’ (emphatic state; absolute sh’mayin) is the direct cognate of Hebrew shāmayim, from the root sh-m-y (“to be high, lofty”). In Daniel’s Aramaic, heaven is not merely the sky but the seat of divine governance. The phrase “the God of heaven” (’elah sh’mayya’, Dan 2:18, 19, 37, 44) is the standard Persian-period designation for the supreme God, reflecting both the cosmic theology of the divine council and the diplomatic language of the Achaemenid empire. Daniel 4:26 contains a remarkable metonymy: “your kingdom will be restored to you when you acknowledge that shemayya’ rules” — “heaven” stands for “the God who dwells in heaven and governs from heaven through his council.” This metonymic usage becomes standard in Second Temple Judaism and explains Matthew’s preference for “the kingdom of heaven” (hē basileia tôn ouranôn) rather than “the kingdom of God” — not because heaven is a place the kingdom exists, but because heaven is a shorthand for God and his governing council.

17.4 Key Phrases — Multi-Word Expressions

Many of the most theologically loaded expressions in the divine-council framework are multi-word phrases whose meaning exceeds the sum of their parts. These phrases function as technical terminology — fixed expressions that carry a specific freight of theological meaning every time they appear. Understanding them as phrases, not just as individual words, is essential for reading the texts as their authors intended.

Full Analysis: b’nei ha’elohim — “Sons of God” across Contexts

The phrase b’nei ha’elohim appears in three distinct biblical contexts, and understanding the differences between these uses is essential for grasping the full scope of the divine-council concept.

Genesis 6:2, 4 — The Boundary Violation. “The b’nei ha’elohim saw that the daughters of man were beautiful, and they took as their wives any they chose” (Gen 6:2). Here the phrase identifies divine beings who transgress the boundary between the spiritual and material realms by entering into sexual unions with human women. The offspring are the Nephilim — a hybrid class that should not exist. The text presents this as an act of cosmic rebellion: divine beings exercising autonomous choice (vayyiqchu lahem nashim mikkol asher bacharu, “they took for themselves wives from all whom they chose”) in defiance of the created order. This interpretation is confirmed by every ancient Jewish source that comments on the passage: the LXX translates b’nei ha’elohim as hoi angeloi tou theou (“the angels of God”) in several manuscripts; 1 Enoch 6–16 narrates the story in elaborate detail; Jude 6–7 explicitly references it; 2 Peter 2:4 summarizes the judgment. The “Sethite interpretation” (that the b’nei ha’elohim are merely the righteous line of Seth) is a late patristic innovation (Augustine, 5th century AD) that finds no support in any pre-Christian source.

Job 1:6; 2:1 — The Council in Session. “Now there was a day when the b’nei ha’elohim came to present themselves before Yahweh, and hashātān also came among them.” Here the phrase describes the divine council in its formal assembly. The b’nei ha’elohim “present themselves” (l’hityatstsev ‘al-Yahweh) before Yahweh — the technical language of a royal court audience. The accuser (hashātān) is among them as a functional member of the council, not as an outsider. Yahweh initiates dialogue, authorizes the accuser’s activity, and sets limits on it. The council is a governance structure, not a democratic parliament: Yahweh presides, the b’nei ha’elohim attend, and decrees are issued by the presider alone.

Job 38:7 — The Creation Witnesses. “When the morning stars sang together and all the b’nei ’elohim shouted for joy?” This is the earliest chronological reference: the divine-council members were present at the creation of the physical world. They are “morning stars” (kokhvei voqer) and “sons of God” simultaneously — reinforcing the connection between the divine council and the celestial bodies that runs throughout the Bible and the ancient Near East. The b’nei ’elohim are created beings who existed before the material cosmos; they witnessed its construction and celebrated it. This means the divine council predates the physical universe — it is part of the infrastructure of creation itself, not an afterthought.

Full Analysis: ts’va’ hashamayim — “Host of Heaven”

The phrase ts’va’ hashamayim (“host of heaven”) appears in two distinct but related contexts, and the tension between them is theologically productive.

Military/Liturgical Context. In 1 Kings 22:19, Micaiah sees Yahweh on his throne with “all the host of heaven standing beside him on his right hand and on his left.” Here the ts’va’ is the assembled divine court — spiritual beings arrayed around the throne in a posture of readiness and service. In Nehemiah 9:6, the Levites declare: “You made the heavens, the heaven of heavens, with all their ts’va’, and the ts’va’ of heaven worships you.” In Psalm 103:21, the ts’va’ are “his ministers who do his will.” In Luke 2:13, the angelic announcement of Jesus’s birth is accompanied by “a multitude of the heavenly host (stratias ouraniou)” — the Greek stratia (“army”) translating the Hebrew military sense of ts’va’. The host of heaven is simultaneously an army, a choir, and a court.

Idolatrous/Astral Context. In Deuteronomy 4:19, Moses warns Israel: “When you look up to the heavens and see the sun, moon, and stars — all the ts’va’ hashamayim — do not be enticed to bow down to them and worship them, things that Yahweh your God has allotted (chalaq) to all the peoples under the whole heaven.” Here the “host of heaven” includes the celestial bodies as objects of worship — and Yahweh is said to have allotted them to the nations. This is the Deuteronomy 32:8–9 worldview in action: the celestial bodies are not merely physical objects but are associated with the divine beings who govern the nations. The nations were assigned their celestial patrons; Israel was assigned to Yahweh directly. In 2 Kings 21:3, Manasseh “worshiped all the ts’va’ hashamayim and served them” — an act of apostasy precisely because Israel was not supposed to worship the host allotted to the nations but was to worship Yahweh alone.

The dual meaning of the phrase encodes the divine-council theology: the host of heaven is legitimate when it serves and worships Yahweh (1 Ki 22:19; Neh 9:6), and illegitimate when it receives the worship of the nations in place of Yahweh (Deut 4:19; 2 Ki 17:16; 21:3, 5). The same beings can function as Yahweh’s loyal servants or as idolatrous rivals, depending on whether they execute their commission faithfully or corrupt it.

Full Analysis: kol b’nei ‘Elyôn — “All Sons of the Most High” (Ps 82:6)

This phrase appears in what may be the most consequential single verse in the entire divine-council corpus. Psalm 82:6: ’anî ’amarti ’elohim ’attem, ûv’nei ‘Elyôn kull’khem — “I said, ‘You are ’elohim, and all of you are sons of the Most High.’” Yahweh addresses the corrupt divine-council members directly. The phrase b’nei ‘Elyôn functions as a title of derivation: they are “sons” of the Most High in the sense that they owe their existence and their authority to him. But verse 7 delivers the verdict: “’akhen (nevertheless), like ’adam (humans) you shall die, and like one of the sarîm (princes) you shall fall.” The judgment is a demotion from the divine category to the mortal category: beings who were ’elohim and b’nei ‘Elyôn will be stripped of their divine prerogatives and subjected to death — a fate that does not naturally belong to their ontological class.

Jesus cites this verse in John 10:34–36, in what amounts to a qal wahomer (lesser to greater) argument: “If he called them theoi (gods) to whom the word of God came — and Scripture cannot be broken — do you say of him whom the Father consecrated and sent into the world, ‘You are blaspheming,’ because I said, ‘I am the Son of God’?” Jesus’s argument assumes the divine-council reading of Psalm 82: the ’elohim of that psalm are genuine divine beings (not human judges, as some later interpreters claimed). If they can be called ’elohim, then Jesus — the one the Father consecrated and sent — can certainly be called “Son of God” without blasphemy. The argument only works if the ’elohim of Psalm 82 are actual divine beings, not metaphorical human rulers.

Full Analysis: rûach rā‘āh — “Evil Spirit”

The phrase rûach rā‘āh (“evil spirit” or “spirit of harm/calamity”) appears in three passages that are central to understanding how the divine council administers judgment.

Judges 9:23. “God sent an rûach rā‘āh between Abimelech and the leaders of Shechem.” This is a divine-council decree executed by a spiritual agent: God dispatches a spirit that sows discord as an act of poetic justice against Abimelech’s illegitimate kingship. The rûach is not acting independently; it is a commissioned agent carrying out Yahweh’s verdict.

1 Samuel 16:14–16, 23. “The Spirit of Yahweh departed from Saul, and an rûach rā‘āh me’et Yahweh (evil spirit from Yahweh) tormented him.” The text is explicit: the tormenting spirit is from Yahweh. It replaces the rûach Yahweh (Spirit of Yahweh) that had previously empowered Saul. The vocabulary choice is deliberate: both the empowering Spirit and the tormenting spirit are rûach — both are spiritual agents operating under Yahweh’s sovereign authority. The evil spirit does not act in rebellion against God; it acts as an instrument of God’s judgment on Saul’s disobedience. David’s music can alleviate the torment (1 Sam 16:23), which implies that the spirit’s activity can be mitigated but not canceled — the judgment stands, but its severity has degrees.

1 Kings 22:19–23. This is the fullest narrative depiction of the rûach as a divine-council agent. Micaiah describes seeing Yahweh on his throne, surrounded by the host of heaven. Yahweh asks, “Who will entice Ahab so that he goes up and falls at Ramoth-gilead?” Various proposals are made. Then a rûach steps forward: “I will go out and be a lying spirit (rûach sheqer) in the mouth of all his prophets.” Yahweh authorizes the mission: “Go out and do so.” This passage provides the most transparent window into divine-council operations: Yahweh consults his council, a specific spiritual being volunteers for a task, the task is authorized, and the spirit goes out to execute the decree. The spirit is an agent, not an independent actor; the deception of Ahab is Yahweh’s judgment, not the spirit’s freelancing.

17.5 The Lexical Arc — From Creation Council to Final Restoration

The vocabulary analyzed in this chapter is not a random collection of terms. It tells a story — or rather, it provides the lexical infrastructure for the story that runs from Genesis to Revelation. The terms map onto a narrative arc that can be traced through four movements.

Movement 1: Creation — The Council Assembled
At creation, the b’nei ’elohim (“sons of God”) are present as witnesses and worshipers (Job 38:7). They are q’dôshîm (“holy ones”), set apart by their divine nature. They constitute the ts’va’ hashamayim (“host of heaven”), the assembled court of Yahweh ‘Elyôn (“the Most High”). The k’rûvîm guard God’s holy space; the s’rāphîm attend his throne. The nāchāsh — the “shining one” — holds a position of honor as an anointed guardian (Ezek 28:14). The council is intact, the hierarchy is functioning, and the vocabulary describes a world of order.
Movement 2: Rebellion — The Council Fractured
The nāchāsh rebels in Eden (Gen 3). The b’nei ha’elohim descend to take human wives (Gen 6:1–4), becoming the ‘irîn (“Watchers”) of the Enochic tradition. Their offspring are the n’philim (“fallen ones”) and later the r’pha’im (giant clans / shades of Sheol). After Babel, Yahweh allots the nations to the b’nei ’elohim as territorial sārîm (“princes”), but they corrupt their mandate: the ts’va’ hashamayim becomes an object of illicit worship (Deut 4:19; 17:3). The ‘adah (“assembly”) continues to function, but some of its members have become adversaries — shātān — and rûach rā‘āh (“evil spirits”) execute judgments on a corrupted earth. The vocabulary of order now describes disorder.
Movement 3: Judgment — The Council Called to Account
Psalm 82 is the pivot point. Yahweh ’elohim stands in the ‘adat-’El (“assembly of God”) and pronounces judgment: the corrupt ’elohim, the b’nei ‘Elyôn, will “die like ’adam” (Ps 82:7). Daniel 7 depicts the execution of this decree: the ‘Attiq Yomin (“Ancient of Days”) convenes the final court session. The bar enash (“Son of Man”) approaches on the clouds and receives the sholtān (“dominion”) that the corrupt sārîm had seized. The Watchers (‘irîn) are bound; the rebel ’elohim are tartaroô-ed (cast into Tartarus, 2 Pet 2:4). The archai and exousiai are “disarmed” and “put to open shame” at the cross (Col 2:15). The kosmokratôres are exposed as agents of darkness (Eph 6:12). The vocabulary of judgment replaces the vocabulary of rebellion.
Movement 4: Restoration — The Council Reconstituted
The final vision of Scripture depicts a new divine council. The risen Christ sits at the right hand of Power (Mark 14:62) — the bar enash enthroned, as Daniel 7 foretold. Redeemed humans, who were once lower than the ’elohim (Ps 8:5), are elevated: they “will judge angelous” (1 Cor 6:3), reversing the hierarchy that the corrupt council exploited. In Revelation 4–5, twenty-four elders sit on thronoi (thrones) around the supreme throne — a reconstituted council of worship. The q’dôshîm of Zechariah 14:5, the holy ones who return with Yahweh, now include both angelic beings and resurrected humans. The ts’va’ hashamayim is restored to its original function: not as an object of idolatrous worship, but as the worshiping court of the King of kings. The stoicheia are no longer masters but servants of the new creation (2 Pet 3:10–13). The sôd that was once restricted to prophets becomes the open inheritance of all who are “in Christ” — “the mystery (mystērion) hidden for ages” is now “revealed to his holy ones (hagioi)” (Col 1:26).
The lexicon of the unseen realm is not a static glossary. It is the vocabulary of a story — a story of a council created in glory, fractured by rebellion, called to account in judgment, and reconstituted in the final restoration. Every term in this chapter finds its place within this arc, and the arc itself is the spine of the entire biblical narrative.

This is the ultimate payoff of the linguistic deep dive. The terms are not curiosities for scholars; they are the building blocks of a worldview. A worldview in which the physical and spiritual realms are not separate compartments but a single integrated reality. A worldview in which history is not merely the record of human actions but the arena of cosmic conflict between Yahweh and the rebellious members of his council. A worldview in which redemption is not merely the forgiveness of individual sins but the reclamation of the entire cosmos from the powers that corrupted it. And a worldview in which the final chapter is not the destruction of the spiritual realm but its healing — the restoration of the divine council to its original purpose: the worship and service of the Most High, now and forever.
