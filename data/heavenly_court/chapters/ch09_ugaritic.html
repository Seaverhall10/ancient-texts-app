Chapter 9

Ugaritic & Canaanite Parallels — The Closest Cognates

Ugarit, a Late Bronze Age city destroyed c. 1185 BC, provides the closest linguistic and conceptual parallels to the Hebrew divine council — proving the concept was deeply rooted in the Semitic world.

The Ugaritic texts, discovered at Ras Shamra (modern Syria) beginning in 1929, are written in a Semitic language closely related to Hebrew. They provide the closest linguistic cognates to biblical divine-council vocabulary, confirming that Israel’s council theology was not a late theological invention but part of the oldest Semitic religious framework.

What the Ugaritic Cognates Prove
1. The divine council is not a late Israelite invention. The vocabulary is shared with texts from c. 1400–1200 BC, predating most biblical composition. Israel did not invent the concept; they inherited and corrected it.
2. The Hebrew writers were aware of the Canaanite framework. When Psalm 82:1 uses ‘adat-’El, the psalmist is deliberately deploying cognate terminology — using the Canaanite council vocabulary to make a polemical claim: Yahweh presides over the assembly that Canaan attributed to El.
3. The differences are as important as the similarities. In Ugarit, El is an aging, sometimes passive deity who can be manipulated by other gods. In Israel, Yahweh is unchallenged sovereign. In Ugarit, Baal fights Mot (Death) and loses temporarily. In Israel, Yahweh defeats all adversaries effortlessly. The biblical texts use the shared vocabulary to subvert the shared mythology.
The Ugaritic evidence demonstrates that the divine council was the common theological currency of the ancient Semitic world. Israel’s contribution was not the concept itself but the radical insistence that one God — Yahweh, the ‘Elyôn — is absolutely supreme, uncreated, and unchallengeable, while all other ’elohim are created, subordinate, and accountable.

The shared vocabulary establishes that Israel and Canaan were working from the same conceptual universe. But Israel was not simply absorbing Canaanite theology — the biblical authors were systematically overwriting it, claiming for Yahweh what Ugarit credited to Baal.

9.2 The Baal Cycle & Biblical Polemic

The Baal Cycle (KTU 1.1–1.6) describes Baal’s conflict with Yamm (Sea) and Mot (Death), his enthronement on Mount Zaphon, and his construction of a palace. Several biblical passages directly polemicize against this narrative:

Isaiah 27:1 — The Ugaritic Smoking Gun
Isaiah 27:1: “In that day the LORD with his hard and great and strong sword will punish Leviathan the fleeing serpent, Leviathan the twisting serpent, and he will slay the dragon that is in the sea.”

KTU 1.5.I.1–3: “When you [Baal] struck Litan the fleeing serpent, finished off the twisting serpent, the tyrant with seven heads…”

The Hebrew livyātān (לִוְיָתָן) is a direct cognate of Ugaritic Ltn (Litan). The epithets “fleeing serpent” (nāchāsh bāriach) and “twisting serpent” (nāchāsh ‘aqallatôn) are word-for-word matches with the Ugaritic. Isaiah is not borrowing a myth — he is reassigning Baal’s victory to Yahweh, declaring that what Canaan credited to a subordinate deity was actually the work of the Most High.

9.3 El, Father of the Gods — The Corrupted Memory of the Most High C

Behind the polytheistic veneer of Ugaritic religion stands a figure who looks remarkably like a distorted memory of the God of Genesis. El (’il), the supreme deity of the Canaanite pantheon, bears epithets that map directly onto attributes of Yahweh — but with each one subtly degraded.

tr ’il — “Bull El” Symbol of virility and power tr = bull, strength
Hebrew cognate: shôr (שׁוֹר, bull). Compare Deuteronomy 32:4: “The Rock” — Yahweh’s strength is immovable, not merely biological.
ab adm — “Father of Humanity” Creator/progenitor title ab = father; adm = humanity
Hebrew cognate: ’āb (אָב) + ’ādām (אָדָם). Echoes Genesis 1:27 — God as creator of ’ādām. In Ugarit, El’s “fatherhood” is biological (he sires gods); in Scripture, it is creative and covenantal.
ltpn — “The Kind / Beneficent One” Character epithet l-t-p-n = skillful, benevolent
El is wise and gentle — the aged patriarch. Compare Psalm 86:15: “You, O Lord, are a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger.” Yahweh’s kindness flows from moral perfection; El’s flows from passivity — he can be manipulated by Baal and Anat.
ab snm — “Father of Years” Primordial age ab = father; snm = years
Compare Daniel 7:9: “the Ancient of Days took his seat” — ‘Attiq Yômîn (עַתִּיק יוֹמִין). The Ancient of Days is eternal and sovereign; El is merely old and increasingly sidelined.
El’s Cosmic Mountain

El’s dwelling is described in language that directly parallels biblical cosmology. He resides at the “source of the two rivers” (mbk nhrm) in the “midst of the fountains of the double-deep” (qrb ’apq thmtm) — a well-watered cosmic mountain where heaven and earth intersect and divine decrees are issued. El and his “assembled congregation” (phr m’d) meet at the “tents of El” (dd ’il).

Why the Biblical Authors Use Canaanite Geography
When Isaiah 14:13 describes the rebel declaring he will ascend to the “mount of assembly in the far reaches of the north,” the Hebrew tsaphôn (צָפוֹן) is the direct cognate of Ugaritic Sapanu — the name of Baal’s mountain. The prophets are not borrowing pagan theology. They are reclaiming cosmic real estate: the mountain Canaan assigned to Baal belongs to Yahweh, and the rebel who tries to seize it is Yahweh’s creature in revolt, not a rival deity.

9.4 The Seventy Sons and the Four-Tier Assembly C

The Ugaritic council was not a loose gathering of equals. It had a formal four-tier hierarchy that maps directly onto the biblical structure of the divine assembly — and it contains a numerical detail that connects it to the most important text in this entire study.

The Four-Tier Council — Ugarit and the Bible

2. Royal Sons / Princes“Sons of El” (bn ’ilm); one holds the title “Most High”“Sons of God” (b’nei ’elohim); holy ones, princesJob 1:6; 38:7; Dan 10:13
3. Functional SpecialistsCraftsman deities (e.g., Kothar-wa-Khasis)Seraphim, cherubim, living creaturesIsa 6:2; Ezek 1:5

The structural parallel is unmistakable. But the critical divergence is at the top: in Ugarit, El shares authority with a consort and can be outmaneuvered by subordinate gods. In Scripture, Yahweh is absolutely sovereign. No consort. No rival. The “sons of God” are created beings, not biological offspring.

The Seventy Sons of El — The Deuteronomy 32 Connection

Here is where the Ugaritic evidence becomes devastating for any attempt to reduce the divine council to metaphor. The Ugaritic texts describe the bn ’ilm as the seventy sons of Asherah and El who serve as the titulary deities presiding over the peoples of the known world.

The number is not coincidental. KTU 1.65 demonstrates the full formula: “il bn il / dr bn il / mphrt bn il” — “El, sons of gods, the circle of the sons of gods, the totality of the sons of gods.”

Deuteronomy 32:8–9 (ESV, following 4QDeutj / LXX)
When the Most High gave to the nations their inheritance, when he divided mankind, he fixed the borders of the peoples according to the number of the sons of God. But the LORD’s portion is his people, Jacob his allotted heritage.
The Seventy — Why This Connection Is Foundational
1. Seventy nations in Genesis 10. The Table of Nations lists exactly 70 peoples descended from Noah’s three sons. This is the total population of the post-Babel world.
2. Seventy sons of El at Ugarit. The Canaanite council contains 70 “sons of El” who preside over the nations. Same number, same cultural region, same cosmic-political arrangement.
3. Deuteronomy 32:8–9 provides the biblical explanation. God divided the nations “according to the number of the sons of God” — 70 nations assigned to 70 divine administrators. Israel alone He kept for Himself: “the LORD’s portion is his people” (v. 9).
4. The Dead Sea Scrolls confirm the original reading. 4QDeutj reads b’nei ’elohim (“sons of God”) where the Masoretic Text has b’nei Yisra’el (“sons of Israel”). The Septuagint agrees (“angels of God”). Ugarit now provides a third independent witness to the original reading.
5. Ugarit preserves the corruption, not the invention. The Canaanites did not invent the 70-son council — they inherited the memory of a real divine arrangement and corrupted it into polytheism. What was originally Yahweh delegating authority to created beings became El biologically siring 70 divine sons.
Three independent streams of evidence — the Hebrew text (Dead Sea Scrolls), the Greek translation (Septuagint), and the Canaanite tablets (Ugarit) — converge on the same picture: 70 divine beings assigned over 70 nations. This is the Deuteronomy 32 worldview, confirmed by Israel’s closest linguistic neighbors. The council is not metaphor. It is not late invention. It is the oldest Semitic theology on record — and Israel’s distinctive claim was not the council itself, but the identity of its sovereign.

9.5 Baal vs. Mot — Death and the Counterfeit Resurrection C

The final two tablets of the Baal Cycle (KTU 1.5–1.6) describe Baal’s confrontation with Mot (“Death” — Ugaritic mt, Hebrew cognate māvet, מָוֶת). The sequence follows a pattern that later echoes in the greatest story ever told — but with critical distortions that reveal the counterfeit.

Why Baal’s “Resurrection” Is a Counterfeit, Not a Prototype
Claim: “The Christ story borrowed from Baal and other dying-and-rising god myths.”
1. Baal dies because he is weak. Mot overpowers him — he is swallowed involuntarily. Christ dies voluntarily: “No one takes my life from me. I lay it down of my own accord” (John 10:18).
2. Baal does not defeat Death himself. His sister Anat destroys Mot while Baal lies dead. Christ conquers death from within: “Through death he might destroy the one who has the power of death” (Heb 2:14).
3. Baal’s return is cyclical, not historical. The Baal–Mot conflict represents the agricultural cycle of seasons — a repeating pattern with no teleology. Christ’s resurrection is a one-time, irreversible historical event: “Christ, being raised from the dead, will never die again” (Rom 6:9).
4. Baal’s return changes nothing cosmically. After his restoration, Mot remains a threat and the cycle continues. After Christ’s resurrection, death itself is sentenced: “The last enemy to be destroyed is death” (1 Cor 15:26).
5. The Deuteronomy 32 explanation. If the nations received corrupted versions of divine truth at Babel, we should expect garbled death-and-resurrection echoes in cultures administered by fallen b’nei ’elohim. These parallels do not undermine the Gospel — they are predicted by the framework.

The Baal Cycle preserves a corrupted echo of the cosmic pattern: a divine king descends into the domain of death and emerges victorious to be enthroned on the holy mountain. That the Canaanites possessed this narrative structure, however garbled, is precisely what the Deuteronomy 32 worldview predicts. The nations carried fragments of the truth. Only Israel received the full story — and only in Christ did the pattern find its historical fulfillment.
