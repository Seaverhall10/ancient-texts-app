Chapter 17

The Destination — New Heaven, New Earth, God With Man

The cosmic war that began in Eden does not end with disembodied souls floating in “Heaven.” It ends with a renewed physical creation, resurrected bodies, and the unmediated presence of God dwelling with humanity on earth — forever.

Every war has a destination. The divine-council conflict described throughout this study — from the creation council of Job 38, through the rebellions of Genesis 3 and 6, through the allotment and corruption of nations, through the cross and the church age — is not heading toward “going to Heaven when you die.” A

The Bible’s destination is far more concrete. It is Eden restored and expanded. It is physical. It is on earth. It is corporate. And it resolves every thread of the cosmic rebellion by permanently reuniting what was separated: God and humanity, in the same place, without mediation.

16.1 The Old Testament Promise — New Heavens and New Earth

Isaiah 65:17, 21–22 ESV
“For behold, I create new heavens and a new earth, and the former things shall not be remembered or come into mind.”… 21“They shall build houses and inhabit them; they shall plant vineyards and eat their fruit. 22They shall not build and another inhabit; they shall not plant and another eat; for like the days of a tree shall the days of my people be, and my chosen shall long enjoy the work of their hands.”

Isaiah describes a renewed physical world — houses, vineyards, labor, and longevity. This is not a vision of ethereal spirits in a cloud realm. It is material creation healed and permanent. The same prophet declares: “Your dead shall live; their bodies shall rise” (Isaiah 26:19). A

Isaiah 65:25 ESV
“The wolf and the lamb shall graze together; the lion shall eat straw like the ox, and dust shall be the serpent’s food. They shall not hurt or destroy in all my holy mountain,” says the LORD.

This reverses Genesis 3:14 — the nachash was cursed to eat dust. In the new creation, the serpent still eats dust, but its dominion is broken. Predator-prey violence is undone. This is not metaphor for political peace — Isaiah describes the removal of the curse from the biological order itself. The “holy mountain” (הַר קָדְשִׁי, har qodshî) is Eden language (Ezek 28:14). Isaiah envisions not a return to Eden but an expansion of Eden’s peace to fill the entire earth. A

Isaiah 66:22–23 ESV
“For as the new heavens and the new earth that I make shall remain before me, says the LORD, so shall your offspring and your name remain. 23From new moon to new moon, and from Sabbath to Sabbath, all flesh shall come to worship before me, declares the LORD.”

Isaiah 66 closes the prophetic book with the same vision: new heavens and new earth. The critical detail: all flesh (כָל־בָּשָׂר, kol-basar) will worship Yahweh. Not Israel alone. All flesh. This reverses Deuteronomy 32:8–9: where Babel scattered the nations under lesser ’elohim, the new creation gathers all nations to worship God directly. The Sabbath worship “from new moon to new moon” implies ongoing, embodied liturgical life — dynamic worship in renewed bodies on renewed ground. A

Key Hebrew Terms — Isaiah’s New Creation
בּוֹרֵא bôrê’
Qal Participle ms ברא b-r-’
“Creating.” The root bara’ (ברא) is used exclusively with God as subject. It denotes creation ex nihilo or sovereign transformation. Isaiah uses the same verb as Genesis 1:1 (bara’ ’Elohim): a new creative act on the same scale as the original. The participle form implies ongoing action — God is in the process of making all things new.
חֲדָשִׁים ḥadashîm
Adjective, masculine plural חדש ḥ-d-sh
“New.” Hebrew distinguishes ḥadash (renewed, fresh, restored) from ’acher (another, different, replacement). Isaiah uses ḥadashîm — newness as renewal and transformation, not replacement. The new creation is not a different creation. It is this creation made new. The groaning creation of Romans 8 is not discarded — it is renewed. The same root appears in Lamentations 3:23: “his mercies are new (ḥadashîm) every morning.” God’s pattern is to make old things new, not to destroy and start over.
Isaiah 11 and 65 — The Curse Reversed in Nature
Genesis 3:14–19 introduced five curse dimensions: (1) serpent cursed; (2) enmity between seeds; (3) pain in childbearing; (4) ground cursed with thorns; (5) death. Isaiah systematically reverses each one.
Isaiah 11:6–9 reverses the animal curse: wolf with lamb, leopard with goat, calf and lion together. The violence from the fall is undone — “they shall not hurt or destroy in all my holy mountain.”
Isaiah 25:8 reverses death: “He will swallow up death forever.” Revelation 21:4 quotes this directly.
Isaiah 55:13 reverses the ground curse: “Instead of the thorn shall come up the cypress.” Thorns (Gen 3:18) replaced by noble trees.
Isaiah 65:23 reverses the childbearing curse: “They shall not labor in vain or bear children for calamity.”
Isaiah walks through the specific curses of Genesis 3 and shows each one undone. The new creation is not a different story. It is the same story completed — every thread reversed, every wound healed, every dimension of the curse lifted. A

16.2 The Whole Creation Groans — Cosmic Scope of Redemption

Romans 8:19–23 ESV
For the creation waits with eager longing for the revealing of the sons of God. 20For the creation was subjected to futility, not willingly, but because of him who subjected it, in hope 21that the creation itself will be set free from its bondage to corruption and obtain the freedom of the glory of the children of God. 22For we know that the whole creation has been groaning together in the pains of childbirth until now. 23And not only the creation, but we ourselves, who have the firstfruits of the Spirit, groan inwardly as we wait eagerly for adoption as sons, the redemption of our bodies.

Paul’s scope is breathtaking: the redemption Christ purchased extends to the creation itself. The physical world is not discarded — it is set free. And the final step of human redemption is not departure from bodies but the redemption of our bodies. Salvation is not escape from the material world. It is the liberation of the material world. A

Key Greek — Romans 8:20–21
ματαιότητι mataiotēti
Noun, feminine, dative singular ματαιότης mataiotēs
“Futility, emptiness, purposelessness.” The LXX’s standard rendering of Hebrew hevel (הֶבֶל) — the keyword of Ecclesiastes. Paul says creation has been placed under the same condition the Preacher laments. But he adds what Ecclesiastes does not: hope. The subjection was done in hope (eph’ helpidi, ἐφ’ ἑλπίδι) — the futility was always intended to be temporary.
δουλείας τῆς φθορᾶς douleias tēs phthoras
Genitive, feminine singular + genitive feminine singular δουλεία douleia + φθορά phthora
“Bondage of decay/corruption.” Douleia = slavery, bondage — the same word used for Israel’s slavery in Egypt (cf. Rom 8:15). Phthora = corruption, decay, dissolution. Paul personifies creation as a slave under the tyranny of entropy. The physical universe is not merely aging — it is enslaved. And enslaved things can be emancipated. Just as God liberated Israel from Egyptian bondage, he will liberate the cosmos from the bondage of decay. The Exodus is the template for the cosmic redemption.
συστενάζει καὶ συνωδίνει systenazei kai synôdinei
Present active indicative, 3rd singular (x2)
“Groans together and suffers birth-pains together.” Both verbs carry the prefix syn- (together) — the whole creation (pasa hē ktisis) in unified anguish. But the metaphor is birth pains, not death throes. Labor produces new life. The suffering of creation is productive — moving toward a delivery, not a funeral. Jesus used the same imagery (John 16:21).
Key Greek — Romans 8:19
ἀποκάλυψιν apokalypsin
Noun, feminine, accusative singular ἀποκάλυψις apokalypsis
“Revealing, uncovering, disclosure.” From apo- (away) + kalyptein (to cover/hide). This is not a quiet disclosure or a private realization. It is the same root that names the Book of Revelation (Apokalypsis Iēsou Christou). Paul is choosing the most dramatic Greek word for “unveiling” available to him — the ripping away of a veil to expose what has been hidden. What creation is waiting for is not a gentle improvement but a dramatic, public manifestation of the true sons of God in their glorified state.
τῶν υἱῶν τοῦ θεοῦ tôn huiôn tou theou
Genitive, masculine plural + genitive singular
“Of the sons of God.” Paul uses huioi (υἱοί, “sons”) — a term of rank, position, and inheritance — not tekna (“children,” emphasizing birth/nature as in John 1:12). This is deliberate. The huioi theou language maps directly onto the LXX rendering of b’nei ’elohim in the divine-council passages. Paul is placing redeemed humanity into the positional category that was previously occupied by the supernatural sons of the heavenly court — and declaring that creation itself is straining forward to see it happen.

Read that again carefully. The b’nei ’elohim — the original supernatural “sons of God” — were given stewardship over creation and the nations. Some of them failed catastrophically: the Watchers crossed boundaries they should not have crossed (Genesis 6; Jude 6). The territorial ’elohim allotted at Babel led the nations into idolatry (Deuteronomy 32:8–9; Psalm 82). Humanity itself, made in God’s image to exercise dominion (Genesis 1:26–28), fell through Adam’s disobedience. Creation has been groaning ever since — subjected to futility not through its own fault, but because its stewards failed at every level. B

Now Paul declares that the solution is not the repair of the old stewards but the revealing of new ones — redeemed human beings, adopted as sons through the Spirit (Romans 8:14–15), co-heirs with Christ (v. 17), about to step into glorified, incorruptible bodies (v. 23). The original b’nei ’elohim corrupted their mandate. Redeemed humanity will fulfill it. That is what creation is waiting for. B

Psalm 8:4–6 ESV
What is man that you are mindful of him, and the son of man that you care for him? 5Yet you have made him a little lower than the heavenly beings and crowned him with glory and honor. 6You have given him dominion over the works of your hands; you have put all things under his feet.

The Hebrew here reads m’‘at me-’elohim (מְעַט מֵאֱלֹהִים) — “a little lower than ’elohim.” The LXX translates this brachý ti par’ angélous (“a little lower than angels”). Humanity was designed to stand just below the divine-council members in the created order — crowned with glory, assigned dominion over creation itself.

Hebrews 2:5–9 ESV
For it was not to angels that God subjected the world to come, of which we are speaking. 6It has been testified somewhere, “What is man, that you are mindful of him, or the son of man, that you care for him? 7You made him for a little while lower than the angels; you have crowned him with glory and honor, 8putting everything in subjection under his feet.” Now in putting everything in subjection to him, he left nothing outside his control. At present, we do not yet see everything in subjection to him. 9But we see him who for a little while was made lower than the angels, namely Jesus, crowned with glory and honor because of the suffering of death, so that by the grace of God he might taste death for everyone.

The author of Hebrews quotes Psalm 8 and then makes the divine-council logic explicit: God did not subject the coming world to angels. That is a direct statement about the transfer of cosmic authority. The old order — where ’elohim administered the nations — is being superseded. The “world to come” belongs to humanity in Christ, not to angelic intermediaries. Jesus fulfilled Psalm 8 first — “crowned with glory and honor” after being made temporarily lower than the angels. Believers follow: “bringing many sons to glory” (Hebrews 2:10). A

1 Corinthians 6:2–3 ESV
Or do you not know that the saints will judge the world? And if the world is to be judged by you, are you incompetent to try trivial cases? 3Do you not know that we are to judge angels? How much more, then, matters pertaining to this life!

Paul states this as a settled, known fact: believers will judge both the world and angels. In the divine-council framework, this is not metaphor — it is the specific fulfillment of what Psalm 82 set in motion. The corrupt ’elohim who judged unjustly (Psalm 82:2) will themselves be judged by the redeemed sons of God. The replaced replace the replacers. B

The Restorative Contrast — Failed Sons, Fulfilled Sons
1. Original supernatural sons failed in stewardship. The b’nei ’elohim were created to serve in the divine council (Job 38:7), administer the nations (Deut 32:8–9), and watch over humanity. Some crossed boundaries forbidden to them (Gen 6:1–4; Jude 6). Others accepted worship meant for Yahweh alone and “judged unjustly” (Psalm 82:2–4). Creation was subjected to futility through their corruption.
2. Humanity failed in its parallel mandate. Adam was given dominion over creation (Gen 1:26–28), crowned “a little lower than the ’elohim” with glory and honor (Psalm 8:5). Through the fall, humanity forfeited its stewardship. The image of God in humanity was not erased but corrupted — we could not fulfill the mandate we were made for.
3. Christ fulfills both mandates. Jesus — fully God and fully man — was made “for a little while lower than the angels” (Heb 2:9) and then “crowned with glory and honor.” He defeated the corrupt powers at the cross (Col 2:15). He was seated above every rule and authority (Eph 1:20–21). He accomplished what both the supernatural sons and the human son (Adam) could not.
4. Redeemed humanity inherits the role. Believers are adopted as huioi theou (Romans 8:14). They receive the Spirit — the “firstfruits” of glorification (Rom 8:23). They are co-heirs with Christ (Rom 8:17). They will judge the world and angels (1 Cor 6:2–3). They will sit with Christ on his throne (Rev 3:21). The “world to come” is subjected not to angels but to glorified humanity (Heb 2:5).
5. Creation is set free through their revealing. This is the logic of Romans 8:19–21. Creation was enslaved by the failure of its stewards. It will be liberated by the manifestation of the true sons. The apokalypsis of the huioi tou theou is not merely a reward for believers — it is the mechanism of cosmic restoration. The whole groaning creation hinges on it.
This is why Romans 8:19 is not a footnote in a discussion about personal salvation. It is a hinge verse in the cosmic narrative. It connects Genesis 1 (humanity’s mandate), Genesis 6 (the Watchers’ transgression), Deuteronomy 32 (the allotment and its corruption), Psalm 8 (humanity’s intended glory), Psalm 82 (the verdict on the corrupt stewards), Hebrews 2 (Christ fulfilling Psalm 8), and Revelation 21 (the restored creation). Every thread converges here: the revealing of the sons of God is the moment when everything that went wrong — in heaven and on earth — is finally and publicly set right. B

16.3 The Resurrection Body — 1 Corinthians 15

1 Corinthians 15:20–28 ESV
But in fact Christ has been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep. 21For as by a man came death, by a man has come also the resurrection of the dead. 22For as in Adam all die, so also in Christ shall all be made alive. 23But each in his own order: Christ the firstfruits, then at his coming those who belong to Christ. 24Then comes the end, when he delivers the kingdom to God the Father after destroying every rule and every authority and every power. 25For he must reign until he has put all his enemies under his feet. 26The last enemy to be destroyed is death. 27For “God has put all things in subjection under his feet.”… 28When all things are subjected to him, then the Son himself will also be subjected to him who put all things in subjection under him, that God may be all in all.

This is the most systematic eschatological passage in Paul’s letters, and every line resonates with the divine-council framework. Christ’s resurrection is not an isolated miracle — it is firstfruits (ἀπαρχή, aparchē), the initial harvest that guarantees the full crop to follow. The agricultural metaphor is deliberate: firstfruits were presented in the temple as a pledge that the entire field would yield. Christ’s risen body is the pledge that every believer will receive one. A

Key Greek — 1 Corinthians 15:24–26
πᾶσαν ἀρχὴν καὶ πᾶσαν ἐξουσίαν καὶ δύναμιν pasan archēn kai pasan exousian kai dynamin
Accusative, feminine singular (x3)
“Every rule and every authority and every power.” The same three terms Paul uses in Ephesians 1:21, 6:12, and Colossians 1:16, 2:15 for the supernatural hierarchy — archai (principalities), exousiai (authorities), and dynameis (powers). When Christ destroys every (pasan) one, the entire administrative structure of the Deuteronomy 32 allotment is dismantled. The territorial ’elohim, the corrupt sarim of Daniel 10, the kosmokratores of Ephesians 6 — all are among the “enemies under his feet.” Christ does not merely defeat the devil. He dissolves the entire fallen cosmic bureaucracy.
ἔσχατος ἐχθρὸς… ὁ θάνατος eschatos echthros… ho thanatos
Predicate adjective + noun, nominative singular
“The last enemy… death.” Eschatos = last, final. Paul implies an order: first the spiritual powers, then earthly opposition, and last, death itself. Death is personified as an enemy (echthros) — not a natural process but a hostile power. Revelation 20:14 shows its destruction: “Death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire.” In the divine-council framework, death entered through the nachash (Gen 3) and was amplified by the Watchers (Gen 6). The last enemy to fall is the first consequence of the first rebellion.
1 Corinthians 15:42–49 ESV
So is it with the resurrection of the dead. What is sown is perishable; what is raised is imperishable. 43It is sown in dishonor; it is raised in glory. It is sown in weakness; it is raised in power. 44It is sown a natural body; it is raised a spiritual body… 45Thus it is written, “The first man Adam became a living being”; the last Adam became a life-giving spirit. 47The first man was from the earth, a man of dust; the second man is from heaven. 48As was the man of dust, so also are those who are of the dust, and as is the man of heaven, so also are those who are of heaven. 49Just as we have borne the image of the man of dust, we shall also bear the image of the man of heaven.
Key Greek — “Spiritual Body” (1 Cor 15:44)
σῶμα ψυχικόν… σῶμα πνευματικόν sôma psychikon… sôma pneumatikon
Noun + adjective, neuter nominative singular (x2)
The contrast is not between “physical” and “non-physical.” Both modify sôma (body) — always a physical entity in Paul. Psychikon (from psychē) = “animated by natural life” — the mortal body powered by biology. Pneumatikon (from pneuma) = “animated by the Spirit” — a physical body powered by God’s Spirit. The resurrection body is not less physical. It is more physical — matter sustained by Spirit, incapable of decay. This is the apokalypsis of the sons of God: glorified, incorruptible, Spirit-powered bodies.

Paul’s Adam-Christ typology drives the chapter. The first Adam bore God’s image but returned to dust. The last Adam bore God’s image perfectly and became a “life-giving spirit.” Believers will bear the image of the heavenly man (v. 49) — not disembodied, but receiving bodies patterned after Christ’s resurrection body. The Psalm 8 mandate is not merely restored. It is upgraded. Glorified humanity will bear bodies that the original b’nei ’elohim never possessed. B

Verse 28 is the theological climax: “that God may be all in all” (ἵνα ᾖ ὁ θεὸς πάντα ἐν πᾶσιν). The final state: God’s unmediated presence permeating all things. No intermediary council. No territorial delegation. No separation between heaven and earth. God is all in all. This is what Eden was supposed to become had Adam not fallen. B

16.4 The Elements Dissolved — 2 Peter 3

2 Peter 3:10–13 ESV
But the day of the Lord will come like a thief, and then the heavens will pass away with a roar, and the heavenly bodies will be burned up and dissolved, and the earth and the works that are done on it will be exposed. 11Since all these things are thus to be dissolved, what sort of people ought you to be in lives of holiness and godliness, 12waiting for and hastening the coming of the day of God, because of which the heavens will be set on fire and dissolved, and the heavenly bodies will melt as they burn! 13But according to his promise we are waiting for new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells.
Key Greek — 2 Peter 3:10–12
στοιχεῖα stoicheia
Noun, neuter, nominative plural στοιχεῖον stoicheion
“Elements, elemental things, heavenly bodies.” The same word Paul uses in Galatians 4:3, 9 and Colossians 2:8, 20 for the stoicheia tou kosmou (“elemental principles/spirits of the world”). In Hellenistic usage, stoicheia could mean physical elements (earth, water, air, fire), basic principles, or astral/spiritual powers. Peter may use the term with deliberate double meaning: the physical elements and the spiritual powers behind them are both consumed. (See Chapter 11 for extended analysis of the stoicheia.)
εὑρεθήσεται heurethēsetai
Future passive indicative, 3rd singular εὑρίσκω heuriskô
“Will be found / will be exposed.” Many manuscripts read katakaēsetai (“will be burned up”), but the best early manuscripts (P72, Codex Vaticanus) read heurethēsetai — laid bare, exposed. This is judgment language, not annihilation language. The fire is purgative and revelatory: just as gold is refined by fire (1 Pet 1:7), the earth is refined — corruption burned away, substance renewed.
Annihilation vs. Renovation — What Happens to the Earth?
“2 Peter 3 teaches that the physical earth will be completely destroyed and replaced with an entirely new creation.”
Three considerations weigh against annihilation. First, Peter’s analogy in 3:6 compares the coming fire to the Noahic flood — but the earth was not annihilated by the flood. It was purged. Peter’s fire does for the cosmos what the flood did for the ancient world. Second, the best manuscript reading (heurethēsetai, “will be exposed”) implies revelation, not annihilation. Third, Isaiah’s ḥadashîm and Paul’s insistence that creation will be “set free” (Rom 8:21) require continuity between old and new. You do not “set free” something you intend to destroy. B
“If the earth is merely renovated, why does Revelation 21:1 say ‘the first heaven and the first earth had passed away’?”
The Greek apēlthan (ἀπῆλθαν) does not require annihilation. The same word appears in 2 Corinthians 5:17: “the old has passed away; behold, the new has come” — describing transformation, not nonexistence. Furthermore, Revelation uses kainos (“new in quality”) rather than neos (“new in time/origin”), reinforcing transformation over replacement. B

Peter’s “new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells” echoes Isaiah and anticipates Revelation. The verb katoikei (“dwells permanently”) means righteousness is the permanent resident of the new creation, not a visitor. In the current age, righteousness struggles against corruption; in the new creation, corruption is the foreign element permanently removed. A

16.5 Key Terms — The Vocabulary of Restoration

Hebrew and Greek Eschatological Terminology
עוֹלָם הַבָּא ‘ôlam habba’
Noun + definite article + participle
“The world to come” / “the age to come.” A rabbinic term distinguishing the present corrupted age (‘olam hazeh) from the future redeemed age. The two-age framework permeates the NT: Jesus speaks of “this age” and “the age to come” (Matt 12:32; Mark 10:30). Paul uses ho aiôn houtos vs. ho aiôn ho mellOn. The ‘olam habba’ is not “heaven” in the popular sense. It is the renewed earth under God’s direct rule. Hebrews 2:5 makes this explicit: “It was not to angels that God subjected the oikoumenēn tēn mellousan,” the “inhabited world to come.”
תִּקּוּן tiqqûn
Noun, masculine singular תקן t-q-n
“Restoration, repair, rectification.” From the root taqan (“to straighten, to set right”). In later Jewish thought, tiqqûn ‘olam (“repair of the world”) became a major theological concept. While the full rabbinic development is post-biblical, the concept permeates the prophetic literature: Isaiah’s new creation, Ezekiel’s restored temple, Joel’s outpoured Spirit all point toward tiqqûn — God setting right what went wrong. The NT equivalent is apokatastasis (Acts 3:21).
παλιγγενεσία palingenesia
Noun, feminine, dative singular πάλιν palin (again) + γένεσις genesis (origin/birth)
“Regeneration, renewal, new genesis.” Used only twice in the NT. In Matthew 19:28, Jesus says: “In the palingenesia, when the Son of Man will sit on his glorious throne, you who have followed me will also sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel.” In Titus 3:5, Paul uses it for individual rebirth. The Matthew usage is extraordinary: palin (“again”) + genesis (“beginning/birth”) = literally a new Genesis. And in this new Genesis, the twelve apostles sit on thrones of judgment, replacing the corrupt stewards who failed. The divine council is reconstituted with redeemed humans.
ἀποκατάστασις apokatastasis
Noun, feminine, genitive singular ἀπο apo (back) + καθίστημι kathistemi (to establish)
“Restoration, restitution.” Used in Acts 3:21: “whom heaven must receive until the time of apokatastaseôs pantôn,” the “restoration of all things.” Peter is explicit: this is what the prophets always said — the fulfillment of Isaiah’s new heavens, Ezekiel’s restored Eden, Joel’s outpoured Spirit. The prefix apo- (“back”) + kata- (“into place”) + stasis (“standing”) = “a re-establishing into the original position.” Creation is being put back where it started — Eden. But expanded.

16.6 Revelation 21–22 — The Resolution

Revelation 21:1–5 ESV
Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and the sea was no more. 2And I saw the holy city, new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband. 3And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, “Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will be with them as their God. 4He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away.” 5And he who was seated on the throne said, “Behold, I am making all things new.”
Key Greek Term — Revelation 21:3
σκηνή skēnē
Noun Feminine Root: skēn-

Meaning: tent, tabernacle, dwelling place. Thematically parallel to Hebrew mishkan — the tabernacle where Yahweh’s presence dwelt among Israel. The final image in Scripture is not humanity ascending to God’s realm but God descending to humanity’s. The city comes down. The tabernacle is pitched here. This is Eden’s architecture restored on a cosmic scale.

The direction matters. Humans do not go up to heaven. God comes down to earth. The skēnē of God is with man. This is the Deuteronomy 32 allotment permanently reversed: no more mediated governance through lesser ’elohim. God himself inherits all the nations directly — the prayer of Psalm 82:8 answered in full. A

Revelation 21:16–18, 22–23 ESV
The city lies foursquare, its length the same as its width. And he measured the city with his rod, 12,000 stadia. Its length and width and height are equal. 17He also measured its wall, 144 cubits by human measurement, which is also an angel’s measurement. 18The wall was built of jasper, while the city was pure gold, like clear glass.… 22And I saw no temple in the city, for its temple is the Lord God the Almighty and the Lamb. 23And the city has no need of sun or moon to shine on it, for the glory of God gives it light, and its lamp is the Lamb.
The Dimensions of the New Jerusalem — A Perfect Cube
12,000 stadia = approximately 1,400 miles. The city is a cube: length, width, and height are equal. This is not merely architectural. In the entire Old Testament, only one structure was a perfect cube: the Holy of Holies (d’bir, דְבִיר) in Solomon’s temple — 20 cubits by 20 cubits by 20 cubits (1 Kings 6:20). The Holy of Holies was where God’s presence dwelt on earth, accessible to one person (the high priest), one day per year (Yom Kippur). The New Jerusalem is the Holy of Holies expanded to the scale of a continent — 1,400 miles in every direction — and accessible to all. A
There is no temple — because the city IS the temple. The structure is unnecessary because God’s presence is no longer contained within a building. The entire city is the Most Holy Place. Every street is the inner sanctuary. The veil torn at the cross (Matt 27:51) is now permanently gone. What the high priest experienced for minutes, the redeemed experience forever.
No sun or moon — because God’s glory replaces created light. In Genesis 1:3, God created light before the luminaries (1:14–16). The kavod (כָּבוֹד) of God was the primordial light; sun and moon were secondary. In the new creation, the original light fills the city directly. This also removes a source of idolatry: Deuteronomy 4:19 warned that the nations worshiped the luminaries God had “allotted.” In the new creation, there is nothing to worship except God himself.
144 cubits and 12,000 stadia. Both are multiples of 12: 12 tribes × 12 apostles = 144; 12 × 1,000 = 12,000. The number 12 governs the architecture — 12 gates, 12 angels, 12 foundations with 12 apostles’ names, 12 kinds of fruit. The old Israel (12 tribes) and the new Israel (12 apostles) are fused into a single structure. The wall bears both sets of names (Rev 21:12–14). Old Covenant and New Covenant people are one city.
John uses temple architecture, Holy of Holies geometry, and tribal-apostolic numerology to make a single point: the entire new creation is a temple, and everyone in it is a priest. Eden was a temple-garden with one priest (Adam). The tabernacle was a portable Eden with one priestly tribe (Levi). The New Jerusalem is the final temple — cosmic in scope, with every redeemed human serving as priest. The trajectory is expanding sacred space until there is nothing left that is not sacred. B
Revelation 21:1b ESV
“…and the sea was no more.”
Key Symbolism — “The Sea Was No More”
ἡ θάλασσα οὐκ ἔστιν ἔτι hē thalassa ouk estin eti
Nominative + negative + present indicative + adverb
“The sea is no more.” In the ANE worldview, the sea (yam, יָם) was the symbol of primordial chaos — the domain of Leviathan (Ps 74:13–14; Isa 27:1), Rahab (Isa 51:9), and the tannin (תַנִּין). At Ugarit, Baal battles Yamm; in the Hebrew Bible, Yahweh subdues the waters at creation (Gen 1:2; Ps 104:6–9) and at the Exodus (Ex 14–15). The “sea” is where the four beasts of Daniel 7 emerge. When John says the sea is “no more,” he declares that the entire domain of chaos has been permanently eliminated. The beast “rising out of the sea” (Rev 13:1) can never rise again. A
Revelation 22:1–5 ESV
Then the angel showed me the river of the water of life, bright as crystal, flowing from the throne of God and of the Lamb 2through the middle of the street of the city; also, on either side of the river, the tree of life with its twelve kinds of fruit, yielding its fruit each month. The leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations. 3No longer will there be anything accursed, but the throne of God and of the Lamb will be in it, and his servants will worship him. 4They will see his face, and his name will be on their foreheads. 5And night will be no more. They will need no light of lamp or sun, for the Lord God will be their light, and they will reign forever and ever.
Revelation 22 — Eden Imagery Restored and Amplified
The River of Life (22:1) = Eden’s River (Gen 2:10). In Eden, a river flowed out of the garden, splitting into four rivers. In the New Jerusalem, the river flows from the throne of God through the city’s main street. The source is no longer a garden spring — it is God himself. Ezekiel 47:1–12 provides the intermediate vision: water flowing from the temple threshold, growing deeper, making even the Dead Sea fresh. John completes the trajectory.
The Tree of Life (22:2) = Eden’s Tree (Gen 2:9; 3:22–24). After the fall, cherubim guarded access to the tree (Gen 3:24). That access is now restored. But where Eden had one tree, the New Jerusalem has trees lining both sides of the river, bearing twelve kinds of fruit — perpetual abundance. The leaves are “for the healing of the nations” (θεραπείαν τῶν ἐθνῶν, therapeian tôn ethnôn). The nations scattered at Babel, corrupted by the allotted ’elohim, and broken by false worship are now healed. The tree of life is not merely a personal immortality source. It is a national healing instrument.
“No longer will there be anything accursed” (22:3) = Genesis 3:17 reversed. The Greek katathema (“accursed thing”) is cognate to anathema; the Hebrew behind it is ḥerem (חֵרֶם) — the same root as Mount Hermon (חֶרְמוֹן), where the Watchers swore their oath. The curse that originated at Eden and was amplified at Hermon is entirely removed.
“They will see his face” (22:4) = The beatific vision. In the Old Testament, seeing God’s face meant death: “You cannot see my face, for man shall not see me and live” (Ex 33:20). Even Moses saw only God’s back (Ex 33:23). In the new creation, the redeemed see God’s face directly — and live. They are in resurrection bodies that can sustain the full, unmediated weight of divine glory.
“They will reign forever and ever” (22:5) = The dominion mandate completed. Genesis 1:26–28 gave humanity dominion over creation. Psalm 8 described humanity crowned with glory just below the ’elohim. Both mandates failed through the fall. Revelation 22:5 declares the mandate fulfilled: redeemed humanity reigns — not for a season, not provisionally, but eis tous aiônas tôn aiônôn (εἰς τοὺς αἰῶνας τῶν αἰώνων), “unto the ages of the ages” — the strongest possible expression of eternity in Greek.
Revelation 22 is Genesis 2–3 rewritten with every failure corrected. The river flows from God’s throne, not a garden spring. The tree is freely accessible, not guarded. The curse is abolished. God’s face is seen. Humanity reigns over all creation. Every element of Eden returns, amplified to cosmic scale. The garden was a seed. The New Jerusalem is the tree. A

16.7 What the Destination Is — And What It Is Not

Matthew 5:5 ESV
“Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth.”

Not “inherit heaven.” The earth. Paul says the same: “If the Spirit of him who raised Jesus from the dead dwells in you, he who raised Christ Jesus from the dead will also give life to your mortal bodies” (Romans 8:11). The resurrection is bodily (1 Corinthians 15:42–49). The destination is physical. The promise is not escape but restoration. A

The Eden → New Jerusalem Bookend
The Bible opens in a garden with God walking among humanity (Genesis 2–3). It closes in a garden-city with God dwelling among humanity (Revelation 21–22). Between these two bookends lies the entire cosmic war: rebellion, corruption, allotment, judgment, incarnation, cross, resurrection, and return. The destination is not somewhere else. It is here, healed, and inhabited by God himself.

What the rebellious ’elohim broke — the separation of God from creation, the corruption of nations, the reign of death — is undone. Completely. Physically. Permanently.

16.8 The Divine Council Restored — Psalm 82 Reversed

If the divine-council narrative is the backbone of this study, then the new creation is its final vertebra. The assembly that began in Job 38:7 — with the b’nei ’elohim shouting for joy at creation — ends in Revelation with a reconstituted council. The old members who failed have been judged. The new members who were redeemed have been seated.

Revelation 3:21 ESV
“The one who conquers, I will grant him to sit with me on my throne, as I also conquered and sat down with my Father on his throne.”
Revelation 20:4 ESV
Then I saw thrones, and seated on them were those to whom the authority to judge was committed. Also I saw the souls of those who had been beheaded for the testimony of Jesus and for the word of God… They came to life and reigned with Christ for a thousand years.

Thrones. Authority. Judgment. Reign. This is council language. In Psalm 82, the corrupt ’elohim judged unjustly. In Revelation, redeemed humans sit on thrones and judge — not with corruptible authority, but with authority derived from Christ’s enthronement. Paul told the Corinthians this: “Do you not know that the saints will judge the world?… We are to judge angels” (1 Cor 6:2–3). The court is reconvened. The bench is restaffed. B

The Reconstituted Divine Council — From Psalm 82 to Revelation 20
Psalm 82:1 — “God has taken his place in the divine council; in the midst of the ’elohim he holds judgment.” The original council is assembled and the corrupt members are on trial.
Psalm 82:6–7 — “You are ’elohim, sons of the Most High, all of you; nevertheless, like men you shall die.” The sentence is pronounced: the old council members lose their status and their immortality.
Psalm 82:8 — “Arise, O God, judge the earth; for you shall inherit all the nations!” The prayer: God himself takes direct sovereignty over every nation. No more intermediaries.
Matthew 19:28 — “In the palingenesia… you who have followed me will also sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel.” Jesus announces the replacement council: twelve apostles on twelve thrones. The administrative structure is being rebuilt with human members.
1 Corinthians 6:2–3 — “The saints will judge the world… We are to judge angels.” The scope expands: not just the twelve apostles, but all the redeemed will exercise judgment — over the world and over the very angels who once held authority.
Revelation 3:21 — “I will grant him to sit with me on my throne.” The conquerors share Christ’s throne — not metaphorically but positionally. They participate in his rule.
Revelation 20:4 — “Thrones, and seated on them were those to whom the authority to judge was committed.” The vision is realized. The saints are seated. The authority is transferred. The council is in session.
The arc: divine council corrupted (Ps 82) → sentenced (Ps 82:7) → authority reclaimed by God (Ps 82:8) → transferred to Christ (Eph 1:20–21) → shared with the redeemed (Rev 3:21; 20:4). Redeemed humanity does not merely go to heaven. It takes its seat in the divine assembly. B
Do Believers Literally “Judge Angels”?
“1 Corinthians 6:3 is rhetorical hyperbole — Paul is using exaggeration to shame the Corinthians for taking each other to pagan courts. He doesn’t literally mean believers will judge angels.”
Paul’s argument depends on the premise being true. His logic: “If X is true (you will judge angels), then surely you can handle Y (trivial disputes).” If X is hyperbole, the argument collapses. Furthermore, ouk oidate (“do you not know?”) implies settled, received teaching — not rhetorical flourish. The divine-council framework supplies the background: if the ’elohim who administered the nations were sentenced (Ps 82), and if redeemed humanity inherits their role (Heb 2:5), then judging those failed administrators is a logical component of that inheritance. B

16.9 The Cosmic Temple — From Eden to New Jerusalem

One of the most illuminating lenses for understanding the new creation is the temple trajectory that runs from Genesis to Revelation. Modern readers tend to separate “garden” and “temple” into distinct categories. Ancient Israelites did not. Eden was both.

Eden as Temple — The Evidence
God “walks” in Eden. Genesis 3:8 uses the Hithpael of halak (מִתְהַלֵּךְ, mithallek). The identical form appears in Leviticus 26:12 for God’s presence in the tabernacle and 2 Samuel 7:6–7 for God moving with the tabernacle. The way God inhabited Eden is the way God inhabited the tabernacle. A
Adam’s task is priestly. Genesis 2:15: לְעָבְדָהּ וּלְשָׁמְרָהּ (l’‘ovdah u-l’shomrah), “to work and keep.” This ‘avad + shamar combination appears elsewhere in the Pentateuch only for priestly duties (Num 3:7–8; 18:7). Adam is the first priest in the first temple. A
Cherubim guard the entrance. After the expulsion, cherubim are stationed east of Eden (Gen 3:24). In the tabernacle, cherubim are woven into the curtain separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place (Ex 26:31) and stand above the ark (Ex 25:18–22). Eden had cherubim at its gate. The temple had cherubim at its veil. The function is identical.
Eden is on a mountain. Ezekiel 28:13–14 places Eden on “the holy mountain of God.” ANE temples were universally placed on mountains or artificial mountains (ziggurats). The Jerusalem temple was on Mount Zion. The New Jerusalem descends as a mountain-city (Rev 21:10). The cosmic temple is always elevated — the meeting point between heaven and earth.
The menorah = the tree of life. The golden lampstand in the tabernacle was designed as a stylized tree with branches, buds, flowers, and almond blossoms (Ex 25:31–36). It stood in the Holy Place as a reminder of the tree Adam lost access to. When the tree of life reappears in Revelation 22:2, the lampstand’s botanical design is explained: it was always pointing back to Eden and forward to the new creation.
The trajectory: Eden (garden-temple, one priest) → Tabernacle (portable Eden, one tribe) → Solomon’s Temple (permanent Eden, one priesthood) → Jesus’ Body (John 2:19–21) → The Church (1 Cor 3:16) → New Jerusalem (cosmic Eden-temple, all the redeemed are priests). Sacred space never stops expanding: garden → tent → building → person → people → the entire renewed cosmos. B

16.10 Contrast with ANE Eschatology — Hope vs. Despair

To appreciate how radical the biblical vision of new creation is, it must be set against the eschatological landscape of the ancient Near East. Israel’s neighbors had almost nothing comparable.

Mesopotamian Pessimism vs. Biblical Hope
The Epic of Gilgamesh — “When the gods created mankind, they allotted death to mankind.” Gilgamesh searches for immortality after Enkidu’s death. He finds a plant of rejuvenation — and a serpent steals it while he sleeps. Immortality is for the gods alone. There is no new creation, no resurrection. The best Gilgamesh can hope for is that his city walls endure. This is the default ANE position: death is final, inevitable, and without remedy. A
The Descent of Ishtar — the underworld as permanent destination. The dead descend to the “Land of No Return” (Kurnugia), a dark, dusty netherworld ruled by Ereshkigal. Even the goddess Ishtar is stripped of her power at each of the seven gates. The dead eat dust and clay. The closest parallel to resurrection is the temporary release of Dumuzi/Tammuz — but even he must return for half the year. There is no permanent escape from death in Mesopotamian thought.
Egyptian afterlife — preservation, not transformation. Egypt came closest to afterlife hope, but Egyptian immortality was about preserving the existing body through mummification so the ka (life force) and ba (personality) could continue in the Duat (underworld). The goal was stasis. The biblical vision is not preservation but transformation: mortal bodies raised imperishable, a decaying creation made new. Egypt wanted to stop the clock. The Bible wants to restart it.
Canaanite religion — the Rephaim and the cult of the dead. Ugaritic texts describe the Rephaim (rp’um) as shades of dead kings invoked through necromantic rituals (cf. the witch of Endor, 1 Sam 28). This is not resurrection hope but veneration of the powerful dead. Israel’s prophets reject this: “The dead know nothing” (Eccl 9:5). But where Ecclesiastes ends in pessimism, Isaiah 26:19 breaks through: “Your dead shall live; their bodies shall rise.” This has no ANE parallel.
The biblical new creation is not an evolution from ANE thought. It is a radical break. Every surrounding culture agreed: death wins. Israel alone proclaimed a God who would raise the dead, renew the earth, destroy death itself, and dwell with his people in a restored, physical, permanent creation. This hope came not from cultural context but despite it. A

16.11 The Watcher Problem Resolved — 1 Enoch’s Eschatological Sections

The canonical texts describe the fate of the fallen divine beings in broad strokes: judgment, destruction, the lake of fire. The Second Temple tradition in 1 Enoch, particularly the Epistle of Enoch (chapters 91–105), provides a more detailed eschatological framework that illuminates how the Watcher crisis is ultimately resolved.

1 Enoch 91:15–16 Nickelsburg translation
“And after this, in the tenth week, in the seventh part, there will be the great eternal judgment in which he will execute vengeance among the watchers. And the first heaven will depart and pass away, and a new heaven will appear, and all the powers of the heavens will shine sevenfold forever.”

The “Apocalypse of Weeks” (1 Enoch 93:1–10; 91:11–17) divides history into ten “weeks” culminating in the final judgment of the Watchers and the appearance of new heavens. This parallels Isaiah 65:17 and Revelation 21:1 — but adds the specific detail that the judgment of the Watchers is the trigger for the cosmic renewal. The old heavens cannot be renewed while the corrupt powers still inhabit them. C

1 Enoch 10:4–6 Nickelsburg translation
“And further the Lord said to Raphael: ‘Bind Azazel hand and foot, and throw him into the darkness. And split open the desert which is in Dudael, and throw him there. And throw on him jagged and sharp stones, and cover him with darkness; and let him stay there forever, and cover his face, that he may not see light. And on the great day of judgment he will be led away to the burning conflagration.’”
1 Enoch 10:11–13 Nickelsburg translation
“And the Lord said to Michael: ‘Go, inform Shemihazah and the others with him who united with the women, to corrupt themselves with them in all their uncleanness… Bind them for seventy generations in the valleys of the earth until the great day of their judgment and their consummation… They will be led away to the fiery abyss, and to the torment, and to the prison where they will be confined forever.’”

1 Enoch describes a two-stage punishment: (1) immediate binding in subterranean darkness, and (2) final consignment to the “fiery abyss” at the great judgment. This maps onto the canonical model: 2 Peter 2:4 (current imprisonment in Tartarus), Revelation 20:10, 14–15 (final lake of fire), and Jude 6 (“kept in eternal chains… until the judgment of the great day”). B

1 Enoch 10:18–22 Nickelsburg translation
“And then the whole earth will be worked in righteousness, and all of it will be planted with trees, and will be full of blessing… 21And all the children of men will become righteous, and all the peoples will worship and bless me, and all will worship me. 22And the earth will be cleansed from all corruption, and from all sin, and from all plague, and from all torment; and I will not again send a flood upon it for generation unto generation, forever.”

After the Watchers’ judgment, 1 Enoch envisions what Isaiah and Revelation describe: a purified earth, universal righteousness, all peoples worshiping God. The Enochic tradition adds the explicit causal link: the earth can be cleansed only after its corrupters are removed. Their final judgment is the trigger. The groaning creation of Romans 8 groans because its corrupters still exist. When they are destroyed, creation’s liberation is complete. C

The Watcher Crisis — From Transgression to Resolution
The transgression (Gen 6:1–4; 1 Enoch 6–8): 200 Watchers descend to Mount Hermon, take human wives, produce the Nephilim, and teach forbidden knowledge. The result: universal corruption, the violence that triggers the flood.
The immediate judgment (Gen 6:5–7; 1 Enoch 10; 2 Pet 2:4; Jude 6): The Watchers are bound in subterranean darkness. Their offspring (the Nephilim) are destroyed. The earth is flooded. But the imprisonment is temporary — they are held “until the judgment of the great day.”
The ongoing effects (Deut 32:8–9; Ps 82; Dan 10): Even after the Watchers are bound, the territorial rebellion continues. The ’elohim allotted to the nations lead them into idolatry. The cosmic war persists through the Old Testament era.
The decisive victory (Col 2:15; Eph 1:20–21): Christ’s cross disarms the principalities and powers. His resurrection and enthronement place him above every name, every authority, every stoicheion. The corrupt powers are defeated in principle, though not yet fully removed.
The final judgment (Rev 20:10–15; 1 Enoch 10:6, 13): The devil, the beast, and the false prophet are thrown into the lake of fire. Death and Hades follow. Every name not in the book of life is judged. The Watchers’ “great day of judgment” arrives. The chains that held them for millennia give way not to freedom but to final destruction.
The new creation (Rev 21–22; 1 Enoch 10:18–22; Isa 65–66): With every corrupt power removed, the earth is cleansed and God dwells directly with humanity. The Watcher problem is reversed: where the Watchers corrupted humanity with forbidden knowledge, the redeemed know God face-to-face (Rev 22:4). Where the Watchers abandoned their dwelling (Jude 6), God takes up permanent dwelling with humanity (Rev 21:3). Where the Watchers brought the ḥerem-curse to Hermon, the new creation contains “nothing accursed” (Rev 22:3).
The Watcher crisis is not a side story. It is the first act of the cosmic war, and its resolution is the last. From Hermon to the lake of fire, from the oath of 200 rebels to the enthronement of millions of the redeemed, the arc is complete. B

16.12 Summary — How the Destination Resolves Everything

How the Destination Resolves the Divine-Council Conflict
Deuteronomy 32:8–9 — Nations were allotted to lesser ’elohim as stewards. Resolved: God dwells with all nations directly (Rev 21:3, 24–26).
Psalm 82:6–7 — Corrupt ’elohim sentenced to die like men. Resolved: They are cast into the lake of fire (Rev 20:10). Their authority is dissolved.
Genesis 3 — Humanity expelled from God’s presence; access to the Tree of Life severed. Resolved: “The tree of life with its twelve kinds of fruit” — access restored (Rev 22:2).
Genesis 6 / 2 Peter 2:4 — Watchers imprisoned; their offspring destroyed. Resolved: “Death shall be no more” (Rev 21:4). Every prison emptied for final judgment (Rev 20:13).
Romans 8:19–23 — Creation groans under corruption. Resolved: “I am making all things new” (Rev 21:5). Creation itself is liberated.
The entire divine-council narrative — from the creation assembly of Job 38 to the final judgment of Revelation 20 — resolves in a single image: God living with humanity on a renewed earth, with no mediating council, no territorial assignment, no adversary, no death. Eden was the prototype. New Jerusalem is the finished product.
