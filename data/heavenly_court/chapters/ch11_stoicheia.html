Chapter 11

The Stoicheia — Cosmic Powers Behind the Elements

Paul uses a single Greek term — stoicheia tou kosmou — to name the spiritual powers that held humanity in bondage before Christ. This chapter traces the word from Greek philosophy through Jewish apocalyptic to Paul’s radical claim: the cross dismantled the elemental rulers of the cosmos.

Three Pauline passages use the term στοιχεῖα τοῦ κόσμου (stoicheia tou kosmou, “elemental things of the world”): Galatians 4:3 and 4:9, and Colossians 2:8 and 2:20. A fourth occurrence in Hebrews 5:12 uses στοιχεῖα alone. Together these texts form a compact but explosive theology — the claim that invisible spiritual powers governed the cosmos and enslaved humanity until Christ’s death broke their authority. Understanding these passages requires tracing the word stoicheion through its full semantic history, from Greek philosophy through Second Temple Judaism to the apostolic proclamation. A

The stoicheia concept is not a Pauline invention. It is the apostle’s Greek-language entry point into a worldview already established by the Hebrew scriptures: the divine-council framework of Deuteronomy 32:8–9, where the Most High allotted the nations to subordinate divine beings who subsequently corrupted their mandate. Paul takes this inherited cosmology and gives it a name his Greco-Roman audience can understand — stoicheia tou kosmou — the cosmic elements, the ruling powers, the spiritual forces behind the physical order. B

11.1 The Greek Term — Στοιχεῖον (Stoicheion)

Etymology and Semantic Range
στοιχεῖον stoicheion
Neuter noun, 2nd declension στοῖχος stoichos
From stoichos (στοῖχος), meaning “a row, rank, series” — a line of soldiers, a column of text, a sequence of items arranged in order. The noun stoicheion originally meant one item in a series: a letter of the alphabet (the most basic unit of written language), a single element in a sequence. The related verb stoicheô (στοιχέω) means “to walk in a row, to be in line, to follow in order” (cf. Gal 5:25, “let us also walk [stoichômen] by the Spirit”; Rom 4:12; Phil 3:16). The semantic core is always orderly arrangement — something fundamental, constitutive, and sequential.

From this root meaning of “basic unit in a series,” the word branched into four distinct senses in the ancient world. Each sense is well-attested, and each has been proposed as the primary meaning in Paul’s usage. The scholarly debate turns on which sense — or which combination — Paul intends.

Four Attested Meanings of Stoicheion in Antiquity
1. Letters of the Alphabet / Rudimentary Principles — The “ABCs.” Herodotus, Plato (Theaetetus 201e–202b), and Isocrates all use stoicheia for the letters of the Greek alphabet. By metaphorical extension, stoicheia came to mean “first principles,” “elementary teachings,” “basics” — the ABC-level foundations of any subject. Xenocrates (4th c. BC) and the Stoics used stoicheia for the fundamental principles of a philosophical system.
2. Physical Elements (Earth, Water, Air, Fire) — Empedocles (5th c. BC) identified four “roots” (rhizômata) of all matter. Plato (Timaeus 48b) adopted the term stoicheia for these: γῆ (earth), ὕδωρ (water), ἀήρ (air), πῦρ (fire). Aristotle systematized this usage, and it became standard in Greek philosophy. The stoicheia were the irreducible building blocks of the physical cosmos.
3. Celestial Bodies / Astral Powers — In Hellenistic astrology, the sun, moon, planets, and zodiacal signs were called stoicheia because they governed (stoicheô) the rhythms of the cosmos — seasons, tides, human destiny. Diogenes Laertius (Lives 6.102) records this usage. The Testament of Solomon (1st–3rd c. AD) associates specific demons with the thirty-six decans of the zodiac, each called a stoicheion.
4. Spiritual Beings Governing the Elements — In Jewish apocalyptic literature, angels or spirits were assigned governance over the physical elements (1 Enoch 60:11–22; 75:1; Jubilees 2:2). This is the bridge to Paul’s usage: if divine beings stand behind the physical elements, then the word stoicheia can denote both the elements and their spiritual rulers simultaneously — the visible phenomenon and the invisible power behind it.
The word stoicheion is polysemous by design. By the first century AD, a Greek speaker hearing stoicheia tou kosmou would simultaneously think: “basic principles,” “physical elements,” “astral forces,” and “governing spirits.” Paul almost certainly exploits this range rather than restricting himself to a single sense. The question is which sense is primary in his argument. B
Morphological Analysis — Stoicheia tou Kosmou
στοιχεῖα stoicheia
Nominative/Accusative neuter plural στοιχεῖον stoicheion
Plural form. In every Pauline occurrence, the word appears in the plural, suggesting Paul is thinking of a category of powers — multiple entities, not a single force.
τοῦ κόσμου tou kosmou
Genitive masculine singular κόσμος kosmos
“Of the world/cosmos.” The genitive is crucial. Kosmos in Paul carries a double sense: (a) the physical created order, (b) the organized system of human life under fallen powers (cf. “the god of this age/world,” 2 Cor 4:4; “the rulers of this age,” 1 Cor 2:6; “the world rulers of this present darkness,” Eph 6:12). When Paul says stoicheia tou kosmou, the genitive tou kosmou can be: (1) attributive — “worldly elements,” i.e., elementary things belonging to the mundane order, or (2) possessive/subjective — “elements that rule the world,” i.e., powers that govern the cosmos. The latter reading gains strength from Paul’s consistent use of kosmos as a domain under hostile spiritual control.

Key Parsing — Galatians 4:3

Perfect passive Participle, nominative masculine plural δουλόω douloô
“Having been enslaved.” From douloô (“to make a slave, reduce to bondage”). The perfect tense indicates a past action with continuing results — “we had been in a state of slavery.” The passive voice means the enslaving was done to humanity, not chosen by it. Combined with the periphrastic construction (ēmetha + participle), this describes a prolonged, ongoing state of bondage. The language is harsh: Paul is not saying humanity was under “basic instruction” but under genuine slavery to powerful forces.
ὑπὸ τὰ στοιχεῖα τοῦ κόσμου hypo ta stoicheia tou kosmou
Preposition + accusative neuter plural + genitive
The preposition hypo + accusative = “under the power/authority of.” This is the same construction Paul uses for being “under the law” (hypo nomon, Gal 4:5), “under sin” (hypo hamartian, Gal 3:22), and “under a guardian” (hypo epitropous, Gal 4:2). In every case, hypo + accusative describes subjugation to a governing authority. Paul places the stoicheia in the same grammatical slot as the law, sin, and a guardian — they are governing powers that held humanity in subjection.

The critical exegetical question is what Paul means by stoicheia tou kosmou in this context. Three major scholarly positions have emerged, and each deserves careful evaluation.

View 1: “Basic Principles” / “Elementary Teachings”
The claim: Stoicheia means “rudimentary principles” — the ABCs of religious knowledge. Before Christ, both Jews (under Torah) and Gentiles (under pagan religion) were at an elementary, immature stage. The “slavery” was subjection to a rudimentary system, not to personal beings. B
Supporters: Burton (ICC Galatians, 1920), Betz (Hermeneia Galatians, 1979), Moo (NICNT Galatians, 2013). This is the most traditional Protestant reading.
Strengths: (1) Hebrews 5:12 clearly uses stoicheia for “basic principles,” proving the sense is available. (2) The child-to-heir metaphor in Gal 4:1–2 fits an “immaturity” reading. (3) Paul parallels the stoicheia with being “under the law” (4:5), which is a system of instruction, not a personal being.
Weaknesses: (1) One does not become “enslaved” (dedoulômenoi) to an ABC lesson. Paul’s slavery language is too intense for mere immaturity. (2) In 4:8, Paul equates the stoicheia with “those that by nature are not gods” (τοῖς φύσει μὴ οὖσιν θεοῖς) — which is explicitly personal. You cannot “be a god by nature” if you are an abstract principle. (3) In 4:9, Paul calls them “weak and worthless” (ἀσθενῆ καὶ πτωχά) — adjectives that describe agents, not principles. (4) The Galatians were formerly pagan, not Jewish — they were never under Torah’s “elementary teachings.” Paul must be describing a universal bondage that applied to both Jew and Gentile.
View 2: Cosmic Elemental Spirits / Personal Spiritual Powers
The claim: Stoicheia tou kosmou are personal spiritual beings — angelic or demonic powers that governed the cosmos, mediated the law, and stood behind pagan worship. Before Christ, humanity was under their authority. A
Supporters: Reicke (1951), Carr (1981), Clinton Arnold (Powers of Darkness, 1992), Michael Heiser (The Unseen Realm, 2015), Wright (Paul and the Faithfulness of God, 2013, with nuance).
Strengths: (1) Paul equates the stoicheia with “those that by nature are not gods” (4:8) — entities that are worshipped as gods but are not truly divine. This maps directly to the Deuteronomy 32:8 b’nei ’elohim who received the nations and accepted worship. (2) The slavery vocabulary (douloô, hypo) is the same Paul uses for bondage under spiritual powers elsewhere (Rom 6:6, 8:15; Gal 5:1). (3) Galatians 3:19 says the law was “ordained through angels” (διαταγεὶς δι’ ἀγγέλων) and delivered via a “mediator” — Paul has already introduced angelic intermediaries into the law-giving context. (4) Paul’s vocabulary matches the “rulers and authorities” (archai kai exousiai) of Colossians 2:15 and Ephesians 6:12 — his broader cosmic powers framework. (5) The “days and months and seasons and years” of 4:10 correspond to calendrical observances governed by celestial bodies that, in the ancient worldview, were controlled by angelic beings (1 Enoch 75:1; 82:10–20).
Weaknesses: (1) Stoicheion is not attested with the clear meaning “personal spirit” in any pre-Pauline Greek text — the personalization may be Paul’s own innovation. (2) The connection to the child-heir metaphor (4:1–2) seems to require an “immaturity” component, not just “hostile powers.”
View 3: The Torah Itself as an Enslaving Stoicheion
The claim: Paul uses stoicheia specifically for the Torah and pagan religious systems as parallel enslavements. Both Jews and Gentiles were under regulatory systems — one revealed, one idolatrous — that functioned as imprisoning powers in the pre-Christ era. B
Supporters: Martyn (AB Galatians, 1997), de Boer (NTL Galatians, 2011). This is an influential position in recent Galatians scholarship.
Strengths: (1) Paul explicitly parallels stoicheia-slavery (4:3) with being “under the law” (4:5). (2) In Gal 4:9–10, the calendar observances (“days, months, seasons, years”) echo the Torah’s festival calendar (Sabbaths, new moons, sabbatical years, jubilees). (3) Paul calls the law a paidagôgos (“guardian/disciplinarian,” Gal 3:24) and a “prison” (3:23) — the same custodial imagery applied to the stoicheia.
Weaknesses: (1) This view struggles to explain why Gentile Galatians were ever under “Torah-slavery” — the Galatians had never been Jews. (2) Paul’s identification of the stoicheia with “those that by nature are not gods” (4:8) cannot refer to the Torah, which Paul elsewhere calls “holy, righteous, and good” (Rom 7:12). (3) The view reduces Paul’s cosmic-powers vocabulary to a metaphor for human religious systems, which contradicts his explicit language about personal spiritual forces in Colossians 2:15 and Ephesians 6:12.
Resolution: The Stoicheia Are Personal Powers Operating Through Systems
The three views are often presented as mutually exclusive, but Paul’s argument actually requires a synthesis. The text itself moves fluidly between descriptions of systems (law, calendars) and descriptions of personal beings (“not gods by nature”). The most coherent reading recognizes that Paul sees personal spiritual powers operating through and behind religious regulatory systems.
The synthesis: The stoicheia tou kosmou are the spiritual beings assigned governance over the nations (Deut 32:8) and the elements of the cosmos. They exercised their authority through religious systems — both the Torah (mediated by angels, Gal 3:19; Acts 7:53; Heb 2:2) and pagan cult (mediated by demons posing as gods, 1 Cor 10:20). Before Christ, both systems functioned as custodial authorities. The Torah was a good law administered by intermediaries who were not ultimate; pagan religion was a corrupt system administered by beings who usurped divine prerogatives. Both held humanity “enslaved.” Christ’s coming terminated the custodial era — humanity is now under direct divine sonship (Gal 4:4–7), no longer mediated through any created power. A
Galatians 4:8–9 — The Decisive Identification
Verse 4:8 is the key to the entire debate. Paul writes: “Formerly, when you did not know God, you were enslaved to those that by nature are not gods” (τοῖς φύσει μὴ οὖσιν θεοῖς). In the next verse he calls these same entities the “weak and worthless stoicheia.” The equation is explicit: stoicheia = beings worshipped as gods but lacking true divinity. A
The phrase φύσει μὴ οὖσιν θεοῖς (“by nature not being gods”) uses the dative of physis (“by nature, in essential being”). These beings function as gods — they receive worship, they govern territories, they issue regulations — but they are not divine in essence. This is precisely the Deuteronomy 32/Psalm 82 framework: the b’nei ’elohim were given authority but are not Yahweh. They are “gods” by role, not by nature. B
The adjectives ἀσθενῆ (asthenē, “weak”) and πτωχά (ptôcha, “worthless/beggarly”) are devastating. These were once powerful entities that held humanity in bondage. Now, after Christ’s victory, Paul mocks them as weak and beggarly — their authority has been stripped (cf. Col 2:15). To return to their worship is not merely foolish; it is returning to defeated powers.
The verb ἐπιστρέφετε (epistrephete, “you turn back,” 4:9) is Paul’s shocked question: how can the Galatians, who have been liberated from cosmic enslavement, turn back to the same category of enslaving power? Whether they turn back to pagan cult or submit to Torah observances imposed by the Judaizers, they are re-entering the custodial order that Christ abolished. Paul treats both moves as functionally equivalent returns to stoicheia-slavery. B
Galatians 4:8–9 makes the “basic principles” reading nearly impossible as a standalone interpretation. Paul does not mock the ABCs as “weak and worthless.” He mocks defeated supernatural pretenders. The stoicheia are personal beings that were worshipped as gods, held humanity in bondage, and have now been rendered powerless by Christ. A
“Days, Months, Seasons, Years” — Galatians 4:10
Paul’s rebuke in 4:10 — “you observe days and months and seasons and years” — connects stoicheia-bondage to calendrical regulation. This is significant because the ancient world understood celestial bodies as the regulators of time, and celestial bodies were governed by spiritual beings.
In the Jewish framework: Angels governed the sun and moon (1 Enoch 72:1; 75:1; 82:10–20), presided over the seasons (Jubilees 2:2), and regulated the calendar on which Torah observances depended. The “days” (Sabbaths), “months” (new moons), “seasons” (festivals), and “years” (sabbatical/jubilee) were all tied to the cosmic clockwork administered by angelic powers.
In the pagan framework: Hellenistic religion was deeply astrological. The planets were gods (Ares/Mars, Aphrodite/Venus, Hermes/Mercury). The days of the week were named after them. Festival calendars tracked astral cycles. Observing these “days and months and seasons and years” meant submitting to the rhythmic governance of the stoicheia — whether one did so through Jewish or pagan practice.
Paul’s point is not that calendars are inherently evil. His point is that calendrical bondage to created powers is bondage nonetheless — and Christ freed humanity from all such custodial authority. The Galatian Judaizers who demanded Torah calendar observance were, in Paul’s eyes, putting people back under the same category of created-power governance that pagan astral worship represented. B

The Colossian “Philosophy” and Its Cosmic Context
The identity of the “Colossian heresy” has been debated for centuries. Paul describes it as “philosophy and empty deceit, according to human tradition, according to the stoicheia tou kosmou” (2:8). It involved: (1) dietary regulations and calendar observances (“food and drink,” “festivals, new moons, Sabbaths,” 2:16); (2) angel worship or angel-mediated access to divine visions (“worship of angels,” “going on in detail about visions,” 2:18); (3) severe ascetic practices (“do not handle, taste, touch,” 2:21–23).
Clinton Arnold (The Colossian Syncretism, 1996) demonstrated from archaeological and literary evidence that the Lycus Valley (where Colossae was located) had a thriving culture of angel invocation, magical practices, and syncretistic Judaism that blended Torah observance with pagan spirit-worship. The “philosophy” Paul opposes was not abstract intellectualism but a practical spiritual technology — a system for managing one’s relationship with the cosmic powers through dietary asceticism, calendar compliance, and angelic mediation. B
Paul’s response is not “these powers don’t exist.” His response is “these powers have been defeated.” Christ has “disarmed the rulers and authorities” (2:15) and believers have “died to the stoicheia” (2:20). The Colossian error was not believing in cosmic powers — it was treating defeated powers as if they still required appeasement.
Colossians 2 confirms the personal-spiritual reading of stoicheia. The stoicheia are equated with archai kai exousiai (“rulers and authorities”), they are “disarmed” and “triumphed over” at the cross, and believers die “from” them. These are not abstract principles. They are defeated powers. A

Parsing — Hebrews 5:12
τὰ στοιχεῖα τῆς ἀρχῆς ta stoicheia tēs archēs
Accusative neuter plural + genitive feminine singular ἀρχή archē
“The elementary things of the beginning” — literally, “the first-elements of the starting-point.” The genitive tēs archēs (“of the beginning”) is the crucial difference from Paul’s usage. Paul writes stoicheia tou kosmou (“of the world/cosmos”); the Hebrews author writes stoicheia tēs archēs (“of the beginning”). The genitive phrase defines the sphere: these are not the governing powers of the cosmos, but the foundational teachings of the faith — the ABCs of God’s revelation.
τῶν λογίων τοῦ θεοῦ tôn logiôn tou theou
Genitive neuter plural + genitive masculine singular λόγιον logion
“Of the oracles of God.” Logion (diminutive of logos) refers to divine utterances — Scripture, prophetic words, revealed truth. This further specifies the domain: the stoicheia here are the foundational elements of God’s spoken revelation, not cosmic forces. The rebuke is that these readers should be advanced teachers but still need remedial instruction in the basics of biblical theology.
Is Hebrews 5:12 the Same as Paul’s Stoicheia?
The case for a different usage: The Hebrews author clearly means “basic principles” — the ABCs. The context is pedagogical (teaching, learning, milk vs. solid food), not cosmological. No personal spiritual powers are in view. This proves that stoicheia could mean “basic teachings” in first-century Christian vocabulary.
The response: No one denies that stoicheia can mean “basic principles.” The question is whether it means only that in Galatians and Colossians, where the context includes slavery, false gods, disarming rulers, and triumphing over authorities. The Hebrews usage actually strengthens the case for a different meaning in Paul: if the Hebrews author wanted to say “basic principles,” he qualified it with tēs archēs (“of the beginning”) and tôn logiôn tou theou (“of God’s oracles”). Paul does not use these qualifiers. Paul writes stoicheia tou kosmou — of the cosmos — and then identifies them with entities worshipped as gods and with rulers who are disarmed at the cross. The two usages are related but distinct, sharing a lexeme but not a referent. B

Nevertheless, a subtle connection should not be missed. Hebrews 5:12–6:1 lists the “elementary” teachings as including “repentance from dead works,” “faith toward God,” “instruction about washings,” “laying on of hands,” “resurrection of the dead,” and “eternal judgment” (Heb 6:1–2). These are not trivial topics — they include eschatological judgment and bodily resurrection. Even the “basics” of the Christian faith operate within a cosmic framework. The author is not saying these truths are unimportant; he is saying his audience should have moved beyond them to maturity — including, perhaps, the kind of cosmic-spiritual understanding that the rest of Hebrews develops (angel Christology in ch. 1, cosmic inheritance in ch. 2, Melchizedek priesthood in ch. 7). The stoicheia of Hebrews may function as the entry-level version of the same cosmic story that Paul explores at the advanced level.

11.5 The Second Temple Jewish Background

Paul did not invent the idea that spiritual beings governed the physical elements and the nations. This concept was deeply embedded in Second Temple Judaism — the intellectual world Paul inhabited. The key texts are:

1 Enoch 60:11–22 Nickelsburg/VanderKam translation
The spirit of the sea is male… the spirit of the frost is his own angel, and the spirit of the hail is a good angel. The spirit of the snow has withdrawn because of its power, and it has a special spirit, and that which rises from it is like smoke… The spirit of the dew has its dwelling at the ends of the heaven… 22And all these [spirits] give thanks before the Lord of the Spirits and glorify him with all their power.
Angels Over Elements in 1 Enoch
1 Enoch 60:11–22 systematically assigns individual angelic spirits to each natural phenomenon: frost, hail, snow, dew, rain, darkness, light. These are not poetic personifications — the text presents a cosmological hierarchy in which every physical element has a governing angel who reports to the “Lord of the Spirits.” B
In 1 Enoch 75:1, the angel Uriel shows Enoch “the leaders of the heads of the thousands who are in charge of all the stars of heaven and all the days.” Stars and calendar-units are governed by angelic “leaders” — the same concept Paul confronts when he warns against calendrical bondage (Gal 4:10).
1 Enoch 82:10–20 describes four “leaders” who “separate the four parts of the year” (the seasons), each governing a group of subordinate angels who regulate the months and days. The entire natural order runs on an angelic administrative structure. These are the Jewish stoicheia — spirit-governors of the cosmic elements. B
Jubilees 2:2 Wintermute translation
For on the first day he created the heavens which are above, and the earth, and the waters and all the spirits which minister before him: the angels of the presence, and the angels of sanctification, and the angels of the spirit of fire, and the angels of the spirit of the winds, and the angels of the spirit of the clouds and darkness and snow and hail and frost, and the angels of the sounds, and of the thunders and the lightnings, and the angels of the spirits of cold and heat, of winter and spring and harvest and summer, and of all of the spirits of his creatures which are in heaven and on earth.
Jubilees’ Comprehensive Angel-Element Inventory
Jubilees 2:2 provides the most systematic Second Temple catalog of element-governing angels. The text assigns angelic spirits to: fire, winds, clouds, darkness, snow, hail, frost, sounds, thunder, lightning, cold, heat, and every season. These angels were created on Day One alongside the heavens and earth — they are part of the fundamental cosmic infrastructure.
The phrase “all of the spirits of his creatures which are in heaven and on earth” establishes total coverage — every element and phenomenon has its governing spirit. This is the cosmology that Paul’s audience inherited. When Paul says stoicheia tou kosmou, his Jewish listeners would immediately think of this angelic administration of the physical world. B
The Second Temple Jewish worldview did not separate “physical elements” from “spiritual governors.” To speak of the stoicheia of the cosmos was always simultaneously to speak of the angel-spirits who governed those elements. Paul’s usage reflects this indivisible concept — which is why the scholarly debate between “elements” and “spirits” may be a false dichotomy. In the ancient Jewish mind, they were the same thing.
The Testament of Solomon — Demons and the Elements
The Testament of Solomon (1st–3rd c. AD) is a Jewish-Christian pseudepigraphal text in which Solomon interrogates demons who are compelled to reveal their names, powers, and the elements they govern. Each of the 36 decanoi (zodiacal decans) is associated with a specific demon who governs a particular disease, natural force, or bodily function. C
In T. Sol. 8:1–4, seven demons present themselves as “the stoicheia, rulers of the world of darkness” (στοιχεῖα, κοσμοκράτορες τοῦ σκότους). This is explosive: the text explicitly calls demonic spirits stoicheia and kosmokratores (“world-rulers”) — the same word Paul uses in Ephesians 6:12 (τοὺς κοσμοκράτορας τοῦ σκότους τούτου, “the world-rulers of this darkness”).
Caution: The Testament of Solomon may be later than Paul (dating is disputed). It may reflect post-Pauline usage rather than background for Paul. However, it demonstrates that the equation stoicheia = demonic spirits governing the cosmos was operative in Jewish-Christian circles. Whether Paul coined the association or inherited it, the conceptual world is continuous. C

11.6 The Deuteronomy 32 Connection

The Second Temple evidence shows that Jewish thinkers assigned angels to every physical element. But Paul’s stoicheia are not merely neutral administrators — they are enslaving powers that held humanity in bondage. The bridge between “angel-governed elements” and “enslaving cosmic tyrants” is the Deuteronomy 32:8–9 tradition of the divine allotment.

Deuteronomy 32:8–9 ESV (DSS/LXX reading)
When the Most High gave to the nations their inheritance, when he divided mankind, he fixed the borders of the peoples according to the number of the sons of God. 9But the LORD’s portion is his people, Jacob his allotted heritage.
From Allotment to Enslavement — The Corruption of the Stoicheia
Step 1 — Legitimate delegation: At Babel, the Most High assigned divine beings (b’nei ’elohim) to govern the nations. These beings also administered the cosmic elements (1 Enoch 60, 75, 82; Jubilees 2:2). They were legitimate intermediaries in God’s cosmic order.
Step 2 — Corruption: Psalm 82 records the verdict: these divine administrators “judge unjustly” and “show partiality to the wicked” (Ps 82:2). They accepted worship for themselves, led the nations into idolatry, and became the “gods” of the pagan pantheons. The legitimate administration became a tyranny.
Step 3 — Paul’s identification: Paul calls these corrupted administrators stoicheia tou kosmou (Gal 4:3,9; Col 2:8,20), archai kai exousiai (Col 2:15; Eph 1:21; 3:10; 6:12), archontes tou aiônos toutou (“rulers of this age,” 1 Cor 2:6–8), and kosmokratores tou skotous (“world-rulers of this darkness,” Eph 6:12). All of these terms describe the same category of being at different levels of specificity. The stoicheia are the divine sons of Deuteronomy 32 who became the cosmic tyrants of the present age. A
Step 4 — Christ’s victory: The cross cancelled the debt-document that gave these powers legal standing (Col 2:14), disarmed them (Col 2:15), and put them to open shame. Believers who have “died with Christ from the stoicheia” (Col 2:20) are no longer under their jurisdiction. The Deuteronomy 32 allotment is being reversed: the nations are reclaimed from the stoicheia through the gospel (Matt 28:19; Col 1:13). A
Paul’s stoicheia tou kosmou is his Greek rendering of the Hebrew divine-council framework. The term captures what no single Hebrew word could: the fusion of cosmic governance (elements, stars, seasons) with territorial spiritual authority (nations, peoples, religions). The stoicheia are the b’nei ’elohim of Deuteronomy 32, the condemned ’elohim of Psalm 82, the archontes of 1 Corinthians 2:6–8, and the archai kai exousiai of Colossians 2:15 — all the same category of being, named differently for different rhetorical contexts. A

The Cosmic Powers in Romans 8
Angeloi (angels): The general category of supernatural beings — both loyal and rebellious. Paul includes them to be comprehensive: even good angels cannot override God’s love.
Archai (principalities/rulers): The same term as in Colossians 2:15 and Ephesians 1:21. These are the territorial rulers — the stoicheia by another name. Paul lists them alongside angels to show he is thinking of the full cosmic hierarchy. A
Dynameis (powers): The third tier of Paul’s hierarchy (cf. Eph 1:21; 1 Cor 15:24). These are forces of supernatural power, whether exercised benevolently or malevolently.
Hypsôma (height) and bathos (depth): These are astrological terms. In Hellenistic astrology, hypsôma was a planet’s highest point (zenith/exaltation) and bathos was its lowest point (nadir/depression). The stoicheia, for all their cosmic authority, remain creatures. A

The stoicheia, the archai, the dynameis, the astral hypsôma and bathos — all of them together — cannot override the love of God in Christ. This is the pastoral application of the Colossians 2:15 triumph: the powers have been publicly humiliated, and believers need not fear them.

11.10 The Ancient Near Eastern Backdrop

Paul’s audience did not need to be told that cosmic powers governed the elements. The entire ancient world assumed it. Mesopotamian religion systematically identified each major god with a celestial body and a natural domain.

Gods, Planets, and the Stoicheia Worldview
In Mesopotamian theology, there was no separation between astronomy and theology. To observe the planets was to observe the activities of the gods. The movement of Venus was the movement of Ishtar. The phases of the moon were the activities of Sin. This is precisely the worldview the stoicheia concept describes: spiritual beings operating through and as the physical elements of the cosmos.
The Greco-Roman world inherited this framework. The planets retained their divine names (Mars, Venus, Mercury, Jupiter, Saturn). The days of the week were named for these planet-gods (Sunday = Sol, Monday = Luna, Tuesday = Mars [Tiw], Wednesday = Mercury [Woden], Thursday = Jupiter [Thor], Friday = Venus [Frigg], Saturday = Saturn). To live in the ancient world was to live inside a calendar governed by the stoicheia — literally, every day was named for a cosmic power. B
This is why Paul’s warning about “days, months, seasons, and years” (Gal 4:10) is not petty calendar policing. It strikes at the heart of the ancient world’s spiritual infrastructure. To organize one’s life around the celestial calendar was to submit to the governance of the beings who controlled it. Paul says: you have died to those powers. Stop living under their schedule.
The Deuteronomy 32 worldview and the Mesopotamian god-planet system describe the same reality from different perspectives. Israel’s prophets said the nations were given to b’nei ’elohim. Mesopotamia said the gods governed through the stars. Paul says both are true — and Christ has defeated them all. B

11.11 The Church Fathers on Cosmic Spirits

The early church did not read the stoicheia passages as abstract theology. They engaged them as descriptions of real cosmic powers that the church confronted daily in a pagan world.

Patristic Readings of the Stoicheia
Origen (c. 184–253 AD) explicitly identified the stoicheia as spiritual beings governing the physical elements. In De Principiis 3.2.1–5, Origen argued that angelic powers were assigned to the natural order — fire, water, air, earth — and that some of these powers fell from their mandate, becoming the hostile forces Paul describes. Origen wrote: “There are certain invisible powers to which earthly things have been entrusted for administration” and that these powers were the ones Christ “despoiled” at the cross (Col 2:15). For Origen, the stoicheia were angel-administrators gone wrong — precisely the Deuteronomy 32 framework. B
Augustine (354–430 AD) addressed the stoicheia from a different angle. In De Civitate Dei (City of God) 8–10, Augustine argued at length against the “theologians” (by which he meant the Neoplatonists and theurgists) who taught that one must appease the daemones governing the elements in order to ascend to God. Augustine insisted that Christ’s mediation rendered all such cosmic intermediaries unnecessary. His critique of “elemental spirits” as false mediators is functionally identical to Paul’s critique of the stoicheia: they are real but defeated, and Christians need not placate them. B
Clement of Alexandria (c. 150–215 AD) in the Stromateis (4.25) discussed the stoicheia as the governing powers of the physical world whose authority was superseded by Christ. Clement connected the angelic governance of the nations (the Deuteronomy 32 tradition) with the stoicheia explicitly, arguing that the “angels of the nations” were the same beings who administered the elemental forces.
Athanasius (c. 296–373 AD) in De Incarnatione (On the Incarnation) 25–27 described the pre-Christian world as one dominated by “the deception of the demons and the working of the elements” — treating stoicheia-worship and demon-worship as two descriptions of the same phenomenon. The incarnation and death of Christ destroyed this deception “at its root.”
The patristic consensus is clear: the early church understood the stoicheia as personal spiritual powers, not abstract principles. Whether they emphasized the angelic (Origen, Clement) or demonic (Augustine, Athanasius) dimension, the fathers consistently read Paul as describing real supernatural entities whose authority over the cosmos was broken by the cross. The “basic principles” reading is a modern development that the ancient church did not share. B

11.12 The Modern Scholarly Debate

The stoicheia question has generated some of the most vigorous academic discussion in Pauline studies. The major modern contributors each bring different evidence to bear.

Clinton Arnold — Powers of Darkness (1992) and The Colossian Syncretism (1996)
Arnold’s contribution was to ground the stoicheia debate in the archaeological and magical evidence from the Lycus Valley and the broader Hellenistic world. He demonstrated that the Colossian “philosophy” Paul opposes was not a theoretical system but a practical spiritual technology involving angel invocation, protective amulets, astral calendar observances, and dietary asceticism — all aimed at managing one’s relationship with the cosmic powers.
Arnold showed that Greek magical papyri (PGM) and Jewish synagogue inscriptions from western Asia Minor routinely invoked angels by name, sought protection from elemental spirits, and treated the stoicheia as personal beings who needed to be appeased. This was the world Paul’s audience inhabited. B
Key conclusion: Paul’s stoicheia language would have been understood by his original audience as referring to personal spiritual powers, not abstract philosophical principles. The “basic principles” reading is a product of post-Enlightenment demythologization, not first-century Greco-Roman semantics.
Michael Heiser — The Unseen Realm (2015) and Demons (2020)
Heiser placed the stoicheia squarely within the Deuteronomy 32 worldview. His argument: Paul’s stoicheia tou kosmou are the divine sons of the Most High who were allotted governance over the nations at Babel and subsequently corrupted their mandate. The stoicheia are not merely “elemental spirits” in a vague cosmic sense — they are the specific territorial rulers described in Deuteronomy 32:8, condemned in Psalm 82, identified as national sarim in Daniel 10, and defeated at the cross in Colossians 2:15. A
Heiser further argued that Paul’s reference to the law being “ordained through angels” (Gal 3:19) is not incidental but structural: even Israel’s Torah was mediated through created intermediaries. Before Christ, all of humanity — Jew and Gentile alike — was under the governance of created spiritual powers. The difference was that Israel’s mediators served Yahweh (however imperfectly), while the nations’ mediators had rebelled. Christ’s coming terminated the entire system of mediated governance and established direct sonship (Gal 4:4–7). B
N.T. Wright — Paul and the Faithfulness of God (2013)
Wright took a characteristically nuanced position. He argued that the stoicheia cannot be reduced to either “basic principles” or “personal spirits” alone. For Wright, Paul’s genius was to use a term that collapses the distinction between the regulatory systems (Torah, pagan religion, astral calendars) and the spiritual powers that operated through them. B
Wright emphasized that Paul’s argument in Galatians 4 deliberately equates the Jewish experience under Torah with the Gentile experience under paganism — not because Torah is equivalent to paganism in moral quality, but because both represent the old age of mediated, custodial governance that Christ has superseded. The stoicheia are the “powers that be” in both systems: real spiritual authorities operating through religious structures, now rendered obsolete by the direct indwelling of the Spirit (Gal 4:6).
Wright’s conclusion: The stoicheia tou kosmou are best understood as “the forces that controlled the old world order” — simultaneously personal, systemic, and cosmic. To try to choose between “personal spirits” and “impersonal principles” is to impose a modern distinction that Paul would not have recognized.
Objection: “Stoicheia as Personal Beings Is a Late Development”
Some scholars (notably Schweizer, 1988; Rusam, 2000) argue that the personal-spiritual meaning of stoicheia is not attested before the 2nd century AD and therefore cannot be Paul’s intended meaning. The earliest clear use of stoicheia for personal spirits comes from texts like the Testament of Solomon, which may postdate Paul.
The response: (1) This argument from silence assumes that surviving texts perfectly represent first-century usage. The Greek magical papyri and Jewish apocalyptic traditions demonstrate that spirit-element associations were widespread well before Paul. (2) 1 Enoch (3rd–1st c. BC) and Jubilees (2nd c. BC) clearly assign angelic spirits to every element — the concept is pre-Pauline even if the specific word stoicheion for “spirit” is not explicitly attested. (3) Paul may be the innovator who first applied the Greek philosophical term stoicheia to the Jewish concept of element-governing angels. The fact that later texts (T. Sol.) follow his usage may indicate that Paul’s own letters established the semantic bridge. (4) Even if the “personal spirit” sense of stoicheia is not pre-Pauline, Paul’s own text demands it: he equates the stoicheia with “those that by nature are not gods” (Gal 4:8) and with the archai kai exousiai defeated at the cross (Col 2:15). The internal evidence overrides the external argument from silence. A

11.13 The Synthesis — What Are the Stoicheia tou Kosmou?

Drawing together the lexical, exegetical, historical, and theological evidence, the following synthesis emerges:

The Stoicheia tou Kosmou — A Comprehensive Definition
1. They are personal spiritual beings — not abstract principles. Paul identifies them with entities “not gods by nature” (Gal 4:8), calls them “weak and worthless” (Gal 4:9), and equates them with the archai kai exousiai that Christ “disarmed” (Col 2:15). Abstract principles cannot be disarmed or put to open shame.
2. They are the divine beings allotted governance over the nations (Deut 32:8–9) who corrupted their mandate (Ps 82:1–7). Their Hebrew name is b’nei ’elohim (“sons of God”); their Greek names include archai, exousiai, archontes, kosmokratores, and stoicheia. The terms vary by context; the referent is continuous.
3. They governed the cosmos through its elements — the physical substances (earth, water, air, fire), the celestial bodies (sun, moon, planets, stars), and the temporal rhythms (days, months, seasons, years). In the Jewish and pagan worldview, these elements were not impersonal — they were the visible face of invisible governing powers.
4. They operated through religious regulatory systems — both the Torah (mediated by angels, Gal 3:19; Heb 2:2) and pagan cult (mediated by demons posing as gods, 1 Cor 10:20). Both systems functioned as custodial regimes that held humanity in bondage until the appointed time (Gal 4:2–4).
5. They wielded the “certificate of debt” (cheirographon, Col 2:14) — the record of human transgression that gave them legal standing to hold humanity under their authority. The law’s condemnation was their weapon; human guilt was their leverage.
6. They were defeated at the cross. Christ cancelled the debt-document (Col 2:14), disarmed the rulers (Col 2:15), triumphed over them in a cosmic public spectacle (Col 2:15), and granted believers co-crucifixion that severed their bond to the stoicheia (Col 2:20). The powers are not yet destroyed (that comes at the eschaton, 1 Cor 15:24), but they are legally defeated, publicly humiliated, and progressively losing territory as the gospel advances.
7. Returning to their governance is apostasy. Whether through Judaizing Torah-observance (Gal 4:9–10) or pagan-syncretistic angel-worship (Col 2:18–23), any return to the regulatory systems of the stoicheia is a return to slavery. Paul’s pastoral urgency on this point is extreme — he calls it re-enslavement and expresses fear that his labor may have been “in vain” (Gal 4:11).
The stoicheia tou kosmou are Paul’s Greek name for the cosmic administration that the Hebrew scriptures call the b’nei ’elohim of Deuteronomy 32 and the condemned ’elohim of Psalm 82. They are personal, territorial, cosmic, and defeated. They governed the old world through elements, calendars, and religious systems. Christ’s death dismantled their authority. The church now lives in the overlap between their defeat and their destruction — free from their jurisdiction, still contending with their resistance (Eph 6:12), and advancing the reclamation of the nations that Psalm 82:8 promised and the Great Commission commands. A

Pastoral Implications: Why This Matters
1. Identity: Christians are not under the jurisdiction of the stoicheia. They have been “delivered from the domain of darkness and transferred to the kingdom of his beloved Son” (Col 1:13). This is not metaphor — it is a change of cosmic citizenship.
2. Freedom: Paul’s warning against returning to “days, months, seasons, and years” (Gal 4:10) and “do not handle, do not taste, do not touch” (Col 2:21) is not anti-ritual per se. It is a declaration that no created power has a regulatory claim on the believer. Superstition, astrology, lucky days, fear of cosmic forces — all of these represent submission to defeated stoicheia.
3. Mission: The Great Commission (Matt 28:19) is the Deuteronomy 32 allotment running in reverse. Every nation was given to a ben ’elohim; now every nation is being reclaimed for Christ. The gospel is a territorial invasion — cosmic transfer on a global scale.
4. Warfare: Ephesians 6:12 makes clear that the defeated stoicheia have not yet been destroyed. The church contends with them “in the heavenly places” until the final subjugation (1 Cor 15:24–28). The armor of God is not ceremonial — it is equipment for real engagement with real cosmic powers.
The stoicheia tou kosmou are not relics of ancient superstition. They are the apostolic identification of the cosmic powers behind human religion, politics, and culture — powers allotted governance by the Most High, corrupted in their administration, defeated at the cross, and progressively being dispossessed as the gospel goes to all nations. To understand the stoicheia is to understand why Paul saw the entire pre-Christian world — both Jewish and pagan — as a single system of bondage, and why he saw Christ’s death as the decisive liberation of the cosmos itself.
