Chapter 5

Psalm 82 — The Verdict on the Gods

Verse by verse, word by word — the most theologically explosive psalm in the Bible, where Yahweh stands in judgment over the divine council and sentences the corrupt ’elohim to die like mortals.

Psalm 82 is eight verses long. In those eight verses, it dismantles any attempt to reduce the biblical worldview to simple monotheism or to dismiss the divine council as metaphor. The grammar is stark, the vocabulary is precise, and the theological implications are staggering. A

With the courtroom scene established — one God standing, many gods being judged — God speaks directly to the accused. What follows is a formal indictment, as precise as any legal charge.

Verses 2–4 — The Indictment

Psalm 82:2–4 ESV
“How long will you judge unjustly and show partiality to the wicked? Selah 3Give justice to the weak and the fatherless; maintain the right of the afflicted and the destitute. 4Rescue the weak and the needy; deliver them from the hand of the wicked.”
Parsing — Psalm 82:2–4
תִּשְׁפְּטוּ־עָוֶל tishp’tu-‘avel

“You judge unjustly.” The verb is 2nd person masculine plural — Yahweh is addressing multiple beings directly. The noun ‘avel (עָוֶל, “injustice/unrighteousness”) functions as an adverbial accusative: they “judge with injustice.” These beings were supposed to govern justly; they have failed catastrophically.
וּפְנֵי־רְשָׁעִים תִּשְּׂאוּ ûph’nei-r’sha‘im tiss’û
Qal Imperfect 2mp נשׂא n-s-’
“And you lift up the faces of the wicked.” The idiom nasa’ panim means “to show favoritism, to be partial.” Literally: “lift the face” = show deference. These ’elohim are accused of favoritism toward the wicked rather than protecting the vulnerable. This is the precise charge: they used their delegated authority to benefit oppressors.
שִׁפְטוּ shiph’tu
Qal Imperative 2mp שפט sh-p-t
“Judge!” (v. 3) The imperative mood — a direct command from Yahweh to the ’elohim. This is not advice; it is a sovereign decree. The objects are dal (weak), yatôm (fatherless), ‘ani (afflicted), rash (destitute) — four categories of the vulnerable. The command in verses 3–4 restates the original mandate: these beings were assigned to protect the powerless and govern with justice.

The indictment is followed by a devastating aside. God turns from addressing the ’elohim directly to speaking about them — and the shift in grammatical person tells us everything.

Verse 5 — The Diagnosis

Psalm 82:5 ESV
They have neither knowledge nor understanding, they walk about in darkness; all the foundations of the earth are shaken.
Parsing — Psalm 82:5
לֹא יָדְעוּ וְלֹא יָבִינוּ lo’ yad’û v’lo’ yavinû
Qal Imperfect 3mp (both) ידע y-d-‘ / בין b-y-n
The shift from 2mp (direct address) to 3mp (third-person description) is significant. Yahweh now speaks about the ’elohim to an audience — the psalmist and, through him, the reader. “They do not know and they do not understand” uses two cognition verbs (yada‘ = experiential knowledge; bin = discernment/understanding) in double negation: total cognitive failure.
יִמּוֹטוּ כָּל־מוֹסְדֵי אָרֶץ yimmôtu kol-môs’dei ’arets
Niphal Imperfect 3mp מוט m-w-ṭ
“All the foundations of the earth totter/shake.” Yimmôṭû is Niphal imperfect 3mp of môṭ (“to totter, shake”). The môs’dôt (foundations) of the earth are unstable because their appointed governors are corrupt. This is cosmic language: the failure of the divine administrators causes structural instability in creation itself. When the spiritual rulers of nations fail, the nations themselves crumble.

Now comes the climax. God turns back to direct address — and delivers the most shocking sentence in the entire Hebrew Bible. He first affirms what they are, and then pronounces what they will become.

Critical Parsing — Psalm 82:6–7
אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם ’elohim ’attem
Noun + Pronoun 2mp Plural
“Gods you-are.” The independent pronoun ’attem (2mp) creates an emphatic verbless clause: “YOU are ’elohim.” God himself declares their status. They are ’elohim — this is not a metaphor or an honorific. It is Yahweh affirming their ontological category. They are divine beings. And yet —
כְּאָדָם תְּמוּתוּן k’’adam t’mûtûn
Qal Imperfect 2mp מות m-w-t
“Like men you shall die.” The preposition k’- (like, as) + ’adam (man/humanity). This is the devastating sentence. The comparison k’’adam (“like man”) only makes sense if these beings are not men. You do not threaten humans with “you will die like humans” — that is a tautology. The simile has force only if the subjects are inherently immortal beings who are now sentenced to mortality. This single word (k’’adam) destroys the “human judges” interpretation.
וּכְאַחַד הַשָּׂרִים תִּפֹּלוּ ûkh’’achad hassarim tippolû
Qal Imperfect 2mp נפל n-p-l
“And like one of the princes you shall fall.” Hassarim (the princes) — which princes? Given the cosmic context, these are likely the fallen ’elohim who have already been brought low (cf. Isa 14:12, “How you have fallen”). The verb naphal (to fall) connects to the Nephilim (“fallen ones”) of Genesis 6 — a verbal echo linking the corruption of the divine council to the pre-Flood catastrophe.
The “Human Judges” Interpretation — A Grammatical Autopsy
“The ’elohim in Psalm 82 are human judges. God calls them ‘gods’ because they represent divine authority.”
Five fatal problems:

(1) K’’adam t’mûtûn. “Like men you shall die.” If they are men, this is meaningless. Telling a human judge “you will die like a human” is not a punishment — it is a statement of the obvious. The simile only functions as a demotion if the beings are above the human category.

(2) Ba-‘adat-’El. “In the assembly of God.” The ‘adah + ’El construct has direct Ugaritic cognate (‘dt ’ilm), which always refers to the divine assembly of the gods, never to a human court. The psalmist uses established divine-council terminology.

(3) B’nei ‘Elyôn. “Sons of the Most High.” This is a variant of b’nei ’elohim (Gen 6:2; Job 1:6, 2:1, 38:7). ‘Elyôn (“Most High”) is exclusively a divine title. “Sons of the Most High” places these beings in the same category as the divine council members of Job.

(4) “All the foundations of the earth are shaken” (v. 5). Human judicial corruption does not “shake the foundations of the earth.” This is cosmic language for the structural instability caused by corrupt divine governance over the nations.

(5) Jesus’ use in John 10:34–36. Jesus quotes Psalm 82:6 with an a fortiori argument: “If he [God] called them gods, to whom the word of God came — and Scripture cannot be broken — do you say of him whom the Father consecrated and sent into the world, ‘You are blaspheming’?” The argument works regardless of whether the ’elohim are human or divine — but the force is far greater if they are divine: “If God calls even divine beings ‘gods,’ how much more can the one God himself sent claim the title?”

The psalm does not end with judgment. It ends with a prayer — the psalmist’s own voice breaking through, turning the courtroom verdict into a cry for God to finish what he started.

Psalm 82: The Complete Arc
V. 1 — Yahweh convenes the divine council for judgment.
V. 2 — The indictment: they judged unjustly and favored the wicked.
V. 3–4 — The restated mandate: protect the weak and vulnerable.
V. 5 — The diagnosis: total failure of understanding; cosmic instability results.
V. 6 — Yahweh affirms their identity: they are ’elohim, sons of the Most High.
V. 7 — The sentence: despite their divine nature, they will die like men.
V. 8 — The eschatological hope: Yahweh himself will arise, judge the earth, and inherit ALL nations — reversing the Deuteronomy 32 allotment and reclaiming what the corrupt ’elohim misruled.

Jesus and Psalm 82 — John 10:34–36

There is exactly one place in the New Testament where Psalm 82 is quoted by name. It is not in a theological treatise or an eschatological vision. It is in the middle of a confrontation where Jesus is about to be stoned for blasphemy — and he reaches for this psalm, the divine-council judgment text, to defend his own identity claim. The passage is John 10:34–36, and when read against the backdrop of everything Chapter 5 has established, it becomes one of the most significant christological arguments in the Gospels. B

John 10:34–36 ESV
Jesus answered them, “Is it not written in your Law, ‘I said, you are gods’? 35If he called them gods to whom the word of God came — and Scripture cannot be broken — 36do you say of him whom the Father consecrated and sent into the world, ‘You are blaspheming,’ because I said, ‘I am the Son of God’?”

The context is electric. The Pharisees have just picked up stones because Jesus declared “I and the Father are one” (John 10:30). The charge is formal: “You, being a man, make yourself God” (10:33). They are ready to execute him for blasphemy. Jesus’ response is not a retreat. It is a devastating counter-argument drawn from the very psalm that describes God’s judgment on the divine council.

The Greek phrase ho nomos hymōn (“your Law”) uses nomos broadly for the entire Hebrew Bible — Psalm 82 is not in the Torah proper. The definite article plus the possessive pronoun gives the phrase a pointed edge: “your own Scripture says this.” Jesus anchors the argument in a text the Pharisees cannot deny.

He quotes Psalm 82:6 directly: theoi este — “gods you are” — the standard LXX rendering of the Hebrew ’elohim ’attem that we parsed above. Then he constructs what rabbinic logic calls a qal wahomer argument — from the lesser to the greater. The logical structure is precise:

The A Fortiori Structure of John 10:34–36
Premise 1: God himself called certain beings theoi (“gods”) — “to whom the word of God came.”
Premise 2: “Scripture cannot be broken” (hē graphē ou dynatai lythēnai) — this designation stands as authoritative. Whatever God called them, that they are.
Premise 3: Jesus is “the one whom the Father consecrated (hēgiasen) and sent (apesteilen) into the world” — a unique, unrepeatable mission of divine origin.
Conclusion: If the title “gods” is biblically legitimate for lesser beings, then accusing Jesus of blasphemy for claiming to be “the Son of God” is incoherent. His claim is greater than theirs, yet their title stands in Scripture without scandal. How much more does the title belong to the one the Father uniquely set apart?

The Greek verb hēgiasen (aorist active indicative of hagiazō, “to consecrate, sanctify, set apart”) describes the Father’s direct action upon the Son — a singular, completed act of consecration. The verb apesteilen (aorist active of apostellō, “to send on a mission”) carries mission language that presupposes pre-existence: the Son was sent from the Father into the world. He did not originate within it.

The crux of interpretation lies in a question this chapter has already answered: who are the ’elohim of Psalm 82:6? Four scholarly positions have emerged, and the force of Jesus’ argument shifts dramatically depending on which reading is adopted.

Four Scholarly Positions on Jesus’ Use of Psalm 82
Position A: The Traditional “Human Judges” A Fortiori (Calvin, Morris, some Reformed tradition). If God called human judges “gods” because they received God’s word and exercised divinely delegated authority, how much more can Jesus — whom the Father personally consecrated and sent — claim to be the Son of God? The argument works, but only at reduced force. As the five fatal problems in the counter-box above demonstrate, the “human judges” reading of Psalm 82 collapses under the weight of k’’adam t’mûtûn. If the ’elohim are already human, the comparison “like men you shall die” is a tautology, and the foundation of Jesus’ argument is weakened at the very point it needs to be strongest.
Position B: Heiser’s Divine Council A Fortiori (PREFERRED). Michael Heiser (SBL Regional 2011 paper; Unseen Realm ch. 29; Naked Bible Podcast #109) argues that the argument reaches its maximum force when the ’elohim are recognized as actual divine beings: “If God himself called divine beings — members of his own heavenly council — ‘gods’ and ‘sons of the Most High,’ and Scripture cannot be broken on this point, then on what possible basis can you accuse me of blasphemy for calling myself the Son of God? If the title ‘god’ is biblically applied to lesser beings, how much more does it belong to the one the Father uniquely consecrated and sent?” The crucial insight is what Jesus is not saying. He is not saying “we are all gods” or “the Pharisees are gods.” He is saying: “Your Bible already contains the category of divine beings other than Yahweh. If that is not blasphemy in your own Scripture, then my claim to unique divine Sonship — which is above their status — certainly is not.”
Position C: D.A. Carson’s Mediating View (The Gospel According to John, Pillar NTC). Carson acknowledges the interpretive difficulty and notes that Jesus’ argument is partly ad hominem — using the Pharisees’ own Scripture against them to blunt their charge. The deeper claim remains: Jesus is the unique Son, consecrated and sent by the Father. Carson does not fully commit to either the human or divine reading of the Psalm 82 ’elohim but recognizes the qal wahomer structure works with either.
Position D: Keener and Beasley-Murray (Rhetorical Strategy). Craig Keener (The Gospel of John, 2003) emphasizes the rhetorical strategy: Jesus uses Scripture to create an unanswerable dilemma. If “gods” in Psalm 82 is legitimate Scripture, then Jesus’ lesser claim to “Son of God” cannot be blasphemy. Beasley-Murray (John, WBC) similarly focuses on the logical force: the argument works regardless of one’s interpretation of Psalm 82 because the point is about scriptural precedent for divine titles applied to non-Yahweh figures. The Pharisees are trapped either way.
Assessment: All four positions agree that Jesus’ argument is an a fortiori: from the lesser case (beings called “gods” in Psalm 82) to the greater case (the one the Father consecrated and sent). The divine council reading (Position B) makes the argument more powerful and more coherent because it does not require the grammatically untenable “human judges” interpretation. Jesus is not lowering himself to the level of the ’elohim. He is standing above them: “If lesser divine beings bear the title, how much more the one whom the Father himself set apart?”

The statement “Scripture cannot be broken” (hē graphē ou dynatai lythēnai) deserves special attention. Jesus affirms the authority of Psalm 82 in the strongest possible terms. He treats the psalm as binding, authoritative, and non-negotiable — including its testimony about divine beings called ’elohim. This is not a throw-away proof-text. Jesus stakes his entire defense on the fact that Psalm 82 means what it says: God himself called these beings “gods.” The argument collapses if that designation is merely metaphorical.

What John 10:34–36 Tells Us About Jesus’ Divine Council Theology
1. Jesus knew Psalm 82 and selected it deliberately. He did not stumble onto this text. In the middle of a lethal confrontation about his divine identity, he reached for the one psalm that describes God’s judgment on the divine council. That is not accidental — it is a man who knows the cosmic architecture and locates himself within it.

2. Jesus presupposes the Deuteronomy 32 framework. His argument only has maximum force if there is a real category of divine beings called ’elohim who are distinct from Yahweh. The Pharisees, steeped in Second Temple Judaism, would have known this tradition. Jesus builds on it rather than explaining it, indicating he expected them to follow the reference.

3. Jesus places himself ABOVE the council members. He is not claiming to be one of the ’elohim of Psalm 82. He is “the one the Father consecrated and sent” — a unique, unrepeatable mission. The ’elohim are subordinates who failed. Jesus is the Son who fulfills. The gap between them is not a matter of degree but of kind.

4. This passage connects Psalm 82 to Christology. The psalm is no longer merely a courtroom scene from Israel’s worship. It is the text Jesus himself invokes to explain who he is. Every time we read Psalm 82, we should hear John 10 echoing underneath it: the one who will “arise, judge the earth, and inherit all the nations” (Ps 82:8) has arrived, and he is standing in the temple courts, stones at his feet, quoting the verdict.

For the framework of this study, Jesus’ use of Psalm 82 is a linchpin. It confirms that the divine council is not a scholarly curiosity or an artifact of comparative religion. The Messiah himself treats it as operative theology — a living reality that explains his own mission, his own identity, and the cosmic stakes of the confrontation he is waging. B

The Divine Council Inversion — Humans Judging Angels

Psalm 82 opens with God judging the ’elohim. It closes with a cry for God to arise and inherit all the nations. But what happens between the verdict and the inheritance? Who executes the sentence? The answer, when the full biblical arc is traced, is staggering: redeemed humans — the very creatures the corrupt ’elohim were supposed to protect — will sit in judgment over them. B

1 Corinthians 6:2–3 ESV
Or do you not know that the saints will judge the world? And if the world is to be judged by you, are you incompetent to try trivial cases? 3Do you not know that we will judge angels? How much more, then, matters pertaining to this life!

Paul drops this statement almost casually — as an aside in a discussion about property disputes among Corinthian believers. His rhetoric presupposes that this teaching was already part of early Christian catechesis: “Do you not know?” (ouk oidate) implies they should already know this. They were taught it and have apparently forgotten its implications.

The Greek is precise. Tous angelous krinoumen — “we will judge the angels.” The verb krinō (future active indicative: krinoumen) means “to judge, render a verdict, decide a case.” The definite article tous before angelous indicates specific, known angels — not angels in general, but the angels. Which angels? The context of the entire divine-council narrative points to one answer: the corrupt ’elohim of Psalm 82 — the territorial princes of Deuteronomy 32, the archai and exousiai disarmed at the cross (Colossians 2:15).

This is where the full chain comes into view. Each link connects Psalm 82’s verdict to its eschatological execution:

The Judgment Chain — From Psalm 82:8 to Revelation 20:4
Psalm 82:8 — “Arise, O God, judge the earth; for you shall inherit all the nations!” The psalmist cries out for Yahweh to execute the verdict on the corrupt ’elohim and reclaim every nation they misruled. The verb nachal (“to inherit, take possession”) directly reverses the Deuteronomy 32:8 allotment.
Daniel 7:9–10 — “Thrones were placed, and the Ancient of Days took his seat… the court sat in judgment, and the books were opened.” The divine court convenes. Multiple thrones are set. The judicial machinery described in Psalm 82 is being activated on a cosmic scale.
Daniel 7:22 — “Judgment was given for the saints of the Most High, and the time came when the saints possessed the kingdom.” The saints do not merely receive a verdict in their favor — they are given the authority to render judgments. The “saints of the Most High” (qaddishē ‘Elyōnin) parallel the “sons of the Most High” (b’nei ‘Elyôn) of Psalm 82:6. The title transfers from the corrupt divine administrators to the redeemed human community.
Matthew 19:28 — “Jesus said to them, ‘Truly, I say to you, in the new world, when the Son of Man will sit on his glorious throne, you who have followed me will also sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel.’” Human beings — former fishermen, tax collectors, zealots — are assigned thrones and judicial authority.
1 Corinthians 6:2–3 — Paul extends the scope beyond Israel to the entire cosmos: the saints will judge the world and the angels. The twelve thrones of Matthew 19 expand to encompass the full eschatological tribunal.
Revelation 20:4 — “Then I saw thrones, and seated on them were those to whom the authority to judge was committed.” John sees the Daniel 7 vision fulfilled: thrones set, judgment authority granted, the saints reigning.
The inversion is complete. The ’elohim who “judged unjustly” (Psalm 82:2) and “showed partiality to the wicked” will themselves be judged by the very humans they were supposed to protect. The corrupt judges become the judged. The weak creatures they neglected become their judges. This is the eschatological conclusion of Psalm 82’s verdict — not merely that the gods are sentenced, but that humanity in Christ is the instrument of that sentence’s execution.

Paul’s statement that the saints will “judge the world” (1 Cor 6:2) connects directly to Psalm 82:8: “Arise, O God, judge the earth.” The psalm says God will do this. Paul says the saints will do this. This is not a contradiction — it is a fulfillment. God judges the earth through his people, in union with Christ. The pattern is consistent throughout Scripture: God delegates, and this time, the delegation goes to a community that will not corrupt the mandate. Unlike the ’elohim of Psalm 82, who received authority and twisted it, the redeemed humanity of 1 Corinthians 6 receives authority as those who have been transformed by the same Christ who disarmed the powers at the cross.

The Replacement of the Divine Administrators
Under the Deuteronomy 32 arrangement, the b’nei ’elohim were the administrators and humanity was the administered. Under the new arrangement, the roles reverse. The ’elohim are stripped of authority (Psalm 82:7: “like men you shall die”). Humanity in Christ is elevated to judicial authority (1 Cor 6:3: “we will judge angels”). The corrupt stewards are replaced not by better angels but by glorified humans — beings who earned the title “sons of God” not by ontological birthright but through suffering, faithfulness, and union with the Son the Father consecrated and sent.

This is the eschatological reversal the psalmist prayed for. “Arise, O God, judge the earth, for you shall inherit all the nations” — and God’s answer is not merely to reclaim the nations from the corrupt ’elohim but to reclaim them through redeemed humanity. The Deuteronomy 32 partition is dissolved. The Psalm 82 verdict is executed. The Daniel 7 thrones are occupied. And the occupants are the saints.

Two details in Paul’s wording deserve final notice. First, his rhetorical question — “Do you not know?” — implies this teaching was part of standard apostolic instruction. The Corinthians were already taught that they would judge angels. This was not peripheral eschatology. It was foundational catechesis. Second, Paul’s logic depends on the judicial task being greater than mundane dispute resolution: “If you will judge angels, surely you can handle property disputes.” The rhetorical force requires that judging angels is cosmically significant — a bigger deal, not a smaller one. This fits the Psalm 82 framework precisely: the saints will sit in judgment over the divine administrators of the nations. If they can handle that, they can certainly handle a disagreement about money.

Psalm 82 begins with Yahweh judging the gods. It ends with a cry for Yahweh to inherit the nations. Between those two points, the New Testament inserts the church — the Body of Christ, drawn from every nation the ’elohim once held, now authorized to execute the very verdict the psalmist prayed for. The arc is complete. B
