Chapter 14

The Book of Giants & Jubilees

Two additional Second Temple texts that expand the Watcher tradition — one found among the Dead Sea Scrolls, the other structuring all of Second Temple chronology.

14.1 The Book of Giants (4Q530–532)

Here is something most people have never heard: there is an ancient text, written in Aramaic, that tells the Genesis 6 story from the Nephilim’s perspective. The giants have names — Ohyah and Hahyah, sons of the Watcher Shemihazah. They experience terrifying prophetic dreams about the coming Flood. They know what they are. They know what is coming. And they send Enoch as intermediary to plead with God on their behalf. God says no.

This is the Book of Giants, and it was found in the Dead Sea Scrolls — not in one cave, but in Caves 1, 4, and 6. Multiple copies. This was not an obscure curiosity. The community that preserved the Hebrew Bible’s oldest manuscripts also preserved this, and they kept it alongside Isaiah, Deuteronomy, and the Psalms.

Why the Book of Giants Matters
It is older than most people realize. The Qumran fragments date to the 2nd–1st centuries BC. This is not a medieval legend or a modern fabrication. It was written before Jesus was born, by Jewish scribes who took Genesis 6 at face value.
It fills a gap Genesis leaves open. Genesis 6:4 mentions the Nephilim in a single verse. The Book of Giants asks: what did these beings experience? Did they know they were doomed? The answer it gives — yes, they dreamed of their judgment and were terrified — contradicts nothing in the biblical text and adds a haunting dimension to the Flood narrative.
It confirms the supernatural reading was standard. Nobody at Qumran read Genesis 6 as the “sons of Seth” marrying the “daughters of Cain.” The scribes who copied and preserved God’s word understood b’nei ha’elohim as supernatural beings — and they wrote entire books expanding on that understanding.
Its reach proves its cultural weight. The Manichaean religion (3rd century AD) adopted the Book of Giants and carried it from Mesopotamia to China. A Jewish Second Temple text about Nephilim became part of a major world religion — the story was too powerful to stay contained.

14.1.1 The Dream Visions of Hahyah and Ohyah

The most striking feature of the Book of Giants is its portrayal of the Nephilim not as mindless brutes but as conscious, terrified beings who receive prophetic warnings of their own destruction. Two sons of Shemihazah — Hahyah and Ohyah — are at the center of the narrative. Both receive dream visions. Both are shaken to the core by what they see. And neither can escape the verdict.

The Nephilim Dreamers
אוהיה ’Ohyah
Proper name (Aramaic)
Son of Shemihazah. The name may derive from a root meaning “Woe!” — an exclamation of lament. A Nephilim whose very name is a cry of grief. He is the primary dreamer and the most vocal of the two brothers. In one fragment (4Q530 col II), Ohyah dreams of a garden being destroyed — the entire earth consumed by fire and water.
ההיה Hahyah
Proper name (Aramaic)
Son of Shemihazah, brother of Ohyah. His dream parallels Ohyah’s: he sees a great tablet being lowered into water, and when it emerges, all but three names have been washed away — only Noah and his family survive. The name may mean “the living one” — yet he is named among the doomed.

Ohyah’s first dream (4Q530 col II) is a vision of cosmic destruction. He sees a great garden — representing the populated earth — being utterly uprooted. Trees are torn out. The garden is submerged. The meaning is unmistakable: the Flood is coming, and everything planted by the Watchers and their offspring will be ripped from the ground.

4Q530 col II (reconstructed) Stuckenbruck translation
“[Ohyah said to Hahyah:] In my dream I was watching this night [and there was a garden…] and gardeners were watering… [two hundred trees and] great branches came out of their root… the whole garden, and all was [covered with water and fire…]”

The imagery is layered: the “200 trees” correspond to the 200 Watchers of 1 Enoch 6. The “great branches” represent the Nephilim offspring. The garden being flooded and burned is the Flood judgment. Ohyah sees his own extinction in symbolic form, and he knows exactly what it means.

Hahyah’s dream is even more direct. He sees a great tablet — the “Tablet of Destiny” or “Garden Tablet” — being plunged into water:

4Q530 frag. 2 (reconstructed) Stuckenbruck translation
“[And behold,] I watched: a great tablet was [lowered from heaven.] And I watched until… [it was immersed in] water. And when it was brought up from the water, upon the tablet [there remained only three names…]”

The three names that survive the water are Noah, Shem, Ham, and Japheth — the Flood survivors. Every other name on the tablet is washed clean. Hahyah sees a divine register, an official ledger of the living, and the Nephilim are not on it. This is not just a vision of destruction. It is a vision of legal erasure — their names are removed from the book of the living.

The Tablet of Destiny — Erased from the Book of Life
The “tablet” motif connects to canonical theology. The idea of a heavenly register of names appears throughout Scripture: Exodus 32:32–33 (“blot me out of the book that you have written”), Psalm 69:28 (“Let them be blotted out of the book of the living”), Daniel 12:1 (“everyone whose name shall be found written in the book”), and Revelation 20:15 (“if anyone’s name was not found written in the book of life”). Hahyah’s dream draws on the same concept: the Nephilim are erased from God’s register. B
This vision makes the Nephilim fully aware of their status. They are not merely destroyed — they are informed of their destruction in advance through dreams they cannot dismiss. The book of Giants treats the Nephilim as morally conscious beings who received fair warning. This parallels the theology of Ezekiel 33: God warns before he judges.
The “three names” detail confirms the text is a Genesis retelling. The survival of Noah’s family is so specific that it rules out the possibility that this is generic mythology. The author of the Book of Giants is reading Genesis 6–9 and telling the same story from the other side of the judgment line.

14.1.2 Mahaway the Messenger

After the dreams terrify the Nephilim, they need an interpreter. But no earthly sage can decode a vision sent from heaven. So the giants turn to the only being they know who stands in God’s council: Enoch.

The problem is that Enoch has already been translated — “God took him” (Gen 5:24). He is no longer accessible on earth by ordinary means. So the Nephilim send a messenger who can reach him: Mahaway, son of the Watcher Baraqiel.

The Nephilim’s Envoy
מהוי Mahaway (also Mahawai)
Proper name (Aramaic) Possibly from מחה m-ḥ-h “to strike, to wipe away”
Son of the Watcher Baraqiel (“Lightning of God”). Mahaway is described as capable of flight — a detail consistent with his half-angelic nature. He is sent by the Nephilim assembly to fly to Enoch and request an interpretation of the terrifying dreams. He functions as a mal’akh (messenger) — the same word used for angels in the Hebrew Bible. The irony is thick: a Nephilim serving as an “angel” (messenger) to an exalted human.

Mahaway’s flight to Enoch is described in vivid terms. He crosses great distances, passing over the desert of desolation. In some fragments, the text describes him nearly failing — burning up like Icarus — before Enoch calls out and saves him. This is not a casual errand. It is a desperate embassy from a doomed race to the one human who might intercede.

When Mahaway reaches Enoch, he delivers the dreams. And Enoch — the scribe of righteousness, the one who “walked with God” (Gen 5:24) — reads the tablet and delivers the verdict:

4Q530 frag. 7 col II (reconstructed) Stuckenbruck translation
“[Enoch the scribe said:] The tablet concerns [the judgment of] all the earth… Concerning the Watchers and concerning the Giants [and concerning the Nephilim:] the judgment [is decreed against you. You will not have peace.] You shall not live [nor shall your offspring endure…]”

There is no negotiation. No appeal. No exception. The verdict is absolute: destruction by flood, no survivors among the Nephilim. Enoch does not merely interpret the dreams — he confirms them. The dreams were true. The garden will be destroyed. The names will be washed from the tablet. And Mahaway must fly home and tell his kinsmen that there is no hope.

14.1.3 Gilgamesh in the Book of Giants

Here is a detail that surprises even scholars who specialize in Second Temple literature: the name Gilgamesh appears in the Book of Giants. But this is not the Gilgamesh of the famous Mesopotamian epic — or rather, the Book of Giants seems to know the legendary figure and reinterpret him as a Nephilim.

Is This the Same Gilgamesh?
Objection: Gilgamesh is a character from Sumerian and Akkadian mythology, not from Jewish tradition. His appearance in the Book of Giants must be a later addition or scribal error.
Response: The Second Temple Jewish authors did not operate in a cultural vacuum. They were well aware of Mesopotamian traditions — Abraham himself came from Ur of the Chaldeans (Gen 11:31). The Book of Giants appears to be doing something deliberate: taking the famous “two-thirds divine, one-third human” hero of Mesopotamian legend and reclaiming him as part of the Watcher narrative. If the Nephilim were real, then the culture-heroes that ancient civilizations worshipped — the semi-divine warriors of legend — may have been distorted memories of those very beings. The Book of Giants takes Gilgamesh from the pagan temple and puts him back where he belongs: among the doomed hybrid offspring of fallen angels. C

In the Qumran fragments, Gilgamesh (Gilgamesh in Aramaic: glgmysh) appears among the Nephilim who receive the terrifying judgment. He is not the heroic seeker of immortality from the Mesopotamian epic. He is one of the condemned — powerful, semi-divine, and facing annihilation. The fragmentary text (4Q530 frag. 2, col. III) places him in the council of giants who debate how to respond to the dream visions.

This identification is theologically significant for a reason that goes beyond literary curiosity. The Mesopotamian Gilgamesh Epic includes a flood narrative — the story of Utnapishtim — that parallels Genesis 6–9 in remarkable detail. If the Book of Giants is placing Gilgamesh among the Nephilim, it may be offering an explanation for why the Mesopotamian civilizations had flood traditions: they were recording, in distorted form, the same events Genesis records. The giants knew the flood was coming — their terror was preserved in the cultural memory of every ancient civilization, retold as heroic epics long after the original context was lost.

14.1.4 The Verdict: Destruction by Flood

The Book of Giants ends where Genesis 7 begins: with the irrevocable decree of total destruction. The Nephilim receive the verdict. Some rage against it. Some accept it. None escape it. The text fragments describe the giants turning on each other in their final hours — violence consuming the violent, chaos swallowing chaos.

4Q531 frag. 1 Stuckenbruck translation
“[And they began] to kill one another, and [to devour…] the great ones. And the earth cried out against the oppressors…”

This self-destruction parallels 1 Enoch 10:9, where God commands the angel Gabriel: “Send them against one another so that they may destroy each other in battle.” The Nephilim do not simply drown in the Flood — they are set against each other first. Their violence, which once ravaged the earth, turns inward. It is a fitting judgment: the weapon by which they terrorized creation becomes the weapon of their own destruction.

The Book of Giants as Theological Witness
It confirms the Nephilim were conscious moral agents. They dream, they deliberate, they send ambassadors, they receive verdicts. This rules out interpretations that reduce the Nephilim to mythological symbols or metaphors for human violence. The Book of Giants presents them as real beings who knew right from wrong and were held accountable for their existence. B
It confirms Enoch’s intercessory role. In 1 Enoch 12–16, Enoch serves as intermediary between the imprisoned Watchers and God. In the Book of Giants, he serves the same function for the Nephilim. This is consistent: one figure, one role, across two independent compositions. The portrait of Enoch as “scribe of righteousness” who delivers divine verdicts is a stable Second Temple tradition, not an ad hoc invention. B
It confirms that the judgment was total and irreversible. No Nephilim survive. No appeal succeeds. The dreams were warnings, not invitations to repent — because the Nephilim are the corruption. They cannot be saved from what they inherently are. This theological point connects directly to the New Testament distinction between demons (spirits of dead Nephilim, permanently condemned) and human sinners (redeemable through Christ).
The Book of Giants is not a fantasy novel about ancient monsters. It is a theological meditation on the finality of divine judgment against hybrid corruption — told, remarkably, from the perspective of the condemned. And the scribes at Qumran kept nine copies. They thought it mattered.

14.1.5 The Full Manuscript Evidence — Ten Dead Sea Scrolls Fragments

The Book of Giants is not a text known from a single crumbling scrap. It survives in ten identifiable manuscripts discovered across four separate Qumran caves — making it one of the better-attested non-biblical texts in the entire Dead Sea Scrolls collection. The manuscript evidence, catalogued definitively by Stuckenbruck (1997), tells us something important: this was not one community’s curiosity. It was widely copied, widely read, and widely preserved alongside the books we call Scripture. A

What the Manuscript Distribution Proves
Four caves, not one. The Book of Giants was found in Caves 1, 2, 4, and 6. If it existed in only one cave, we might conclude it was one scribe’s personal project. Four caves means the text was part of the shared library of the Qumran community — copied, circulated, and stored in multiple locations.
4Q203 is the earliest and foundational manuscript. Milik (1976) first identified it as part of the Enochic corpus. Its fragments preserve the Watcher names and the initial dream-vision framework. Paleographic dating places it in the late 2nd century BCE — making the composition itself likely 3rd–2nd century BCE. This is older than most people realize.
4Q530 is the crown jewel. Stuckenbruck’s reconstruction of its three columns provides the most complete reading of the dream sequences, Mahaway’s flight, and the Gilgamesh reference (Aramaic: glgmysh). Without 4Q530, we would have fragments; with it, we have a narrative.
Milik’s original theory. J.T. Milik argued that the Book of Giants was the fifth section of 1 Enoch, replacing the Parables/Similitudes. Stuckenbruck and most subsequent scholars treat it instead as an independent but closely related composition within the broader Enochic literary corpus. Either way, its connection to 1 Enoch is organic, not incidental. B
The manuscript evidence alone settles an important question: the Book of Giants was not marginal. It was preserved with the same care, in the same caves, and in similar quantities as books we consider Scripture. The scribes at Qumran thought it mattered — and they were the same scribes who gave us the oldest manuscripts of Isaiah, Deuteronomy, and the Psalms.

14.1.6 The Dreams Reconsidered — Divine Council Notification Protocol

The dream visions of Ohyah and Hahyah (covered in section 14.1.1 above) are not merely prophetic warnings. Viewed through the lens of divine council theology, they constitute a sophisticated literary device functioning as formal divine notification. The giants do not stumble upon bad news. They receive it through the same channels God uses throughout Scripture: visions in the night, symbolic imagery requiring interpretation, and an irrevocable verdict delivered through an authorized intermediary. The Nephilim are treated as defendants in a cosmic court who receive formal notice of their sentence. B

The deeper symbolic layers of each dream deserve closer attention:

Ohyah’s Garden Dream — Eden Inverted
200 trees = 200 Watchers. The garden contains two hundred trees, matching exactly the number of Watchers who descended on Mount Hermon (1 Enoch 6:6). The “great branches” growing from their roots are the Nephilim offspring. The “gardeners watering” are the Watchers nurturing their corrupt lineage on earth.
The garden submerged in water and fire. This dual-element destruction is not accidental. Water = the Flood judgment of Genesis 7. Fire = the eschatological judgment that will complete what the Flood began (2 Peter 3:7: “the present heavens and earth are stored up for fire”). The dream compresses both judgments into a single image.
The Eden inversion. The Aramaic word used is ginta (garden/paradise), deliberately evoking the Garden of Eden. Where God planted a garden for humanity’s flourishing, the Watchers “planted” a corrupt lineage. Where Eden was a place of divine-human communion, the Watchers’ garden is a place of illegitimate mixing. And just as Eden was closed after the Fall (Genesis 3:24), this garden is destroyed by flood. The literary architecture is intentional. B
Hahyah’s Tablet Dream — Blotted from the Book of Life
The tablet lowered from heaven is the heavenly book of life — the divine register of the living. The verb used in the broader tradition is machah (to erase, to blot out), the same verb that appears in Exodus 32:32–33 (“Whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book”) and Psalm 69:28 (“Let them be blotted out of the book of the living”). The Nephilim are not merely killed. They are erased from God’s register. Their destruction is bureaucratic as well as physical — a formal act of cosmic de-registration.
Three names survive. Noah, Shem, Ham (with Japheth implied). The specificity of this detail proves the author is reading Genesis 6–9. This is not generic apocalyptic imagery. It is a retelling of the Flood from the other side of the judgment line, with precise knowledge of who survived.
The Tablet of Destinies parallel. In Mesopotamian mythology, the Tablet of Destinies (tuppi shimati) determined the fates of gods and men. The Enochic tradition reclaims this concept for monotheistic theology: there is indeed a heavenly record of fates, but it belongs to YHWH alone, and he writes and erases as the sovereign Judge. The giants see their names absent from this tablet. They are not destined for anything. They are destined for nothing. C

Stuckenbruck identifies evidence of what may be a second round of dreams in fragmentary portions of 4Q531. These appear to involve direct visions of angelic armies — possibly the archangels Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, and Sariel — descending with instruments of judgment. If correctly reconstructed, this means the giants received not one but multiple rounds of divine notification, each progressively more explicit. The first dreams were symbolic (gardens, tablets). The second were literal (armies of destruction approaching). God warned before he judged — even the Nephilim received due process in the cosmic court. C

14.1.7 Mahaway the Mediator — The Theological Problem of a Nephilim Messenger

Section 14.1.2 above describes Mahaway’s flight to Enoch. But the theological significance of his role runs deeper than the narrative details. Mahaway creates a structural irony that inverts every normal category of divine council theology — and the ancient authors knew exactly what they were doing. B

Five Layers of Theological Irony
1. A Nephilim serves as a mal’akh. The Hebrew/Aramaic word mal’akh means both “angel” and “messenger.” Mahaway — the illicit offspring of a rebellious council member — functions in the role of an “angel” (messenger) to the one human who has been granted legitimate council access. A hybrid performs the angel’s job. A mortal holds the authority. Every category is inverted.
2. Power does not equal authority. Mahaway possesses angelic flight capabilities inherited from his father Baraq’el (“Lightning of God”). He can fly across the Great Desolation. He has supernatural abilities. But he cannot approach God directly. He lacks what Enoch possesses: authorized standing in the divine council. The giants have power but no credentials. This parallels the broader biblical pattern: the Watchers had legitimate council positions but forfeited them through rebellion (Jude 6: “angels who did not stay within their own position of authority”). B
3. The Great Desolation as jurisdictional boundary. Mahaway must cross the churba rabba (Great Desolation) — a liminal wilderness separating the world of the condemned from Enoch’s paradisiacal location. This geographic boundary represents a jurisdictional boundary: the giants have been territorially excluded from divine presence. The journey is not merely physical distance but ontological distance — the condemned crossing toward the realm of the authorized.
4. Mahaway’s near-destruction. Some Manichaean recensions describe Mahaway nearly burning up during his flight — approaching too close to divine glory without authorization, like Icarus approaching the sun. Enoch must call out to save him. If this detail reflects the Jewish original (Vorlage), it parallels the biblical prohibition against unauthorized approach to God’s presence: Leviticus 16:1–2, the death of Nadab and Abihu, who offered “unauthorized fire before the LORD.” Proximity to holiness without authorization is lethal. C
5. Why Enoch and not their own fathers? The giants send an emissary to Enoch rather than to their Watcher fathers. The answer is devastating: the Watchers are already imprisoned (1 Enoch 10:4–6). The fathers cannot help. The giants are cosmically orphaned — cut off from both their angelic lineage (imprisoned) and their human lineage (corrupted). Enoch alone bridges the gap, functioning as the mediator that neither angelic nor human parentage can provide.
Mahaway’s embassy is not a minor narrative detail. It is a theological statement about the nature of authority in the divine council. The Nephilim have power without standing. Enoch has standing without supernatural nature. In the economy of the heavenly court, authorization outranks ability every time. This principle persists into the New Testament: Jesus does not come as a supernatural warrior in his first advent. He comes as an authorized human — the Son of Man who receives dominion (Daniel 7:13–14) precisely because he is the righteous one who walks with God.

14.1.8 Prosopography of the Named Giants — Who Were the Nephilim?

The first four names (Ohyah, Hahyah, Mahaway, Gilgamesh) are attested in both the Qumran Aramaic fragments and the Manichaean recensions — independently preserved across centuries and continents. The last four appear only in the Manichaean tradition or in highly disputed Qumran readings, meaning they may be secondary additions from the Manichaean transmission process. Scholarly caution is warranted for Hobabish, Sam, Nariman, and Atambish.

Beyond the names, the fragments preserve evidence of specific activities:

• Warfare and mutual slaughter (4Q531 frags. 1–3) — Giants fighting among themselves, consistent with 1 Enoch 10:9 where Gabriel is commissioned to “send them against one another.” Their violence turns inward before the Flood even arrives.
• Escalating consumption (cross-referenced with 1 Enoch 7:3–5) — The giants devour first the produce of the earth, then animals, then humans, then each other. Corruption is inherently self-consuming.
• Deliberative assembly (4Q530 col. II) — The giants convene a formal council to hear dreams, debate their interpretation, and commission Mahaway. This is the “dark council” motif — a parody of the divine council’s structure. The Nephilim replicate their fathers’ organizational pattern, but in service of their own doomed agenda.
• Reception of the verdict (4Q530 frag. 7 col. II) — Some fragments suggest varied reactions among the giants: rage, despair, acceptance. They are not a monolith. They respond as individuals to their collective sentence.

14.1.9 What the Book of Giants Adds — The Gap Neither Genesis Nor 1 Enoch Fills

Why does the Book of Giants matter? Because it answers a question that neither Genesis nor 1 Enoch addresses: What did the Nephilim experience? Genesis tells the story from God’s perspective (divine judgment). 1 Enoch tells it from the angelic perspective (the Watchers’ rebellion and punishment). The Book of Giants tells it from the condemned’s perspective — and in doing so, it reveals theological dimensions that neither of the other texts provides. B

Why This Matters Theologically
The Nephilim are morally culpable. By portraying the giants as beings who received prophetic dreams, convened assemblies, sent ambassadors, and processed verdicts, the Book of Giants establishes them as moral agents — not mere collateral damage of their parents’ sin. They are held responsible for what they are and for what they did. This is not arbitrary judgment but informed judgment: they knew and they were destroyed anyway. B
The pattern of fair warning is preserved. The God who warns Noah (Genesis 6:13), warns Nineveh through Jonah, warns Israel through the prophets, and will warn the world through Revelation’s trumpets and bowls — that same God sends dream-visions to the Nephilim before the Flood. Even the most corrupt beings in creation receive notice before judgment falls. This is not mercy (they cannot repent what they inherently are), but it is justice — the same principle expressed in Ezekiel 33:11: “I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked.”
The “dark council” motif illuminates spiritual warfare. The giants’ assembly — their deliberation, their embassy, their reception of the verdict — structurally mirrors the divine council. Just as the sons of God present themselves before YHWH (Job 1:6), the Nephilim present themselves before their own crisis. This parody-council anticipates every subsequent biblical depiction of organized evil: the “rulers and authorities in the heavenly places” of Ephesians 6:12, the “kings of the earth” who “take counsel together against the LORD” in Psalm 2:2. Evil organizes. It mimics legitimate structure. And it fails.
The Book of Giants does not contradict Genesis 6 or 1 Enoch. It completes them. It answers the question every careful reader eventually asks: What was it like for the Nephilim? The answer is haunting: they knew. They dreamed of their own destruction. They sent someone to ask if there was any hope. The answer was no. And the scribes at Qumran — the same scribes who preserved Isaiah and the Psalms — thought this mattered enough to keep nine copies.

14.2 The Book of Jubilees

If you have never heard of the Book of Jubilees, you are not alone. Most modern Christians have no idea it exists. But the ancient world knew it well — and now that the Dead Sea Scrolls have been found, we can prove that.

What it is: Jubilees (written c. 160–150 BC) retells Genesis 1 through Exodus 14, dividing all of history into “jubilee” periods — cycles of 49 years (7 × 7). Think of it as an ancient study Bible: it walks through the same events as Genesis and Exodus, in the same order, but adds explanatory detail, fills in gaps, and answers questions the Torah leaves open. It does not contradict the canonical text. It comments on it.

The format is distinctive: the entire book is framed as a revelation from the “Angel of the Presence” to Moses on Mount Sinai. God commands the angel to dictate to Moses the full history of creation through the Exodus, organized by jubilee cycles. Every event gets a precise date on a solar 364-day calendar.

14.3 The Mastema Deep Dive — Jubilees’ Single Most Important Contribution

If Jubilees contributed nothing else, the Mastema framework alone would make it indispensable for understanding Second Temple demonology — and, by extension, the world Jesus walked into.

Here is the problem Jubilees solves: the Bible tells us that the rebellious angels are imprisoned. Jude 6 says they are kept “in eternal chains under gloomy darkness.” 2 Peter 2:4 says God “cast them into Tartarus.” But the Bible also describes active demonic opposition throughout history — the Exodus plagues, the affliction of Saul, the demonized people Jesus encounters. If the fallen angels are locked up, who is doing all this?

Jubilees 10:1–9 provides the answer:

Jubilees 10:7–9 Wintermute translation
“And the LORD our God spoke to us so that we might bind all of them. And the chief of the spirits, Mastema, came and said: ‘O LORD, Creator, leave some of them before me, and let them obey my voice…’ And he said: ‘Let a tenth of them remain before him, but let nine parts go down into the place of judgment.’”

The logic is precise:

• The Watchers (the original fallen angels) are bound — imprisoned, exactly as Jude and 2 Peter describe.
• The Nephilim (their hybrid offspring) were killed in the Flood — their bodies died.
• But the Nephilim were part supernatural. Their spirits did not simply cease to exist. They became disembodied spirits — what the New Testament calls “unclean spirits” or “demons.”
• God ordered all of them bound. But Mastema (whose name comes from the Hebrew root s-t-m, שׂ-ט-מ, meaning “hostility, accusation”) negotiated to keep 10% active to test humanity.
• God permitted it — the same pattern as Job 1–2, where the adversary operates within divinely set boundaries.

This framework explains why Jesus encounters demons who are not the imprisoned Watchers — they are the spiritual residue of the Nephilim, operating under Mastema (a Satan-parallel figure) with divine permission. It also explains why demons in the Gospels are desperate, afraid of judgment, and beg not to be sent to “the abyss” (Luke 8:31) — they know the 90% are already there. They are the 10% on borrowed time.

14.3.1 Mastema’s First Appearance — The Petition (Jubilees 10:8–9)

The first time Mastema appears in Jubilees, he does something remarkable: he approaches God directly and negotiates. This is not a rogue spirit acting unilaterally. This is a being who recognizes divine authority, comes before the divine throne, and makes a formal petition — exactly as Satan does in Job 1:6–12.

Jubilees 10:8–9 (expanded context) Wintermute translation
“And the chief of the spirits, Mastema, came and said: ‘O LORD, Creator, leave some of them before me, and let them obey my voice. And let them do everything which I tell them, because if some of them are not left for me, I will not be able to exercise the authority of my will among the children of men because they are intended to corrupt and lead astray before my judgment because the evil of the sons of men is great.’ And he said: ‘Let a tenth of them remain before him, but let nine parts go down into the place of judgment.’”

Notice the layers of this exchange:

• Mastema does not demand — he petitions. He recognizes that God has the authority to bind all the spirits. His power depends entirely on divine permission.
• He gives a reason: “I will not be able to exercise the authority of my will among the children of men.” He needs the demon-spirits as tools to test, tempt, and discipline humanity.
• He frames his purpose in surprisingly theological terms: “because the evil of the sons of men is great.” He is arguing, in effect, that humans deserve to be tested because of their own wickedness.
• God grants the petition — but only partially. Ninety percent are bound. Only a fraction remain. Mastema gets less than he asked for. God sets the boundary.

This is precisely the pattern of Job 1–2, where Satan appears “among the sons of God” (divine council members), proposes a test for Job, and receives limited permission: “Behold, all that he has is in your hand. Only against him do not stretch out your hand” (Job 1:12). Both passages depict the same dynamic: the adversary operates within the divine council framework, under divine constraint, with divine permission. He is not an equal opposing power. He is a functionary with a leash.

14.3.2 Mastema and the Aqedah (Jubilees 17:16)

The binding of Isaac — the Aqedah — is one of the most theologically dense passages in the Bible. Genesis 22:1 introduces it with three simple words: “God tested Abraham.” But why? What provoked the test? The canonical text does not explain. Jubilees does.

Jubilees 17:16 Wintermute translation
“And Prince Mastema came and said before God: ‘Behold, Abraham loves Isaac his son. And he is more pleased with him than everything. Tell him to offer him as a burnt offering upon the altar. And you will see whether he will do this thing. And you will know whether he is faithful in everything in which you test him.’”

It is reading Genesis through the same theological lens that the author of Job used — the lens of the divine council, where the adversary has a recognized role as prosecutor/tester, and where God permits tests within boundaries that serve his larger purposes. Whether the author of Jubilees was “right” about Mastema’s role in the Aqedah is an open question. But his reading is theologically coherent: it takes a canonical pattern (the adversary of Job) and applies it to a passage where the same pattern fits naturally.

14.3.3 Mastema’s Attack on Moses (Jubilees 48:2–4)

One of the most mysterious episodes in the Torah is Exodus 4:24–26, where God (or his agent) seeks to kill Moses on the way to Egypt:

Exodus 4:24–26 ESV
“At a lodging place on the way the LORD met him and sought to put him to death. Then Zipporah took a flint and cut off her son’s foreskin and touched Moses’ feet with it and said, ‘Surely you are a bridegroom of blood to me!’ So he let him alone.”

This passage has generated centuries of rabbinic and scholarly debate. Why would God commission Moses in Exodus 3–4 and then try to kill him in Exodus 4? Jubilees offers an answer: it was not God himself, but Mastema acting with divine permission.

Jubilees 48:2–4 Wintermute translation
“And Prince Mastema stood up against you and desired to make you fall into the hand of Pharaoh. And he aided the magicians of Egypt who worked miracles before you… And when you were on your way [to Egypt] at the lodging place, did he not desire with all his might to kill you on account of the circumcision of your son? And I delivered you from his hand.”

Jubilees thus resolves the theological tension in Exodus 4:24 by distinguishing between God’s permission and Mastema’s agency. This is the same hermeneutical move that 1 Chronicles 21:1 makes with 2 Samuel 24:1 — where “the LORD incited David” becomes “Satan incited David.” Both accounts are true; they describe different levels of causation. God permits what Mastema executes.

The Exodus 4:24 Problem and the Mastema Solution
The canonical text is genuinely difficult. “The LORD met him and sought to put him to death” creates a theological tension that no reading can entirely dissolve. God just commissioned Moses. Why is he now killing him? The standard answer — Moses had neglected to circumcise his son — explains the what but not the how. How does God, who just spoke from a burning bush, suddenly appear as a lethal threat on the road?
Jubilees offers a coherent mechanism. Mastema, operating with divine permission, acts as the agent of the threat. This does not contradict the Exodus text — it explains how the LORD “sought to put him to death” while simultaneously preserving the commission he just gave. The same pattern appears in Job (God permits the adversary to act) and in 1 Chronicles 21 (the adversary executes what God’s anger permits). B
Zipporah’s circumcision resolves the crisis in both accounts. In Exodus, the blood of circumcision turns aside the threat. In Jubilees, the same act defeats Mastema. The covenant sign — the mark of belonging to YHWH — functions as a spiritual defense. This anticipates the Passover lamb’s blood on the doorposts, which in Jubilees also specifically defeats Mastema (48:15–18).

14.3.4 Mastema at the Exodus (Jubilees 48:9–18)

Jubilees’ account of the Exodus brings Mastema to the peak of his activity — and his ultimate defeat. According to Jubilees 48, Mastema was the power behind the Egyptian magicians who opposed Moses, the force that hardened Pharaoh’s heart, and the agent who pursued Israel at the Red Sea. But on Passover night, he was bound.

Jubilees 48:15–18 Wintermute translation
“And on the fourteenth day and on the fifteenth and on the sixteenth and on the seventeenth and on the eighteenth, Prince Mastema was bound and shut up from behind the children of Israel so that he might not accuse them. And on the nineteenth we sent them out so that they might help the children of Israel… And Prince Mastema was let loose from behind the children of Israel so that he might pursue them. And they pursued them. And the LORD cast the Egyptians into the midst of the sea.”

The sequence is stunning:

• Before the Exodus: Mastema is active, aiding the Egyptian sorcerers, hardening Pharaoh’s heart, and opposing Moses at every step. He is the “strong man” (cf. Mark 3:27) guarding Egypt’s hold on Israel.
• On Passover night: Mastema is bound. While the angel of death strikes the firstborn, the Prince of Demons is chained — unable to accuse, unable to oppose, unable to interfere. The blood of the lamb silences the accuser.
• After the crossing begins: Mastema is released — specifically so that he drives the Egyptians to pursue Israel into the sea. His hostility, once unbound, becomes the instrument of his own allies’ destruction. God uses Mastema’s aggression to draw Pharaoh’s army into the trap at the Red Sea.
• The result: The Egyptians drown. Mastema’s own malice destroys the army he was trying to help.

Passover as Binding of the Adversary
Jesus’ death was at Passover. If the original Passover involved the binding of the Prince of Demons (Mastema), then Jesus dying at Passover takes on an additional dimension: the lamb’s blood did not merely save Israel from physical death. It bound the accuser. When Jesus — the Lamb of God — dies at Passover, Colossians 2:15 says he “disarmed the rulers and authorities and put them to open shame, by triumphing over them in him.” The Jubilees framework suggests that this was what Passover was always about — the binding of the adversary through sacrificial blood. B
Revelation 20:2 echoes this pattern. “And he seized the dragon, that ancient serpent, who is the devil and Satan, and bound him.” The final binding of the adversary follows the same pattern as the Passover binding of Mastema: the accuser is restrained, his authority is revoked, and God’s people pass through judgment unscathed.
The hardening of Pharaoh’s heart gains a mechanism. Exodus attributes the hardening variously to God (Ex 9:12) and to Pharaoh himself (Ex 8:15). Jubilees adds a third agent: Mastema, operating with divine permission, reinforces Pharaoh’s own stubbornness. This is not a contradiction — it is a three-level causation model: God permits, Mastema influences, Pharaoh chooses. All three are true simultaneously.
Whether or not we accept Jubilees’ specific claims about Mastema and the Exodus, its theological framework — the adversary bound by sacrificial blood, released only to bring about his own defeat — is exactly the framework the New Testament applies to the cross. The earliest Christians understood the crucifixion through Passover. Jubilees shows us what Passover meant in the Second Temple period: not just deliverance from Egypt, but the binding of the cosmic accuser.

The functional overlap is enormous. Mastema accuses (like Satan), leads demons (like Belial), and opposes God’s people at the Exodus (like the broader adversary tradition). The New Testament consolidates. By the time of the NT, “Satan” and “the devil” (diabolos = “slanderer,” the Greek equivalent of satan) have become the dominant names, absorbing the functions of all the earlier titles. Revelation 12:9 makes the consolidation explicit: “that ancient serpent, who is called the devil and Satan.” The various Second Temple names converge into a single portrait of the cosmic adversary.
Azazel may be a special case. While the other names seem to describe the same being from different angles, Azazel in 1 Enoch is specifically one of the imprisoned Watcher leaders — bound in the desert, not free to operate. If Azazel is chained (1 En 10:4–6) while Mastema/Satan is still active, they cannot be identical. Azazel may be a particular fallen Watcher; Mastema/Satan may be a higher-order being who commands the spirits of the Nephilim (Azazel’s offspring). This distinction — between imprisoned Watchers and the still-active commander of their offspring’s spirits — is one of the most important structural insights Jubilees provides.
The proliferation of names is not confusion. It is precision — different communities naming different aspects of the same cosmic opposition. Understanding these distinctions is essential for reading the New Testament in its original context, where Jesus’ audience carried all of these associations simultaneously.

The Watcher narrative is not a late invention or a creative embellishment by a single imaginative scribe. The New Testament authors — Peter, Jude, and arguably Paul — wrote within this tradition. Recovering it is not adding to the Bible. It is recovering the context that the Bible’s own authors took for granted.

14.5 Jubilees’ Unique Contributions

Beyond the Mastema framework, Jubilees makes several distinctive contributions to Second Temple theology that shaped the world the New Testament authors inhabited. Four of these deserve special attention.

14.5.1 The 364-Day Solar Calendar

Jubilees insists — repeatedly, emphatically, and with clear theological conviction — that the true calendar ordained by God is a 364-day solar calendar, not the lunar calendar that would become standard in rabbinic Judaism. This is not a minor calendrical dispute. For Jubilees, the calendar is a matter of cosmic order.

Jubilees 6:32–38 Wintermute translation
“And command thou the children of Israel that they observe the years according to this reckoning — three hundred and sixty-four days, and these will constitute a complete year… For there will be those who will assuredly make observations of the moon — how it disturbs the seasons and comes in from year to year ten days too soon… And all the children of Israel will forget and will not find the path of the years, and will forget the new moons, and seasons, and sabbaths and they will go wrong as to all the order of the years.”

Why does this matter? Because 364 divides evenly into 52 weeks (364 ÷ 7 = 52). This means that on a 364-day calendar, every festival falls on the same day of the week every year. Passover is always on a Wednesday. The Feast of Weeks (Pentecost/Shavuot) is always on a Sunday. There is no annual variation, no calendrical dispute, no uncertainty about when to observe the appointed times.

The Qumran community adopted this calendar. The standard Jerusalem temple establishment used a lunisolar calendar. This calendrical disagreement was one of the primary reasons the Qumran community separated from the Jerusalem priesthood. They believed the temple establishment was observing the festivals on the wrong days — and therefore performing its most sacred duties at the wrong times, rendering the sacrificial system invalid.

Calendar as Cosmic Order
Jubilees treats the calendar as a creation ordinance. The 364-day year is not a human convention — it is built into the structure of creation itself (Jub 2:9). The sun, moon, and stars were created to mark these exact divisions. Using a different calendar is not merely inconvenient — it is a violation of the created order, a form of cosmic disobedience.
The Watcher connection is explicit. Remember that the Watchers’ forbidden teachings included astronomy and astrology (1 En 8:3). For Jubilees, the corruption of the calendar — switching from the divinely ordained solar system to a human lunar system — is itself a consequence of Watcher-derived knowledge. The wrong calendar is part of the Watchers’ legacy.
This explains Qumran’s preservation pattern. The Qumran community kept 15+ copies of Jubilees (more than most biblical books) because Jubilees defended the solar calendar they used. It was not just a theological text — it was the constitutional basis for their liturgical life. Every time they celebrated a festival on a different day from the Jerusalem temple, they were asserting the authority of Jubilees’ calendar over the temple establishment’s. B

14.5.2 The Angel of the Presence as Narrator

Jubilees is not narrated by a human author. It is narrated by the Angel of the Presence (mal’akh ha-panim, מַלְאַךְ הַפָנִים) — a being who stands in God’s immediate presence, sees what God sees, and has access to the full record of cosmic history. This angel speaks in the first person, recounting events from creation through the Exodus as an eyewitness.

Jubilees 1:27–29 Wintermute translation
“And he said to the Angel of the Presence: ‘Write for Moses from the first creation until my sanctuary is built in their midst forever and ever. And the LORD will appear in the sight of all…’ And the Angel of the Presence, who went before the camp of Israel, took the tablets of the division of years… and he dictated to Moses.”

The phrase “who went before the camp of Israel” identifies this angel with the pillar of cloud and fire (Exodus 13:21; 14:19). In Jubilees’ theology, the being who guided Israel through the wilderness is the same being who narrates the book — an angel who has been present at every event in cosmic history, from creation to Sinai, and who now reveals the full context to Moses.

This framing device accomplishes something remarkable: it presents the Genesis and Exodus narratives as a divine-council insider’s account. The human Moses receives the Torah; the Angel of the Presence fills in everything that happened behind the scenes — the Watcher rebellion, the Mastema negotiation, the heavenly tablets on which all of history was pre-recorded. Jubilees positions itself as the “director’s commentary” on the Torah, narrated by a being who was there for all of it.

14.5.3 The Heavenly Tablets

Throughout Jubilees, the Angel of the Presence repeatedly refers to the “heavenly tablets” (luḥot shamayim) — a pre-written divine record that contains the full course of history, including laws, judgments, festival dates, and the fates of nations and individuals. These tablets exist before the events they describe. They are not a record of what happened — they are a record of what was decreed to happen.

Jubilees 5:13–14 Wintermute translation
“And he wrote this testimony as a testimony for him and set it on high, for it was written on the heavenly tablets, that he should act as judge and witness against them.”

The heavenly tablets appear dozens of times in Jubilees. Laws are “written on the heavenly tablets” (Jub 3:10, 31; 4:5; 6:17). Judgments are “engraved on the heavenly tablets” (Jub 5:13). Even the names of the righteous and the wicked are recorded there. This concept connects to several canonical passages:

• Exodus 32:32–33 — Moses asks God to blot him out of “your book.” God replies: “Whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book.”
• Psalm 139:16 — “In your book were written, every one of them, the days that were formed for me, when as yet there were none of them.”
• Daniel 10:21 — “I will tell you what is inscribed in the book of truth.”
• Revelation 5:1 — A scroll “written within and on the back, sealed with seven seals” — the eschatological completion of the heavenly-tablet concept.

The heavenly tablets function in Jubilees as the divine master plan — the record of God’s sovereign will for all of history. Nothing happens that is not “written.” This is not fatalism (human choice remains real in Jubilees). It is the conviction that God is not improvising — that the entire sweep of history, from creation to consummation, is known, planned, and recorded in advance.

14.5.4 Jubilee Chronology — History in 49-Year Cycles

The book’s namesake is its chronological system: all of history is divided into jubilee cycles of 49 years (7 weeks of years, 7 × 7). This is not arbitrary. It derives from the Levitical jubilee of Leviticus 25:8–12, where every 50th year brings release, restoration, and the return of all things to their original state.

Jubilees dates every event in Genesis and Exodus to a specific year within a specific jubilee. Adam’s creation, Cain’s murder of Abel, the Watchers’ descent, Noah’s flood, Abraham’s call, the Exodus — all receive precise jubilee dates. The result is a mathematical framework for all of sacred history, structured around the number seven (creation’s number, completion’s number, covenant’s number).

Why the Jubilee System Matters
It points to eschatological restoration. If history moves in jubilee cycles, and if each jubilee culminates in release and restoration (Lev 25), then history itself has a jubilee destination — a final cosmic release, a return of all things to their original created order. This is the theology behind Jesus’ inaugural sermon in Luke 4:18–19, where he reads Isaiah 61 and announces “the year of the LORD’s favor” — a jubilee proclamation. Jesus is declaring that the ultimate jubilee has arrived. B
It structures Daniel’s 70 weeks. Daniel 9:24–27 prophesies “seventy weeks” (literally “seventy sevens”) that are “decreed” concerning Israel. This is 70 × 7 = 490 years — which is exactly 10 jubilee cycles. Whether the author of Jubilees influenced Daniel or vice versa, they share the same mathematical theology: God’s plan unfolds in cycles of seven, and the appointed end comes at the completion of a predetermined number of cycles.
It connects to the Enochic calendar. The 364-day solar year that Jubilees mandates (52 weeks exactly) and the 49-year jubilee cycle are mathematically harmonious — both built on multiples of seven. The calendar and the chronology reinforce each other: time itself is structured by divine sevens, from the week to the year to the jubilee to the eschaton.
Jubilees’ chronological system is not a quirky mathematical exercise. It is a theological claim about the nature of time: that history is not random or cyclical but purposive, structured by divine sevens, and heading toward a specific jubilee consummation. The New Testament writers inherited this conviction. When Paul writes that God acted “when the fullness of time had come” (Gal 4:4), he is expressing the same idea: time is not empty. It is full — pregnant with divine purpose, measured out in appointed seasons.

14.5.5 Why 15 Copies? — Jubilees’ Authority at Qumran

The sheer number of Jubilees manuscripts at Qumran demands explanation. Fifteen-plus copies places it in the top tier of the Qumran library — more copies than Exodus, more than Leviticus, more than any prophetic book. Why?

The answer lies in Jubilees’ unique position in the Qumran community’s self-understanding:

• It authorized their calendar. The 364-day solar calendar was the Qumran community’s defining liturgical practice. Jubilees was the text that most vigorously defended it. Every copy of Jubilees was a copy of their calendrical constitution.
• It explained their separation. Why had the “sons of light” separated from the Jerusalem temple establishment? Because the temple used the wrong calendar, observed the wrong festival dates, and had departed from the cosmic order written on the heavenly tablets. Jubilees provided the theological justification for the community’s existence.
• Other Qumran texts cite it as authoritative. The Damascus Document (CD 16:3–4) explicitly refers to “the Book of the Divisions of the Times into their Jubilees and Weeks” — a clear reference to Jubilees — as a text whose authority the community is expected to accept. This is not a library curiosity. This is a text the community treated as a rule of faith and practice.
• It connected their story to cosmic history. Jubilees told the Qumran community that they were not an isolated sect in the Judean desert. They were participants in a cosmic drama that began at creation, turned at the Watcher rebellion, passed through the patriarchs and the Exodus, and was now reaching its climax. Every jubilee cycle brought them closer to the appointed end.

Still Scripture for Millions
Jubilees is not in the Protestant or Catholic biblical canon. But it is canonical Scripture for the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church — one of the oldest Christian communions in the world, tracing its roots to the Ethiopian eunuch of Acts 8:27. The Ethiopian canon includes both Jubilees and 1 Enoch.

The early church fathers knew the book as well. Epiphanius (c. 310–403 AD) referenced it. Justin Martyr (c. 100–165 AD) reflected its traditions. The Chronicon of the medieval Ethiopian church treats its chronology as authoritative.

This is not a text that was forgotten and then rediscovered by modern scholars looking for something exotic. It was preserved in continuous use for over two thousand years — by Jews at Qumran, by Ethiopian Christians, and by scholars who recognized that understanding the Bible requires understanding what the biblical authors were reading.

Why Jubilees Answers Questions the Bible Leaves Open
If the Watchers are imprisoned (2 Peter 2:4; Jude 6), why are there still demons? Genesis does not address this. Jubilees does: God bound the Watchers but allowed a fraction of their disembodied offspring — the spirits of the dead Nephilim — to remain active under a leader called Mastema. This is the only ancient explanation that accounts for both imprisoned angels and active demons without contradiction. Jesus’ description of unclean spirits wandering “waterless places” (Matt 12:43) fits this framework exactly. C
Why were the Watchers on earth in the first place? Genesis 6 says they “saw the daughters of man.” But why were they there to see them? Jubilees 4:15 answers: they were originally sent by God to instruct humanity in righteousness. Their sin was the corruption of a divinely assigned mission — a pattern that directly parallels the Deuteronomy 32 allotment, where divine beings were given legitimate authority over nations and then abused it.
Why does Jesus use the pattern of divine permission for demonic activity? In Luke 22:31, Jesus tells Peter: “Satan demanded to have you, that he might sift you like wheat — but I have prayed for you.” The adversary requests; God permits within limits. This is exactly the Jubilees pattern — Mastema petitions, God grants a bounded permission. Whether or not Jesus had read Jubilees, his audience lived inside this theological framework. B
Jubilees does not replace Genesis. It reads like a commentary written by someone who took Genesis 6 at face value and asked the obvious follow-up questions — the same questions any careful reader would ask today.
The Second Temple Consensus — And Why It Was Silenced
Between 300 BC and 100 AD, Jewish writers across multiple communities — Qumran, Alexandria, Jerusalem, the Diaspora — shared a common understanding: the “sons of God” of Genesis 6 were angelic beings who transgressed their boundaries, produced the Nephilim, and were imprisoned as a result. This was not a fringe view. It was the mainstream Jewish interpretation. It was the interpretation Jesus’ disciples grew up with. It was the interpretation Jude and Peter wrote under.

Then, in the 2nd century AD, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai pronounced a curse on anyone who interpreted b’nei ha’elohim as angels. The “Sethite” view — that the “sons of God” were merely the godly line of Seth marrying the ungodly line of Cain — emerged not from new grammatical evidence but from theological discomfort. The angelic reading was too disturbing. Too supernatural. Too difficult to explain.

But here is the problem: the grammar has not changed. B’nei ha’elohim still means what it meant in Job 1:6, Job 2:1, and Job 38:7 — divine beings in God’s council. Every time that exact phrase appears in the Old Testament, it refers to supernatural beings. Every time. The Sethite reading requires Genesis 6 to be the sole exception — with no grammatical justification for the exception.

The Second Temple texts do not create this interpretation. They preserve it. They show us what the Bible’s original audience understood — before theological tradition replaced grammatical evidence.
